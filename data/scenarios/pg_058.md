# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline for museum acquisition due diligence. An offered object's file arrives as a descriptive submission in `object_file.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted authentication and provenance assessment and then a complete acquisition dossier ready for committee review.

The five agents:

- **collections coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the assessment loop, then the dossier loop, and tracks progress. It does no authentication, provenance research, dossier drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **assessment loop** — the **provenance researcher** turns the object file into an authentication and provenance assessment with a chosen due-diligence strategy; the **forgery-risk reviewer** attacks the case from the perspective of forgery, misattribution, and weak ownership history. They iterate until the assessment is sound.
- **dossier loop** — the **acquisition dossier writer** prepares the acquisition dossier from the vetted assessment; the **due-diligence auditor** reviews the dossier and tests whether the museum can responsibly proceed. They iterate until the dossier is correct.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `object_file.md` — the original offered-object file, given as input. All four domain agents (provenance researcher, forgery-risk reviewer, acquisition dossier writer, due-diligence auditor) read it.
- `ASSESSMENT.md` — the authentication and provenance assessment. The provenance researcher writes it; the forgery-risk reviewer reviews it; the acquisition dossier writer reads it to prepare the dossier.
- `DOSSIER.md` — the acquisition dossier and its recommendation. The acquisition dossier writer writes it; the due-diligence auditor reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the collections coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: offered-object context

You are working on a museum acquisition due-diligence matter. The offered object may be an archaeological object, painting, sculpture, manuscript, ethnographic item, design object, or mixed-media work. The original offer and supporting materials are given as a natural-language file in `object_file.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: provenance researcher's deliverable

Produce the authentication and provenance assessment for the offered object and write it to `ASSESSMENT.md`. A complete assessment has two parts — a structured case analysis and one chosen due-diligence strategy.

The **case analysis** must state:

- **Object category and risk class** — object type, likely date/culture/artist or workshop, and whether the case is low, moderate, high, or prohibitive risk; this must be precise.
- **Submitted evidence** — the key documents, images, labels, invoices, export papers, exhibition history, publication references, conservation reports, and owner statements, with what each one purports to prove.
- **Identity and attribution claims** — the exact claim being evaluated, including title, maker or culture, date range, materials, dimensions, inscriptions, marks, and any claimed findspot or collection history.
- **Authentication rationale** — the evidence supporting or undermining the object's authenticity, attribution, dating, and physical integrity.
- **Provenance and title chain** — every known ownership, custody, export, import, sale, and transfer event, listed explicitly with dates, places, parties, and gaps.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which authentication and provenance checks to pursue and in what order;
- the research method and its key initial assumptions;
- the documentary, technical, or comparative cross-check the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route.

# f3: due-diligence toolbox

Which due-diligence method suits which kind of object or risk — use it to choose the strategy:

- **Connoisseurship and stylistic comparison** — useful for paintings, sculpture, decorative arts, and manuscripts with established corpora; weak if the corpus is poorly documented or heavily forged.
- **Technical examination and materials analysis** — pigments, binders, alloy composition, wood dating, paper fiber, ink, tool marks, imaging, and condition structure; strongest when a claimed date or technique can be falsified.
- **Archival provenance research** — dealer records, collection inventories, exhibition catalogues, shipping papers, estate files, photographs, and correspondence; essential for ownership chain and attribution history.
- **Legal title and cultural property screening** — stolen-art databases, sanctions checks, patrimony laws, export permits, import records, and post-1970 UNESCO sensitivity; mandatory for archaeological, ethnographic, and conflict-zone material.
- **Scientific dating** — radiocarbon, thermoluminescence, dendrochronology, or other date testing; powerful for some materials, destructive or inconclusive for others.
- **Artist, foundation, or catalogue raisonné consultation** — valuable when a recognized authentication body exists; can be decisive but may be slow, costly, or unavailable.

Match the checks to the object's category and risk profile; do not force an ill-suited method.

# f4: writing the acquisition dossier

Read the assessment in `ASSESSMENT.md` and prepare the acquisition dossier in `DOSSIER.md`:

- Include every required section: object identification, acquisition proposal, summary of submitted evidence, authentication conclusion, provenance chronology, title and export/import analysis, condition and conservation concerns, valuation basis, ethical and reputational risks, unresolved questions, and a clear recommendation.
- Carry over the vetted facts exactly. State each ownership event, document reference, date range, attribution, condition issue, and risk flag as the assessment presents it, and do not invent missing links in the chain of title.
- Resolve the recommendation yourself and follow it through to completion — do not stop at a partial outline. If the record exposes a fatal gap, say so plainly and recommend decline or hold pending evidence; do not bury problems in vague language.
- Sanity-check the dossier: a claimed continuous provenance should not contain unexplained decades-long gaps, an export document should not predate the object's alleged discovery, and a high-risk cultural-property object should not be recommended for acquisition without legal clearance.

Write the dossier and its conclusion up in `DOSSIER.md`.

# f5: dossier doc discipline

Keep `DOSSIER.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page committee note; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: assessment review

Review the assessment in `ASSESSMENT.md`:

- **Case analysis** — does it faithfully capture the offered object and the submitted file? Check that the object category, identity and attribution claims, authentication rationale, provenance chronology, and title-chain gaps are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — are the chosen authentication and provenance checks a sound fit, is the route actually feasible, and is it likely to reach a defensible acquisition recommendation?

Be brief and specific. Do not write the acquisition dossier yourself — only review.

# f7: dossier review

Review `DOSSIER.md` — the acquisition dossier and its recommendation. Verify it directly against the offered-object file.

- Inspect the reported recommendation for anomalies, unsupported confidence, unreasonable valuation assumptions, missing risk flags, or contradictions in the provenance chronology.
- Write a separate `AUDIT_CHECKLIST.md` that re-checks every required due-diligence condition against the reported dossier, and complete it; name any ownership gaps, document conflicts, or legal-clearance failures.
- Read the dossier and spot-check it for defects — unsupported claims, missing source references, stale assumptions, internal contradictions, or language that downplays a material risk. A defect that quietly makes a risky acquisition look safe is worse than one that is obvious.
- Treat the dossier as finished only when every material authentication, provenance, title, export/import, and ethical issue has been addressed and the recommendation is sound — not merely when the document is polished.
- Check the length of `DOSSIER.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `DOSSIER.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never authenticate, research, draft the dossier, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the provenance researcher to draft the assessment.
- **Assessment loop** — have the provenance researcher and forgery-risk reviewer iterate on the authentication, provenance, and strategy until the forgery-risk reviewer scores the assessment 10.
- **Dossier loop** — once the assessment is settled, have the acquisition dossier writer and due-diligence auditor iterate on the dossier until the due-diligence auditor scores it 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the critic's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: assessment revision pass

The authentication and provenance assessment lives in `ASSESSMENT.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the forgery-risk reviewer as a loop.

- If `ASSESSMENT.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the assessment from scratch.
- If `ASSESSMENT.md` already exists, it carries the forgery-risk reviewer's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `ASSESSMENT.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the assessment and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
