# background

I need you to set up a 6-agent pipeline for investigative journalism fact-checking. A lead, tip, or assignment arrives as a written brief in `assignment.md`, and the pipeline turns it into competing vetted investigation angles, then into a publishable article draft that has been adversarially fact-checked before publication.

The six agents:

- **editorial coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the investigation-planning phase, then the article-drafting phase, and tracks progress. It does no reporting, writing, or reviewing itself.
- **investigation angle developer** is invoked once at init and produces K differentiated candidate investigation angles, one per `angles/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. It never enters the per-angle revision loop.
- **reporting plan reviser** is invoked once per plan-refinement step in the loop. It revises a single assigned angle's `PLAN.md` based on the skeptical editor's last review for that angle. It never creates new angles.
- **skeptical editor** reviews the reporting plan it is currently assigned to.
- **story writer** reports and drafts the article for the angle it is currently assigned to.
- **fact-check editor** reviews the article draft, sourcing, and verification record for the angle it is currently assigned to.

The pipeline hands work off through angle-namespaced files:

- `assignment.md` — the original lead or assignment brief, given as input. All five domain agents (investigation angle developer, reporting plan reviser, skeptical editor, story writer, fact-check editor) read it.
- `angles/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md` — one reporting plan per directory, K angles total. The investigation angle developer writes K differentiated candidates at init; the reporting plan reviser revises a specific assigned angle in the loop; the skeptical editor reviews the plan it is assigned to; the story writer reads the plan it is assigned to.
- `angles/{id}-{slug}/ARTICLE.md` — the article draft, sourcing notes, and verification summary for that angle. The story writer writes it; the fact-check editor reviews it.

Each editor records their score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file they reviewed, and the editorial coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

The editorial coordinator round-robins across the K angles, first in a planning phase (each angle iterates its reporting plan reviser ↔ skeptical editor loop) and then in a drafting phase (each angle iterates its story writer ↔ fact-check editor loop). Cross-angle reading does not happen: an agent assigned to one angle never reads another angle's files.

# f1: assignment context

You are working on an investigative journalism assignment. The original lead, tip, or newsroom brief is given in `assignment.md`; read it before starting your part. The assignment may involve public records, interviews, data, financial trails, official statements, court filings, corporate records, scientific claims, or a combination of evidence types.

# f2: investigation angle developer's deliverable

Produce K differentiated candidate investigation angles, each in its own `angles/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. The K angles should differ meaningfully in their reporting theory and evidence strategy so distinct routes can be compared downstream, not in trivial ways like changing the headline wording.

A complete plan in any `PLAN.md` has two parts — an evidence map and one chosen reporting strategy.

The **evidence map** must state:

- **Investigative question** — the exact claim, wrongdoing, accountability question, or public-interest issue this angle will test.
- **Known facts** — the concrete facts already present in `assignment.md`, distinguishing confirmed facts from allegations or assumptions.
- **Key sources and records** — the people, agencies, datasets, filings, archives, or physical locations that could confirm or disprove the angle.
- **Claims to verify** — the factual assertions the article would need to establish before publication.
- **Evidentiary gaps and risks** — missing records, likely denials, conflicts of interest, legal sensitivity, safety concerns, or weak links in the chain of proof.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which reporting methods to use and in what order;
- the first outreach, records requests, database searches, or field checks to perform;
- the triangulation approach the route relies on;
- the biggest editorial or evidentiary risk on this route.

# f3: reporting and evidence toolbox

Which reporting method suits which kind of evidentiary problem — use it to choose the strategy:

- **Public records requests** — best for government decisions, contracts, permits, inspections, emails, disciplinary records, and enforcement histories; slow and sometimes heavily redacted.
- **Court, property, corporate, and campaign-finance filings** — strong for ownership, money flows, litigation histories, lobbying ties, political influence, and timelines; requires careful identity matching.
- **On-the-record interviews** — essential for accountability, direct explanation, impact, and right of reply; vulnerable to spin, selective memory, and rehearsed statements.
- **Confidential or background source development** — useful for internal processes, hidden incentives, whistleblower leads, and document roadmaps; must be corroborated independently before publication.
- **Data analysis** — strong for patterns across many cases, outliers, disparities, spending trends, and repeated failures; depends on data completeness, definitions, and reproducible cleaning.
- **Field reporting and observation** — best for conditions on the ground, access claims, environmental or workplace realities, and human impact; may not establish causation by itself.
- **Independent expert review** — useful for technical, scientific, medical, financial, or legal claims; experts must be qualified, independent, and not used as substitutes for primary evidence.

Match the reporting method to the claim being tested; do not force an ill-suited method just because it is familiar.

# f4: writing the article

Read the plan in your assigned angle's `PLAN.md` and carry out its reporting strategy as an article draft:

- Include the working headline, nut graf, key findings, article body, source list, and a concise evidence note for every material factual claim. Attribute claims precisely, distinguish allegation from established fact, and include the subject's response or a clear note of outreach attempts.
- Follow the plan's evidence map: verify each listed claim, pursue the specified records and interviews, and document what was confirmed, disproved, or left unresolved. Do not present an unresolved allegation as fact.
- Work the draft through to completion — do not stop with placeholders such as “TK,” “needs interview,” or “awaiting records” unless the draft explicitly explains why the point has been removed or narrowed.
- Sanity-check the article: dates should align, names and titles should be consistent, numbers should add up, and no quote or statistic should appear without a source.

Write the article draft and its reporting results up in your assigned angle's `ARTICLE.md`. Do not read or touch other angles' files.

# f5: article doc discipline

Keep your assigned angle's `ARTICLE.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page draft and sourcing note; record iteration-by-iteration reporting history in the angle's `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: plan review

Review the reporting plan in your assigned angle's `PLAN.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned angle; do not look at other angles.

- **Evidence map** — does it faithfully capture the assignment? Check that the investigative question, known facts, claims to verify, sources, records, gaps, and risks are all accurate and complete.
- **Strategy** — are the chosen reporting methods a sound fit, is the route actually reportable, and is it likely to produce publishable, well-corroborated findings?

Be brief and specific. Do not write the article yourself and do not conduct reporting — only review.

# f7: article fact-check review

Review your assigned angle's `ARTICLE.md` — the article draft, sourcing, and reported findings. Verify it directly against the assignment brief and the evidence cited in the draft. Operate strictly on the assigned angle; do not look at other angles.

- Inspect the reported findings for anomalies, overclaims, unsupported allegations, missing context, numerical errors, timeline conflicts, or statements that go beyond the evidence.
- Write a separate `fact_check_matrix.md` under the angle's directory that re-checks every material factual claim against the cited source or reporting note, and complete it; name any claims that fail verification or need narrowing.
- Read the draft and spot-check it for editorial bugs — unattributed assertions, dead links, mismatched names, quotes without sourcing, figures that contradict tables, or language that implies certainty where the evidence is weaker. A subtle overstatement that could mislead readers is worse than an obvious gap.
- Treat the article as publication-ready only when every material factual claim is supported and the framing is fair — not merely when the draft is readable.
- Check the length of the angle's `ARTICLE.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the angle's `ARTICLE.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this isn't verified, but I'm telling you it isn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never report, write, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the investigation angle developer once to draft K differentiated candidate investigation angles, each in its own `angles/{id}-{slug}/` directory. After it returns, read the directory list to learn the K angle ids. The investigation angle developer is not invoked again after this.
- **Planning phase** — round-robin across the K angles. For each angle in turn, dispatch reporting plan reviser (revise that angle based on its skeptical editor's review) then skeptical editor (score that angle). After one round-robin pass through all K angles, start the next pass. An angle is considered done in this phase when its skeptical editor score reaches 10; stop scheduling more planning-phase calls for it but continue with the others. After all K angles hit skeptical editor 10, or each angle has had 10 planning-phase iterations, move to the drafting phase.
- **Drafting phase** — round-robin across the K angles. For each angle in turn, dispatch story writer (revise that angle's article based on its fact-check editor's review) then fact-check editor (score that angle's article). The pipeline stops when any angle reaches fact-check editor score 10, or each angle has had 10 drafting-phase iterations.
- When dispatching, always pass the angle's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `PLAN.md` / `ARTICLE.md` to operate on.
- Keep the current phase, per-angle iteration counts, per-angle latest scores, and current angle index in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant editor's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: plan revision pass

Read your assigned angle's `PLAN.md`. It carries the skeptical editor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the angle's `PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Preserve the two-part structure (evidence map plus chosen reporting strategy). Don't discard the angle and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. You revise; you do not create new angles. Do not read or touch other angles' files.

# f11: article revision pass

Your article draft and reporting results live in your assigned angle's `ARTICLE.md`.

- If your assigned angle's `ARTICLE.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that angle: produce the article from scratch based on the angle's `PLAN.md`.
- If the angle's `ARTICLE.md` already exists, it carries the fact-check editor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the angle's `ARTICLE.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the article and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. Do not read or touch other angles' files.
