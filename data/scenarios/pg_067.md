# background

I need you to set up a 6-agent pipeline that reviews clinical cases. A case arrives as a structured clinical narrative in `case.md`, and the pipeline turns it into several competing diagnostic and treatment pathways, a safety-vetted care plan, and then a patient-specific care recommendation audited for medical errors, omissions, and contraindications.

The six agents:

- **case-flow coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the pathway phase, then the recommendation phase, and tracks progress. It does no clinical reasoning, recommendation writing, or reviewing itself.
- **diagnostic pathway proposer** is invoked once at init and produces K differentiated candidate diagnostic and treatment pathways, one per `pathways/{id}-{slug}/CARE_PLAN.md`. It never enters the per-pathway revision loop.
- **care-plan reviser** is invoked once per care-plan refinement step in the loop. It revises a single assigned pathway's `CARE_PLAN.md` based on the patient-safety challenger's last review for that pathway. It never creates new pathways.
- **patient-safety challenger** reviews the pathway it is currently assigned to.
- **clinical recommendation writer** converts the strategy into a concrete patient-specific care recommendation for the pathway it is currently assigned to.
- **medical audit reviewer** reviews the care recommendation and safety checks for the pathway it is currently assigned to.

The pipeline hands work off through pathway-namespaced files:

- `case.md` — the original clinical case, given as input. All five clinical agents (diagnostic pathway proposer, care-plan reviser, patient-safety challenger, clinical recommendation writer, medical audit reviewer) read it.
- `pathways/{id}-{slug}/CARE_PLAN.md` — one care plan per directory, K pathways total. The diagnostic pathway proposer writes K differentiated candidates at init; the care-plan reviser revises a specific assigned pathway in the loop; the patient-safety challenger reviews the pathway it is assigned to; the clinical recommendation writer reads the pathway it is assigned to.
- `pathways/{id}-{slug}/RECOMMENDATION.md` — the patient-specific care recommendation and supporting rationale for that pathway. The clinical recommendation writer writes it; the medical audit reviewer reviews it.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the case-flow coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

The case-flow coordinator round-robins across the K pathways, first in a pathway phase (each pathway iterates its care-plan reviser ↔ patient-safety challenger loop) and then in a recommendation phase (each pathway iterates its clinical recommendation writer ↔ medical audit reviewer loop). Cross-pathway reading does not happen: an agent assigned to one pathway never reads another pathway's files.

# f1: clinical case context

You are working on a clinical case review. The original case is given as a structured narrative in `case.md`; read it before starting your part. The case may include presenting symptoms, vital signs, exam findings, medical history, medications, allergies, pregnancy status, labs, imaging, prior treatments, social context, and patient preferences.

# f2: diagnostic pathway proposer's deliverable

Produce K differentiated candidate diagnostic and treatment pathways, each in its own `pathways/{id}-{slug}/CARE_PLAN.md`. The K pathways should differ meaningfully in their clinical strategy so distinct routes can be compared downstream, not in trivial ways like rewording the same differential or ordering the same tests in a different sentence.

A complete care plan in any `CARE_PLAN.md` has two parts — a formalized clinical assessment and one chosen diagnostic/treatment strategy.

The **assessment** must state:

- **Clinical problem type and acuity** — for example unstable emergency, urgent inpatient workup, outpatient diagnostic clarification, chronic disease optimization, medication adverse event, postoperative complication, and so on; this must be explicit.
- **Patient data** — the key clinical inputs and what each one implies, including age, relevant comorbidities, medications, allergies, vital signs, exam findings, labs, imaging, and risk factors.
- **Key clinical decisions** — the diagnostic and therapeutic choices that must be made, with clear names and timing.
- **Clinical objective** — the exact goal of the pathway, such as ruling out a life-threatening diagnosis, treating the most likely condition safely, preventing deterioration, minimizing medication harm, or arranging appropriate follow-up.
- **Safety constraints** — every relevant contraindication, allergy, drug interaction, renal/hepatic dosing issue, pregnancy/lactation concern, infection-control issue, procedure risk, monitoring requirement, and escalation trigger.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which diagnostic tests, treatments, monitoring steps, and consultations to use, and in what order;
- the clinical algorithm and its key initial thresholds or decision points;
- the guideline principle, bedside rule, risk-stratification tool, or safety workaround the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route.

# f3: clinical pathway toolbox

Which clinical approach suits which kind of case — use it to choose a strategy:

- **Emergency stabilization first** — for airway, breathing, circulation, sepsis, stroke, myocardial infarction, anaphylaxis, severe hypoglycemia, active bleeding, or altered mental status; do not delay immediate treatment for a complete diagnostic workup.
- **Syndrome-based differential diagnosis** — best when the presenting problem is nonspecific, such as chest pain, dyspnea, abdominal pain, fever, syncope, headache, weakness, or confusion; prioritize life-threatening and common causes before rare ones.
- **Guideline-directed disease pathway** — best when the diagnosis is already likely or established, such as asthma exacerbation, heart failure, diabetes, COPD, evaluationulitis, community-acquired pneumonia, UTI, anticoagulation management, or hypertension.
- **Risk-stratified testing** — use validated bedside rules or pretest probability when appropriate, such as Wells/PERC for pulmonary embolism, HEART/TIMI for chest pain, Centor/McIsaac for pharyngitis, Ottawa rules for imaging, or CHA₂DS₂-VASc/HAS-BLED for anticoagulation.
- **Medication-safety reconciliation** — essential when the case involves polypharmacy, anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, sedatives, QT-prolonging drugs, immunosuppressants, renal impairment, hepatic disease, pregnancy, breastfeeding, or documented allergies.
- **Watchful waiting with explicit follow-up** — appropriate only for low-risk, stable patients when immediate testing or treatment is unlikely to change management; it must include red flags, follow-up timing, and a contingency plan.
- **Specialty consultation or transfer** — use when the case exceeds local scope, requires a procedure or inpatient service, involves diagnostic uncertainty with high risk, or has complex comorbidities.

Match the pathway intensity to the patient's acuity and risk; do not force a low-acuity outpatient approach onto an unstable case, and do not expose a low-risk patient to unnecessary invasive testing.

# f4: writing the care recommendation

Read the care plan in your assigned pathway's `CARE_PLAN.md` and convert its strategy into a concrete patient-specific care recommendation:

- Include every clinically relevant component: assessment summary, prioritized diagnoses, diagnostic tests, treatments, medication names and doses when appropriate, monitoring, patient counseling, disposition, follow-up, and escalation criteria.
- Check every recommendation against the case details: allergies, current medications, pregnancy/lactation status, renal and hepatic function, age, vital signs, comorbidities, infection risk, anticoagulation status, and patient preferences.
- Do not hide uncertainty. If a diagnosis is not confirmed, state what finding would confirm or refute it and what should happen next.
- Sanity-check the recommendation: a medication should not contradict an allergy, a dose should be plausible for the patient, an outpatient plan should not ignore unstable vital signs, and a reassurance plan should not miss a red flag.

Write the recommendation and its supporting rationale in your assigned pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md`. Do not read or touch other pathways' files.

# f5: recommendation doc discipline

Keep your assigned pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page note; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history into the pathway's `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: care-plan safety review

Review the care plan in your assigned pathway's `CARE_PLAN.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned pathway; do not look at other pathways.

- **Assessment** — does it faithfully capture the case? Check that the clinical problem type, acuity, patient data, key decisions, objective, and safety constraints are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — are the diagnostic tests, treatments, monitoring, consultations, and escalation thresholds a sound fit; are they implementable; and are they likely to reach a safe and correct clinical recommendation?
- **Safety** — look specifically for missed contraindications, allergy conflicts, drug interactions, renal/hepatic dosing problems, pregnancy concerns, inappropriate disposition, delayed emergency care, and failure to address red flags.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the final care recommendation yourself — only review.

# f7: medical audit review

Review your assigned pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md` — the care recommendation and its rationale. Verify it directly against the case statement. Operate strictly on the assigned pathway; do not look at other pathways.

- Inspect the recommendation for anomalies, unsafe treatments, unreasonable doses, missing monitoring, inappropriate disposition, contraindications, or failure to address a dangerous diagnosis.
- Write a separate `audit_checklist.md` under the pathway's directory that re-checks every active medication, test, procedure, monitoring step, and disposition decision against the reported case details, and complete it; name any allergies, drug interactions, contraindications, abnormal vital signs, lab values, or comorbidities that are violated or ignored.
- Read the recommendation and spot-check it for clinical logic errors — copied assumptions not present in the case, unused abnormal findings, omitted red flags, contradictory instructions, or advice that contradicts the stated diagnosis. A subtle recommendation that appears reasonable but increases patient risk is worse than one that clearly fails and gets stopped.
- Treat the result as finished only when the recommendation is internally consistent, addresses the important risks, and has no unresolved contraindication or safety gap — not merely when the document is well written.
- Check the length of the pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this is unsafe, but I'm telling you it is unsafe" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never perform clinical reasoning, write care recommendations, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the diagnostic pathway proposer once to draft K differentiated candidate pathways, each in its own `pathways/{id}-{slug}/` directory. After it returns, read the directory list to learn the K pathway ids. The diagnostic pathway proposer is not invoked again after this.
- **Pathway phase** — round-robin across the K pathways. For each pathway in turn, dispatch care-plan reviser (revise that pathway based on its patient-safety challenger's review) then patient-safety challenger (score that pathway). After one round-robin pass through all K pathways, start the next pass. A pathway is considered done in this phase when its patient-safety challenger score reaches 10; stop scheduling more pathway-phase calls for it but continue with the others. After all K pathways hit patient-safety challenger 10, or each pathway has had 10 pathway-phase iterations, move to the recommendation phase.
- **Recommendation phase** — round-robin across the K pathways. For each pathway in turn, dispatch clinical recommendation writer (revise that pathway's recommendation based on its medical audit reviewer's review) then medical audit reviewer (score that pathway's recommendation). The pipeline stops when any pathway reaches medical audit reviewer score 10, or each pathway has had 10 recommendation-phase iterations.
- When dispatching, always pass the pathway's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `CARE_PLAN.md` / `RECOMMENDATION.md` to operate on.
- Keep the current phase, per-pathway iteration counts, per-pathway latest scores, and current pathway index in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant pathway's reviewer `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: care-plan revision pass

Read your assigned pathway's `CARE_PLAN.md`. It carries the patient-safety challenger's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the pathway's `CARE_PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Preserve the two-part structure (formalized clinical assessment plus chosen diagnostic/treatment strategy). Don't discard the pathway and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. You revise; you do not create new pathways. Do not read or touch other pathways' files.

# f11: recommendation revision pass

Your care recommendation and its rationale live in your assigned pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md`.

- If your assigned pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that pathway: produce the recommendation from scratch based on the pathway's `CARE_PLAN.md`.
- If the pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md` already exists, it carries the medical audit reviewer's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the pathway's `RECOMMENDATION.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the recommendation and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. Do not read or touch other pathways' files.
