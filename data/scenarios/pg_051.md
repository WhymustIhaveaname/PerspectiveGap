# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that conducts counter-terrorism intelligence analysis. Raw intercepts arrive as natural-language transcripts and logs in `intercepts.md`, and the pipeline turns them into a vetted threat hypothesis and then a finalized, verified tactical operations dossier.

The five agents:

- **station-chief** orchestrates the run: it drives the hypothesis loop, then the dossier loop, and tracks progress. It does no intelligence analysis, compiling, or reviewing itself.
- **hypothesis loop** — the **lead-analyst** turns the raw intercepts into a formal threat model and analytic strategy; the **red-team-challenger** actively hunts for cognitive biases and logical gaps to review it. They iterate until the hypothesis is rock-solid.
- **dossier loop** — the **dossier-compiler** implements the strategy, synthesizing the intercepts into a tactical operations dossier; the **fact-verification-inquisitor** scrutinizes the dossier and its conclusions. They iterate until the tactical picture is fully verified.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `intercepts.md` — the original raw intercepts, given as input. All four domain agents (lead-analyst, red-team-challenger, dossier-compiler, fact-verification-inquisitor) read it.
- `HYPOTHESIS.md` — the threat hypothesis. The lead-analyst writes it; the red-team-challenger reviews it; the dossier-compiler reads it to guide the compilation.
- `DOSSIER.md` — the tactical operations dossier and finalized intelligence picture. The dossier-compiler writes it; the fact-verification-inquisitor reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the station-chief reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: intelligence context

You are working on a counter-terrorism intelligence analysis problem — it may involve a lone wolf, a coordinated local evaluation, a transnational network, or a hybrid threat. The original raw intercepts and data logs are provided as natural-language text in `intercepts.md`; read it thoroughly before starting your part.

# f2: lead-analyst's deliverable

Produce the threat hypothesis based on the intercepts and write it to `HYPOTHESIS.md`. A complete hypothesis has two parts — a formalized threat model and one chosen analytic strategy.

The **model** must state:

- **Threat type** — Lone Wolf, Evaluation, State-Sponsored, Syndicate, etc.; this must be exact.
- **Data** — the key intercepts, signals, and what each piece of evidence represents.
- **Target entities** — their profiles, aliases, and known operational domains, with descriptive identifiers.
- **Primary objective** — the exact operational intent or target the threat actors are pursuing.
- **Constraints** — every logistical, temporal, and geographical constraint limiting the threat actors, listed explicitly.

The **strategy** — weigh the intelligence angles and commit to the single most promising analytic route — must state:

- which intelligence framework/database to leverage and how to query it;
- the core analytic methodology and its key assumptions;
- the specific behavioral pattern or operational signature the route relies on;
- the biggest risk or intelligence blind spot on this route.

# f3: analytic toolbox

Which intelligence framework suits which kind of threat — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Analyst's Notebook** — pure link analysis and visual entity mapping only; no geospatial telemetry support.
- **Palantir Gotham** — multi-modal data integration (SIGINT, HUMINT, OSINT); the broadest coverage, and source-agnostic.
- **Maltego** — open-source intelligence (OSINT), infrastructure footprinting, and digital shadow mapping.
- **SIGINT Suite Alpha** — top performance on encrypted communications metadata and frequency correlation; restricted classification; not for pure HUMINT networks.
- **Cellebrite Matrix** — the strongest specialized option for local device exploitation and digital forensics networks; on wide-area transnational analysis, slower to yield insights than SIGINT Suite.
- **GEOFence Tracker** — large-scale pattern-of-life analysis and geospatial movement tracking.

Match the framework to the threat type and available intercepts; do not force an ill-suited one onto the data.

# f4: compiling the dossier

Read the hypothesis in `HYPOTHESIS.md` and implement its strategy into a finalized tactical operations dossier:

- Include every relevant intercept citation. Detail each target entity with the correct aliases and attributes, define the primary objective and every logistical constraint exactly as the model states, synthesize the data, check the operational status of the targets, and output both the tactical recommendation and the overall confidence level.
- Run the timeline and geospatial correlation yourself and trace the event chain through to completion — do not stop while there are unresolved intelligence gaps. If a contradiction arises, find and fix the root cause; do not bury inconsistencies under caveats or vague language.
- Sanity-check the intelligence: a suspect who is confirmed detained in one location cannot simultaneously be organizing an attack halfway across the world in person.

Write the comprehensive intelligence picture and tactical recommendations up in `DOSSIER.md`.

# f5: dossier doc discipline

Keep `DOSSIER.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page tactical brief; record only the finalized intelligence picture and move iteration-by-iteration analytic history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is prioritized.

# f6: hypothesis review

Review the threat hypothesis in `HYPOTHESIS.md`:

- **Model** — does it faithfully capture the raw intercepts? Check that the threat type, target entities, primary objective, and constraints are all logically sound and derived from evidence.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen framework and methodology a sound fit, is the analytic route actually actionable without falling prey to cognitive bias, and is it likely to yield a verified tactical picture?

Be brief and specific. Do not write any dossier content yourself — only hunt for cognitive biases and logical gaps.

# f7: dossier review

Review `DOSSIER.md` — the tactical operations dossier and its synthesized conclusions. Verify it directly against the raw intercepts.

- Inspect the reported tactical picture for anomalies, unreasonable leaps in logic, or timeline/geographical violations.
- Write a separate `verify.py` script that computationally cross-references every temporal and geographical constraint against the reported locations and timestamps in the dossier, and run it; name any entities that violate the space-time continuum or logistical constraints.
- Read the dossier and spot-check it for analytical bugs — unused key intercepts, dead-end leads, or tactical recommendations that contradict what the evidence says. An analytical bug that silently degrades the threat picture is worse than an overt formatting error.
- Treat the result as finished only when every timeline constraint holds and the tactical picture is empirically sound — not merely when the dossier reads smoothly without grammatical errors.
- Check the length of `DOSSIER.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `DOSSIER.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "the timeline doesn't make sense, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing intelligence product, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never analyze intercepts, compile dossiers, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the lead-analyst to draft the threat hypothesis.
- **Hypothesis loop** — have the lead-analyst and red-team-challenger iterate on the threat model and analytic strategy until the red-team-challenger scores the hypothesis 10.
- **Dossier loop** — once the hypothesis is settled, have the dossier-compiler and fact-verification-inquisitor iterate on the tactical dossier until the fact-verification-inquisitor scores it 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the critic's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: hypothesis revision pass

The threat hypothesis lives in `HYPOTHESIS.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the red-team-challenger as a loop.

- If `HYPOTHESIS.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the hypothesis from scratch.
- If `HYPOTHESIS.md` already exists, it carries the red-team-challenger's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `HYPOTHESIS.md` in place to address every logical gap or cognitive bias raised. Don't discard the hypothesis and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: dossier revision pass

The tactical operations dossier and its findings live in `DOSSIER.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the fact-verification-inquisitor as a loop.

- If `DOSSIER.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: compile the dossier from scratch.
- If `DOSSIER.md` already exists, it carries the fact-verification-inquisitor's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `DOSSIER.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the dossier and start over, and don't leave a flagged intelligence gap or contradiction unaddressed.
