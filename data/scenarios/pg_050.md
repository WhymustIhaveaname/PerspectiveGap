# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent legal defense pipeline that handles high-stakes civil litigation. A legal dispute arrives as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted defense theory and then formal, bulletproof court filings.

The five agents:

- **managing-partner** orchestrates the run: it drives the strategy loop, then the drafting loop, and tracks progress. It does no legal theorizing, drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **strategy loop** — the **strategy-architect** turns the dispute into a formal defense theory and procedural plan; the **mock-opposing-counsel** runs a murder board to aggressively review and break it. They iterate until the strategy is airtight.
- **drafting loop** — the **brief-drafter** implements the strategy as formal court filings; the **legal-loophole-auditor** reviews the filings and their arguments for fatal flaws. They iterate until the filings are legally unassailable.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `problem.md` — the original legal complaint and facts, given as input. All four domain agents (strategy-architect, mock-opposing-counsel, brief-drafter, legal-loophole-auditor) read it.
- `PLAN.md` — the strategy. The strategy-architect writes it; the mock-opposing-counsel reviews it; the brief-drafter reads it to implement it.
- `SOLUTION.md` — the formal court filings and argument summaries. The brief-drafter writes it; the legal-loophole-auditor reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the managing-partner reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: case context

You are working on a high-stakes civil litigation defense — it may involve contract disputes, torts, regulatory violations, or a combination. The original complaint and factual background are given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: strategy-architect's deliverable

Produce the defense plan for the case and write it to `PLAN.md`. A complete plan has two parts — a formalized theory of the case and one chosen procedural strategy.

The **theory** must state:

- **Case type** — breach of contract, negligence, fraud, statutory violation, and so on; this must be exact.
- **Key evidence** — the crucial factual inputs and what each piece of evidence represents.
- **Core arguments** — the legal claims and defenses, with clear descriptive names.
- **Ultimate objective** — the exact legal outcome we are trying to achieve (e.g., complete dismissal, reduced damages).
- **Constraints** — every limiting factor, such as binding precedents, time bars, or PR constraints, listed explicitly.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which procedural maneuver to use and how to file it;
- the core legal narrative and its key initial framing;
- the reframing or narrative trick the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route.

# f3: procedural toolbox

Which procedural maneuver suits which kind of case — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Rule 12(b)(6) Motion to Dismiss** — pure legal insufficiency only; no factual dispute support.
- **Rule 56 Summary Judgment** — broad coverage, applies to both legal and factual insufficiency; relies on a fully developed record.
- **Jurisdictional Challenge** — missing diversity, improper venue, or lack of personal jurisdiction.
- **Affirmative Defenses** — top performance on statutory bars like statute of limitations and laches; highly specialized; not for general merits defense.
- **Counterclaims** — the strongest aggressive maneuver for commercial disputes and turning the tables; on pure defense, slower and costlier than standard motions.
- **Early Settlement Leverage** — large-scale, complex multi-party exposure where trial risks are catastrophic.

Match the maneuver to the case type; do not force an ill-suited one.

# f4: drafting the filings

Read the plan in `PLAN.md` and implement its strategy as formal court filings:

- Include every necessary legal citation. Frame each argument with the correct scope and legal standard, define the objective and every constraint exactly as the theory states, apply the precedent, check the argument's viability, and explicitly state both the conclusion and the ultimate defense achieved.
- Proofread the logic yourself and trace every argument through to its conclusion — do not leave legal tests half-applied. If a precedent contradicts you, find and fix the root flaw in your argument; do not bury bad law under vague footnotes.
- Sanity-check the argument: a motion requiring undisputed facts must not rely on heavily contested witness testimony.

Write the filings and their exact legal arguments up in `SOLUTION.md`.

# f5: filing doc discipline

Keep `SOLUTION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page legal memo; record only the final filings and move iteration-by-iteration redlines into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if every sentence is bolded for emphasis, nothing is emphasized.

# f6: strategy review

Review the plan in `PLAN.md` aggressively as a murder board:

- **Theory** — does it faithfully capture the legal reality of the case? Check that the case type, core arguments, ultimate objective, and constraints are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen procedural maneuver and narrative a sound fit, is the route actually fileable in court, and is it likely to secure a win?

Be brief and specific. Do not draft any court filings yourself — only review the strategy.

# f7: filing review

Review `SOLUTION.md` — the formal filings and their legal arguments. Verify it directly against the original case context.

- Inspect the drafted arguments for logical leaps, unreasonable interpretations of the law, or ignored constraints.
- Write a separate `cite_check.py` script that automatically validates every case citation and statutory reference against the drafted arguments, and run it; name any arguments that misrepresent or violate binding law.
- Read the text of the brief and spot-check it for bugs — unused facts, dead boilerplate, logic that contradicts what the filing claims to prove. A flaw that silently undermines our credibility with the judge is worse than one that gets instantly struck.
- Treat the draft as finished only when every legal test holds and the ultimate objective is soundly argued — not merely when the document looks like a proper legal brief.
- Check the length of `SOLUTION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `SOLUTION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real legal merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this argument doesn't hold up in court, but I'm disclosing that it doesn't" is still a failing brief, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never strategize, draft, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the strategy-architect to draft the plan.
- **Strategy loop** — have the strategy-architect and mock-opposing-counsel iterate on the theory and maneuver until the mock-opposing-counsel scores the plan 10.
- **Drafting loop** — once the strategy is settled, have the brief-drafter and legal-loophole-auditor iterate on the filings until the legal-loophole-auditor scores them 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the critic's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: strategy revision pass

The strategy lives in `PLAN.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the mock-opposing-counsel as a loop.

- If `PLAN.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the defense plan from scratch.
- If `PLAN.md` already exists, it carries the mock-opposing-counsel's `<review>` block: read that aggressive feedback and revise `PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the strategy and start over, and don't leave a flagged legal vulnerability unaddressed.

# f11: filing revision pass

The formal filings and arguments live in `SOLUTION.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the legal-loophole-auditor as a loop.

- If `SOLUTION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the filings from scratch.
- If `SOLUTION.md` already exists, it carries the legal-loophole-auditor's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `SOLUTION.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the draft and start over, and don't leave a flagged loophole unaddressed.
