# background

I need you to set up a 6-agent pipeline that drafts a comprehensive legal defense strategy and finalizes the exact court motions to survive summary judgment in a high-stakes corporate litigation case. A case arrives as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted plan and then fully drafted, verified court motions.

The six agents:

- **litigation-manager** orchestrates the run: it drives the plan phase, then the drafting phase, and tracks progress. It does no legal analysis, drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **lead-strategist** is invoked once at init and produces K differentiated candidate plans, one per `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. It never enters the per-plan revision loop.
- **argument-developer** is invoked once per plan-refinement step in the loop. It revises a single assigned plan's `PLAN.md` based on the moot-court-judge's last review for that plan. It never creates new plans.
- **moot-court-judge** reviews the plan it is currently assigned to.
- **motion-drafter** drafts the court motions for the plan it is currently assigned to.
- **opposing-counsel-proxy** reviews the drafted motions and arguments of the plan it is currently assigned to.

The pipeline hands work off through plan-namespaced files:

- `problem.md` — the original case statement, given as input. All five domain agents (lead-strategist, argument-developer, moot-court-judge, motion-drafter, opposing-counsel-proxy) read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md` — one plan per directory, K plans total. The lead-strategist writes K differentiated candidates at init; the argument-developer revises a specific assigned plan in the loop; the moot-court-judge reviews the plan it is assigned to; the motion-drafter reads the plan it is assigned to.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/SOLUTION.md` — the drafted motions and arguments for that plan. The motion-drafter writes it; the opposing-counsel-proxy reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the litigation-manager reads those scores to drive the loops.

The litigation-manager round-robins across the K plans, first in a plan phase (each plan iterates its argument-developer ↔ moot-court-judge loop) and then in a drafting phase (each plan iterates its motion-drafter ↔ opposing-counsel-proxy loop). Cross-plan reading does not happen: an agent assigned to one plan never reads another plan's files.

# f1: case context

You are working on a high-stakes corporate litigation case — it may involve breach of contract, antitrust, intellectual property, or a combination. The original case fact pattern is given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: lead-strategist's deliverable

Produce K differentiated candidate plans, each in its own `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. The K plans should differ meaningfully in their core legal theories so distinct routes can be compared downstream, not in trivial ways like formatting.

A complete plan in any `PLAN.md` has two parts — a formalized case theory and one chosen defense strategy.

The **case theory** must state:

- **Litigation type** — Antitrust, IP infringement, breach of contract, etc.; this must be exact.
- **Evidence** — the key factual inputs and what each document or testimony represents.
- **Legal elements** — the elements the plaintiff must prove, with descriptive definitions.
- **Vulnerabilities** — the exact weaknesses in the plaintiff's arguments to exploit.
- **Defenses** — every affirmative defense, listed explicitly.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which legal precedent to use and how to apply it;
- the procedural mechanism and its key initial arguments;
- the legal nuance or procedural trick the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route.

# f3: legal precedent toolbox

Which precedent suits which kind of litigation — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Chevron / Loper Bright** — Administrative law and agency deference only; no private contract support.
- **Twombly / Iqbal** — Pleading standards for all federal civil claims; the broadest coverage for early dismissal.
- **Alice / Mayo** — Patent eligibility and abstract ideas.
- **Rule 12(b)(6) versus Rule 56** — Use 12(b)(6) for facial insufficiency; Rule 56 when relying on undisputed facts to defeat claims pre-trial.
- **Statute of Limitations / Laches** — The strongest absolute bar for stale claims; fails if tolling applies.
- **Force Majeure / Impracticability** — Contractual non-performance defense.

Match the precedent to the litigation type; do not force an ill-suited one.

# f4: drafting the motions

Read the plan in your assigned plan's `PLAN.md` and implement its strategy as drafted court motions:

- Include every required section. Draft the introduction, define the statement of undisputed material facts and every legal argument exactly as the case theory states, construct the argument, cite the law, and draft the conclusion and proposed order.
- Read through the drafted motion yourself and verify it against the local rules — do not stop while it is incomplete. If an argument is legally unsupported, find and fix the root cause; do not bury weak points under aggressive rhetoric.
- Sanity-check the logic: a timeline that must be chronological should not jump around confusingly.

Write the motions and their supporting memos up in your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md`. Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f5: motion doc discipline

Keep your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a twelve-page brief; record only the final draft and move iteration-by-iteration history into the plan's `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: plan review

Review the plan in your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned plan; do not look at other plans.

- **Case theory** — does it faithfully capture the case? Check that the litigation type, legal elements, vulnerabilities, and defenses are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen precedent and procedural mechanism a sound fit, is the route actually arguable in court, and is it likely to survive summary judgment?

Be brief and specific. Do not write any motion drafts yourself — only review.

# f7: motion review

Review your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` — the drafted court motions and arguments. Verify it directly against the case statement. Operate strictly on the assigned plan; do not look at other plans.

- Inspect the drafted arguments for logical leaps, misstated facts, or waived defenses.
- Write a separate `verify.py` script under the plan's directory that parses the citations and cross-checks every legal element against the drafted arguments, and run it; name any claims that lack a proper defense.
- Read the drafted motions and spot-check them for flaws — unused precedents, dead-end arguments, rhetoric that contradicts what the brief claims it proves. A legal flaw that silently weakens the defense is worse than a formatting error.
- Treat the draft as finished only when every legal element is defended and the core argument is sound — not merely when the document is fully formatted.
- Check the length of the plan's `SOLUTION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the plan's `SOLUTION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this argument fails, but I'm telling you it fails" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never analyze, draft, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the lead-strategist once to draft K differentiated candidate plans, each in its own `plans/{id}-{slug}/` directory. After it returns, read the directory list to learn the K plan ids. The lead-strategist is not invoked again after this.
- **Plan phase** — round-robin across the K plans. For each plan in turn, dispatch argument-developer (revise that plan based on its moot-court-judge's review) then moot-court-judge (score that plan). After one round-robin pass through all K plans, start the next pass. A plan is considered done in this phase when its moot-court-judge score reaches 10; stop scheduling more plan-phase calls for it but continue with the others. After all K plans hit moot-court-judge 10, or each plan has had 10 plan-phase iterations, move to the drafting phase.
- **Drafting phase** — round-robin across the K plans. For each plan in turn, dispatch motion-drafter (revise that plan's drafted motions based on its opposing-counsel-proxy's review) then opposing-counsel-proxy (score that plan's drafted motions). The pipeline stops when any plan reaches opposing-counsel-proxy score 10, or each plan has had 10 drafting-phase iterations.
- When dispatching, always pass the plan's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `PLAN.md` / `SOLUTION.md` to operate on.
- Keep the current phase, per-plan iteration counts, per-plan latest scores, and current plan index in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant plan's critic `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: plan revision pass

Read your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`. It carries the moot-court-judge's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the plan's `PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Preserve the two-part structure (formalized case theory plus chosen defense strategy). Don't discard the plan and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. You revise; you do not create new plans. Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f11: motion revision pass

Your drafted court motions and arguments live in your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md`.

- If your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the drafted motions from scratch based on the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `SOLUTION.md` already exists, it carries the opposing-counsel-proxy's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the plan's `SOLUTION.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the draft and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. Do not read or touch other plans' files.
