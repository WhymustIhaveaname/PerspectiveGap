# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that designs aerospace vehicles by alternating between mathematical aerodynamic calculations of performance bounds and computational fluid dynamics (CFD) physical simulations, with each side informing the other. A mission profile and design requirements arrive as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the aerodynamic math (bounds, flow invariants, aerodynamic claims) and revising the CFD simulation (a mesh and solver setup that achieves those conditions).

The five agents:

- **chief-engineer** orchestrates the run by alternating one aerodynamics step with one CFD step and tracking progress. It does no calculating, meshing, or reviewing itself.
- **aerodynamics loop** — the **aerodynamicist** writes the mathematical aerodynamic calculations to `THEORY.md`; the **flight-dynamics-evaluator** judges it. The aerodynamicist reads `SOLUTION.md` on each revision pass to incorporate empirical CFD evidence.
- **CFD loop** — the **cfd-modeler** writes CFD solver scripts and flow results to `SOLUTION.md`; the **telemetry-auditor** judges them. The cfd-modeler reads `THEORY.md` on each revision pass to align the simulation with the latest mathematical aerodynamic claims.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `problem.md` — the original mission requirements, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `THEORY.md` — the mathematical aerodynamic calculations. Aerodynamicist writes; flight-dynamics-evaluator reviews; cfd-modeler and telemetry-auditor read it to know which aerodynamic bounds and flow invariants the CFD simulation must respect.
- `SOLUTION.md` — the CFD simulation script and flow results. Cfd-modeler writes; telemetry-auditor reviews; aerodynamicist and flight-dynamics-evaluator read it to ground the mathematical theory in observed physical numbers.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The chief-engineer reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a plan-then-code pipeline where the mathematical plan must converge before the CFD starts, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. Aerodynamic calculations may tighten after seeing CFD's empirical numbers; CFD simulations may correct mesh or solver settings after seeing the math's refined bounds.

# f1: mission requirements context

You are working on an aerospace vehicle design problem, which may involve subsonic, transonic, supersonic, or hypersonic flight regimes. The original design requirements and mission profile are given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: aerodynamicist's deliverable

Produce the theoretical aerodynamic calculations for the vehicle and write it to `THEORY.md`. A complete analysis covers four kinds of claim, each justified with a mathematical derivation or a citation.

- **Performance bounds** — theoretical upper limits on lift-to-drag ratio, lower bounds on wave drag, or maximum structural thermal loads based on isentropic relations.
- **Flow regimes and structural properties** — compressibility effects, expected shockwave detachment, boundary layer transition points, and vehicle cross-sectional area distributions. State what holds, where, and under which assumptions.
- **Invariants** — quantities every physical flow field must preserve (mass conservation laws, stagnation enthalpy, specific heat ratios). The CFD solver's output should satisfy them; the telemetry-auditor will check with `verify.py`.
- **Flow stability and solver convergence** — expected convergence rates for the iterative fluid solvers based on mesh Reynolds numbers, or identifying regions of physical flow instability (e.g., vortex shedding frequencies, flutter margins).

Ground your claims against `SOLUTION.md` if it has reported CFD flow metrics. A predicted drag coefficient that is grossly off from observed CFD numbers means either the aerodynamic calculations need revision or the CFD mesh/solver has a bug — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. "The lift coefficient is bounded above by lifting-line theory, value 1.2" is fine; "the vehicle has some lift" is not.

# f3: aerodynamic calculation toolbox

Match the mathematical tool to the flight regime and vehicle geometry; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in theoretical aerodynamic analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Lifting-line theory / Thin airfoil approximations | Subsonic flow; estimating lift distributions, induced drag, and lift curve slopes |
| Oblique shock / Prandtl-Meyer expansion relations | Supersonic regimes; predicting shockwave angles, pressure jumps, and wave drag |
| Whitcomb area rule | Transonic design; wave drag minimization through smooth longitudinal cross-section distributions |
| Navier-Stokes analytical scaling / Blasius solution | Boundary layer analysis; estimating skin friction drag and separation points |
| Isentropic flow and normal shock relations | Compressible aerodynamics; calculating stagnation pressure, temperature, and Mach number changes |
| Panel methods (inviscid) | Low-speed complex geometries; surface pressure distributions before viscous corrections |
| Thermodynamic cycle analysis | Propulsion integration; theoretical thrust, specific impulse, and thermal efficiency limits |
| Linearized perturbation theory | Dynamic stability; computing longitudinal and lateral-directional stability derivatives |

# f4: building the CFD simulation

Read `problem.md` and `THEORY.md`, then implement the CFD simulation script:

- Include every import (e.g., meshing libraries, CFD solver python APIs like SU2 or OpenFOAM wrappers). Generate the mesh with the correct dimensions and boundary conditions, define the fluid properties, and set the turbulence model exactly as the mission requires. Run the simulation, check convergence status, and print both the flow field metrics (lift, drag, moments) and localized peak values.
- Make the simulation testable against `THEORY.md`. If the aerodynamic calculations state a theoretical minimum wave drag D, your CFD should achieve a drag at least D. If the math states a thermodynamic invariant, your output should satisfy it.
- Run the CFD script yourself and watch it through to completion — do not stop while the flow iterations are still converging. If it diverges or crashes, find and fix the root cause; do not bury solver errors under try/except.
- Sanity-check the answer against `THEORY.md`. A Mach number that must be strictly supersonic downstream should not drop to subsonic unphysically; a pressure bound the math says holds should hold.

Write the meshing/solver code and its numerical results up in `SOLUTION.md`.

# f5: CFD simulation doc discipline

Keep `SOLUTION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page note; record only the final mesh configuration, solver setup, and converged results, moving iteration-by-iteration residual history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: aerodynamic calculations review

Review `THEORY.md` against `problem.md` and against the numbers reported in `SOLUTION.md` (if any).

- **Faithfulness** — do the aerodynamic calculations correctly describe the vehicle mission stated in `problem.md`? Check velocity regimes, atmospheric conditions, and geometric constraints.
- **Math soundness** — for each claim, walk through the equation derivation or citation. Flag hand-waving steps. Verify cited aerodynamic relations apply under the stated assumptions (e.g., no incompressible formulas at Mach 2).
- **Empirical grounding** — open `SOLUTION.md`. If the CFD reports lift and drag forces, do they fall within the math's predicted bounds? An analytical wave drag estimate that is off by orders of magnitude from CFD is a signal the underlying assumption is too weak; tighten it, or note explicitly that the formulation is only illustrative.

Be brief and specific. Do not write aerodynamic calculations yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: CFD simulation review

Review `SOLUTION.md` against `problem.md` and against `THEORY.md`.

- Inspect the reported CFD results for anomalies, non-physical turbulence values, or boundary condition violations stated directly in `problem.md`.
- Open `THEORY.md`. For every mathematical bound and invariant the aerodynamicist claims, write a check into a separate `verify.py`: does the CFD solver's flow field satisfy the thermodynamic invariant, fall within the predicted lift/drag bounds, achieve the predicted pressure ratio? Run it. Name any variable or node quantity that violates.
- Read the CFD meshing and solver setup code and spot-check it for bugs — coarse mesh densities in high-gradient regions, wrong turbulence models, or timestep logic that contradicts the chosen flow regime. A boundary condition bug that silently degrades the flow field is worse than one that crashes the solver.
- Treat the result as finished only when every boundary condition holds, every mathematically claimed flow invariant holds, and the integrated forces are consistent with the aerodynamic bounds — not merely when the solver converges without error.
- Check the length of `SOLUTION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `SOLUTION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this flow field diverges, but I'm telling you it diverges" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never calculate aerodynamics, write CFD code, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Dispatch the aerodynamicist for the first aerodynamics step (aerodynamicist, then flight-dynamics-evaluator), then the cfd-modeler for the first CFD step (cfd-modeler, then telemetry-auditor).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one aerodynamic math step with one CFD step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even perfect-looking math may need to revise once the CFD simulation reports actual flow numbers.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 aerodynamics steps + 10 CFD steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: aerodynamics revision pass

The mathematical aerodynamic calculations live in `THEORY.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the flight-dynamics-evaluator as a loop, alternating with the CFD loop.

- If `THEORY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the aerodynamic math from scratch based on `problem.md`.
- If `THEORY.md` already exists, it carries the flight-dynamics-evaluator's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `SOLUTION.md` and look at the latest reported CFD flow numbers — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your mathematical claims. Revise `THEORY.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any empirical evidence that changes your analysis. Don't discard the previous calculations and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: CFD revision pass

The CFD meshing/solver code and its flow results live in `SOLUTION.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the telemetry-auditor as a loop, alternating with the aerodynamics loop.

- If `SOLUTION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the CFD solution from scratch based on `problem.md`.
- If `SOLUTION.md` already exists, it carries the telemetry-auditor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `THEORY.md` and check whether the latest aerodynamic math introduces bounds, invariants, or flow claims your current CFD simulation doesn't yet respect — for example a tighter wave drag bound your solver is now far from achieving, or a thermodynamic invariant your flow field violates. Revise `SOLUTION.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest math. Don't discard the previous simulation and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the chief-engineer: what you did, what difficulties you hit (or didn't resolve), and any open questions.
