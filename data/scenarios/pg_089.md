# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that develops a wildfire incident response plan by refining fire-behavior forecasts and tactical operations planning in parallel, with each side informing the other. An incident package arrives as a natural-language briefing in `incident.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the forecast assumptions (spread potential, trigger points, weather sensitivities, exposure concerns) and revising the operations plan (divisions, resources, tactics, contingencies, and safety controls).

The five agents:

- **incident coordination officer** orchestrates the run by alternating one fire-behavior step with one operations step and tracking progress. It does no forecasting, tactical planning, or reviewing itself.
- **fire-behavior loop** — the **fire behavior analyst** writes the forecast and fire-behavior rationale to `FORECAST.md`; the **red-team fire model reviewer** challenges it. The fire behavior analyst reads `IAP.md` on each revision pass to incorporate operational observations, resource constraints, and field-reported conditions.
- **operations loop** — the **operations plan chief** writes the tactical incident action plan to `IAP.md`; the **incident safety officer** judges it. The operations plan chief reads `FORECAST.md` on each revision pass to align tactics with the latest fire-behavior claims.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `incident.md` — the original incident briefing, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `FORECAST.md` — the fire-behavior forecast and rationale. Fire behavior analyst writes; red-team fire model reviewer reviews; operations plan chief and incident safety officer read it to know which spread scenarios, trigger points, and exposure risks the plan must respect.
- `IAP.md` — the tactical operations plan and operational results. Operations plan chief writes; incident safety officer reviews; fire behavior analyst and red-team fire model reviewer read it to ground the forecast in actual assignments, access limits, and field constraints.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The incident coordination officer reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a forecast-then-plan pipeline where modeling must settle before tactics start, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. Forecast assumptions may tighten after seeing operational reports and feasible resource deployments; the operations plan may change after seeing a revised wind scenario, spotting-probability assessment, or evacuation trigger.

# f1: incident context

You are working on a wildfire incident response planning problem. The original incident is given as a natural-language briefing in `incident.md`; read it before starting your part. It may describe fuels, topography, forecast weather, communities at risk, roads and access points, available crews and equipment, aviation limits, evacuation concerns, and command objectives.

# f2: fire behavior analyst's deliverable

Produce the fire-behavior forecast for the incident and write it to `FORECAST.md`. A complete forecast covers four kinds of claim, each justified with a calculation, fire-behavior principle, local observation, or cited model assumption.

- **Spread and intensity bounds** — expected and worst-plausible rates of spread, flame-length ranges, spotting distances, arrival-time windows at key values at risk, and the conditions under which those ranges change.
- **Structural properties** — wind alignment, slope effects, fuel continuity, barriers, diurnal recovery, plume-dominated potential, channeling in drainages, or opportunities created by recent burns, roads, rivers, or fuel transitions. State what holds, where, and under which assumptions.
- **Operational invariants** — facts every safe tactical plan must preserve, such as keeping crews out of chimneys during wind shifts, maintaining egress and safety zones, honoring evacuation trigger points, preserving lookout-communication-escape-safety coverage, and avoiding direct attack where flame lengths exceed safe handline thresholds.
- **Uncertainty and update behavior** — confidence in weather inputs, sensitivity to wind speed and direction, expected change at burn-period transitions, likelihood of spotting or crown-fire transition, and what field observations would invalidate the current forecast.

Ground your claims against `IAP.md` if it has reported assignments, resource limits, or field observations. A predicted fire spread that is grossly inconsistent with observed perimeter movement or planned division placement means either the forecast needs revision or the operations plan is unsafe or infeasible — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. “With 15 mph southwest winds, the eastern flank can reach Ridge Road in 3–5 hours unless held at the dozer line” is useful; “the fire may move quickly” is not.

# f3: fire-behavior analysis toolbox

Match the tool to the incident structure; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in fire-behavior analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Rothermel surface fire-spread estimates | Grass, brush, timber litter; rate-of-spread and flame-length estimates under specified fuel, slope, and wind |
| Campbell Prediction System alignment analysis | Identifying where wind, slope, and aspect combine to create high-risk runs |
| Haines Index and plume-dominated assessment | Evaluating vertical instability, plume growth, spotting potential, and erratic fire behavior |
| Spotting-distance and ember-cast analysis | Wind-driven spotting across roads, drainages, rivers, or control lines |
| Fuel-moisture and Energy Release Component trends | Burn-period severity, ignition probability, resistance to control, and overnight recovery |
| WFDSS / FSPro-style scenario reasoning | Probabilistic spread pathways, values-at-risk exposure, and strategic risk comparison |
| Trigger-point and time-distance analysis | Evacuation timing, point-protection deadlines, and tactical withdrawal thresholds |
| After-action analogue comparison | Incidents in similar fuel, weather, and terrain where observed behavior constrains plausible forecasts |

# f4: writing the operations plan

Read `incident.md` and `FORECAST.md`, then produce the tactical operations plan:

- Include the incident objectives, operational period, division or group assignments, available resources, communications assumptions, medical plan references, evacuation coordination needs, contingency branches, and clear tactical tasks. Assign resources with realistic capability limits and travel/access constraints.
- Make the plan testable against `FORECAST.md`. If the forecast states a trigger point, your plan should specify the action tied to that trigger. If the forecast states an invariant such as required egress, safety zones, or no direct attack above a flame-length threshold, your plan should preserve it.
- Walk the plan through to completion for the operational period — do not stop at broad strategy. If a tactic depends on an unavailable crew type, closed road, grounded aircraft, or unrealistic production rate, find and fix the root cause; do not hide the gap in vague language.
- Sanity-check the plan against `FORECAST.md`. A hand crew should not be assigned direct attack in flame lengths the forecast says are unsafe; an evacuation trigger should not occur after the modeled arrival time at the community; a division should not rely on an egress route threatened by the same run it is assigned to hold.

Write the plan and its operational rationale up in `IAP.md`.

# f5: incident action plan doc discipline

Keep `IAP.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page operational note; record iteration-by-iteration history in a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: fire model review

Review `FORECAST.md` against `incident.md` and against the tactical assignments reported in `IAP.md` (if any).

- **Faithfulness** — does the forecast correctly describe the incident stated in `incident.md`? Check fuels, topography, wind direction, access constraints, values at risk, resource availability, and time references.
- **Reasoning soundness** — for each claim, walk through the calculation, model assumption, analogy, or cited fire-behavior principle. Flag hand-waving steps. Verify that spread estimates, spotting assumptions, and trigger times apply under the stated fuel, slope, wind, and moisture conditions.
- **Operational grounding** — open `IAP.md`. If the plan reports division placements, control lines, evacuation timing, or resource limits, do they fit within the forecast's predicted spread windows and hazard areas? A forecast that ignores a division working below an unstable slope or assumes aviation support that the plan says is unavailable is not operationally grounded.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the forecast yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: operations and safety review

Review `IAP.md` against `incident.md` and against `FORECAST.md`.

- Inspect the reported tactics for anomalies, unsafe assignments, unrealistic production expectations, missing resources, or contradictions with hazards stated directly in `incident.md`.
- Open `FORECAST.md`. For every trigger point, spread estimate, safety invariant, and high-risk scenario the forecast claims, write a check into a separate `SAFETY_CHECK.md`: does the operations plan preserve egress, keep resources out of predicted entrapment zones, initiate evacuations before forecast arrival times, and match tactics to expected flame length and spotting risk? Mark each check PASS, FAIL, or NEEDS INFORMATION. Name any division, resource, route, or value at risk that violates the forecast.
- Read the operations plan and spot-check it for dangerous assumptions — unused resources that were counted on elsewhere, dead-end egress routes, tactics that contradict the stated strategy, aviation missions planned in smoke or wind conditions that would likely ground aircraft, or night operations without adequate lookouts and communications. A plan that silently exposes crews to an avoidable hazard is worse than one that openly says a task cannot be done.
- Treat the plan as acceptable only when every incident constraint holds, every forecast-claimed safety invariant holds, and the tactics are consistent with the forecast's spread windows and hazard areas — not merely when the plan sounds complete.
- Check the length of `IAP.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `IAP.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — “this is unsafe, but I am telling you it is unsafe” is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never forecast, plan tactics, or review yourself — you only assign the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Assign the fire behavior analyst for the first forecast step (fire behavior analyst, then red-team fire model reviewer), then the operations plan chief for the first operations step (operations plan chief, then incident safety officer).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one fire-behavior step with one operations step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even a strong-looking forecast may need revision once the operations plan exposes resource constraints or field observations.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 fire-behavior steps + 10 operations steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each assignment. Read each score from the reviewer’s `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent’s brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: fire forecast revision pass

The fire-behavior forecast lives in `FORECAST.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the red-team fire model reviewer as a loop, alternating with the operations loop.

- If `FORECAST.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the forecast from scratch based on `incident.md`.
- If `FORECAST.md` already exists, it carries the red-team fire model reviewer’s `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `IAP.md` and look at the latest operational assignments, constraints, and reported field observations — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your claims. Revise `FORECAST.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any operational evidence that changes your forecast. Don’t discard the previous forecast and start over, and don’t leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: operations plan revision pass

The tactical operations plan lives in `IAP.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the incident safety officer as a loop, alternating with the fire-behavior loop.

- If `IAP.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the plan from scratch based on `incident.md`.
- If `IAP.md` already exists, it carries the incident safety officer’s `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `FORECAST.md` and check whether the latest forecast introduces trigger points, safety invariants, spread scenarios, or structural fire-behavior claims your current plan does not yet respect — for example a tighter evacuation deadline, a new spotting concern across the primary line, or an egress route forecast to be compromised. Revise `IAP.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest forecast. Don’t discard the previous plan and start over, and don’t leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the incident coordination officer: what you did, what difficulties you hit or could not resolve, and any open questions.
