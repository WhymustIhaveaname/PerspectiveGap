# background

I need you to set up a 2-agent pipeline for a research mathematics proof challenge. The two roles are prover and referee: prover is responsible for developing proofs or counterexamples for nontrivial mathematical claims, referee is responsible for judging whether what the prover submitted is valid, complete, and actually resolves the claim. The flow is roughly:

```
WHILE TRUE:
    1. prover picks an idea it likes and explores it deeply, or appends more ideas to ideas.xml and then picks one to explore.
    2. prover spawns a **fresh** referee to evaluate it.
```

# f1: problem definition

You are participating in a research mathematics proof challenge.

Read the challenge rules first: ./challenge-rules.md

# f2: proof tactics

The claims in this challenge are nontrivial — any short, routine argument would likely already be known or would fail on a hidden hypothesis. So:

- Don't submit arguments that are only plausible at a glance (e.g. "apply compactness" without verifying the space is compact) — if it were that direct, it would not be a challenge problem
- Truly valuable submissions hinge on careful handling of quantifiers, edge cases, hidden regularity assumptions, and exact theorem hypotheses

**Writing the submission**

Each idea corresponds to its own submission file at `reports/{id}-{slug}.md`, where `{id}` and `{slug}` come from the idea's id and slug attributes in ideas.xml.

The submission must contain four h2 sections in this fixed order:

- `## Claim Title` — concise, clear title of the theorem, lemma, or counterexample being submitted
- `## Result Description` — what the submission establishes and why it matters for the challenge claim
- `## Short Description` — short writeup of the proof strategy or counterexample construction, with references to definitions, lemmas, and cited results
- `## Verification Details` — a complete proof or counterexample check that the referee can follow line by line without filling in major gaps

Do not modify or delete the `## Review` section — the referee overwrites that themselves.

If after investigation you find the idea doesn't hold, still briefly record your analysis and the reason for rejection in the submission file.

# f3: evaluation criteria

Check the following dimensions one by one:

| Dimension | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Scope | Addresses the stated challenge claim or a clearly relevant lemma, reduction, or counterexample |
| References and statements | Cited definitions, theorem statements, hypotheses, and notation must exactly match the challenge materials or cited sources |
| Result description | Claimed result consistent with the submission's own argument, no self-contradiction, no overstatement |
| Root cause analysis | Pinpoint the exact mathematical mechanism: why the proof closes, or why the counterexample violates the claim |
| Verification details | Complete derivation or construction from the author's perspective; must not assume the desired conclusion or hide essential steps |
| Reproducibility | Argument can be checked end-to-end by the referee without inventing missing lemmas, choosing unspecified objects, or repairing broken cases |
| Deduplication | Search the challenge archive, cited literature, public preprints, and discussion threads to confirm the result is not already known or already resolved |
| Strength beyond triviality | The submission must exceed a vacuous, degenerate, or already-excluded case and genuinely resolve a nontrivial part of the claim |

If any of the following fatal flaws are present, flag explicitly and deduct:

- References to nonexistent definitions, lemmas, papers, or AI-hallucinated theorem statements
- Result description contradicts the submission's own argument
- Proof assumes the conclusion, uses circular reasoning, or modifies the problem's hypotheses
- References or theorem hypotheses inconsistent with the challenge materials or cited sources
- Verification details are incomplete and require the referee to supply major missing lemmas or case analyses
- Result is only a vacuous, degenerate, or already-excluded case, with no nontrivial contribution

# f4: dispatch rules

After finding a proof or counterexample, submit it to a **fresh** referee for review. Do not review your own submission.

**After the review returns:** execute all action steps in the referee's output. Then immediately go back to step 1: re-read ideas.xml and pick the next idea by UCB to keep working. Don't wait for user instructions, don't stop. One round = write submission → review → execute actions → back to step 1.

# f6: idea selection

Before starting work, read ideas.xml.

1. Each idea's notes contain the referee's **quality score (0-10)**. A score of 0 means the idea is dead — don't touch it again. `none` means it hasn't been attempted yet.
2. Prefer ideas with `attempts="0"` and `score="none"` first.
3. Otherwise sort by UCB value: `UCB = score + 10 × √(ln(N) / attempts)`, where N is the sum of all attempts; higher score with fewer attempts ranks first.
4. If no idea is worth trying, search the challenge statement, definitions, known lemmas, and related literature for new ideas, append them to ideas.xml, then pick one to start.

**Recording rule during exploration**

While exploring the mathematics, the moment you spot any potential new approach, obstruction, special case, reduction, or counterexample pattern — even if you're unsure it holds — immediately append it to ideas.xml (`score="none"`, `attempts=0`). Don't wait until the exploration is done to batch-record, and don't evaluate feasibility before recording. Recording ≠ committing to attempt it, just preventing forgetting. Evaluation belongs to the selection phase.
