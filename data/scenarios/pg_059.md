# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline for an environmental impact assessment for a proposed construction project. Project materials arrive as a natural-language package in `project.md`, and the pipeline turns them into a vetted impact-analysis approach and then a permit-ready EIA report that can withstand regulator-style scrutiny.

The five agents:

- **permitting coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the impact-assessment approach loop, then the report loop, and tracks progress. It does no impact analysis, report writing, or reviewing itself.
- **assessment approach loop** — the **impact assessment lead** turns the project materials into a formal assessment scope and evidence strategy; the **regulatory challenge reviewer** reviews it. They iterate until the approach is sound.
- **report loop** — the **EIA report author** writes the permit-ready report from the approved approach; the **permit-compliance auditor** reviews the report and its support. They iterate until the report is complete and defensible.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `project.md` — the original project materials, given as input. All four domain agents read it.
- `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md` — the impact-analysis approach. The impact assessment lead writes it; the regulatory challenge reviewer reviews it; the EIA report author reads it to prepare the report.
- `EIA_REPORT.md` — the permit-ready environmental impact assessment report and its supporting findings. The EIA report author writes it; the permit-compliance auditor reviews it.

Each reviewer records their score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file they reviewed, and the permitting coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: project context

You are working on an environmental impact assessment for a proposed construction project. The original project materials are given as a natural-language package in `project.md`; read it before starting your part. The materials may describe the site, proposed works, schedule, construction methods, land disturbance, traffic, utilities, sensitive receptors, habitats, water bodies, emissions sources, and any prior studies or agency correspondence.

# f2: impact assessment lead's deliverable

Produce the impact-analysis approach for the project and write it to `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md`. A complete approach has two parts — a formalized assessment scope and one chosen evidence strategy.

The **assessment scope** must state:

- **Project and permit context** — the project type, likely permitting pathway, jurisdictional triggers, and the decision the assessment must support.
- **Baseline data** — the key environmental setting information and what each item represents, including site conditions, nearby receptors, habitats, water resources, cultural resources if relevant, traffic setting, air/noise baseline, and known data gaps.
- **Impact pathways** — the construction and operational activities that could cause effects, with clear source-pathway-receptor links.
- **Significance framework** — the thresholds, standards, guidance, or professional criteria used to judge whether impacts are minor, significant, or require mitigation.
- **Required analyses and mitigations** — every impact topic to be assessed, the evidence needed for each, and the mitigation or monitoring commitments that must be evaluated.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which assessment methods, models, surveys, agency guidance, and permit checklists to use;
- the sequencing of analyses and key assumptions;
- the screening or scoping rationale the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route, such as a missing seasonal survey, unsupported baseline, underestimated cumulative effect, or unclear jurisdictional trigger.

# f3: assessment toolbox

Which assessment approach suits which kind of project issue — use it to choose the methods for the strategy:

- **Regulatory checklist and gap analysis** — best for identifying permit triggers, required attachments, agency-specific completeness items, and missing documentation; not enough by itself for technical impact conclusions.
- **GIS constraints and receptor mapping** — best for early screening of wetlands, floodplains, protected habitats, schools, residences, cultural resources, and other sensitive receptors; depends on current, authoritative spatial data.
- **Field surveys and habitat delineation** — necessary where mapped resources are uncertain, protected species habitat may be present, wetlands or watercourses need confirmation, or seasonal conditions affect findings.
- **Construction impact matrix** — useful for linking activities to impacts across noise, dust, traffic, erosion, stormwater, waste, and community disruption; must be backed by project-specific quantities and durations.
- **Quantitative modeling** — appropriate for topics such as air emissions, greenhouse gases, noise, traffic level of service, stormwater runoff, sediment transport, or visual simulations when thresholds or receptors require numerical evidence.
- **Cumulative impact assessment** — needed when the project overlaps with other past, present, or reasonably foreseeable projects affecting the same receptors or resources.
- **Mitigation hierarchy review** — use to test whether the approach avoids impacts first, then minimizes, restores, and only offsets where allowed; weak if mitigation commitments are vague or unenforceable.

Match the methods to the project’s risks and permit pathway; do not force a generic checklist where technical evidence is required.

# f4: writing the EIA report

Read the approved approach in `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md` and write the permit-ready report in `EIA_REPORT.md`:

- Include every required report section. Describe the proposed project, existing conditions, assessment methods, impact findings, significance determinations, mitigation measures, monitoring commitments, residual impacts, cumulative impacts, and permit-compliance conclusions.
- Carry over each impact pathway from the assessment scope. For every topic, state the evidence used, the applicable threshold or regulatory criterion, the finding, and the mitigation or monitoring commitment if needed.
- Check the report yourself from start to finish. If a conclusion is unsupported, trace the root cause and fix it; do not bury uncertainty under vague language.
- Sanity-check the findings: a receptor described as sensitive should not be dismissed without evidence, a required permit trigger should not disappear, and mitigation commitments should be specific enough to enforce.

Write the final report and its supporting findings up in `EIA_REPORT.md`.

# f5: report doc discipline

Keep `EIA_REPORT.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page permit memorandum; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: assessment plan review

Review the impact-analysis approach in `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md`:

- **Assessment scope** — does it faithfully capture the project and likely environmental review obligations? Check that the project context, baseline data, impact pathways, significance framework, required analyses, and mitigation topics are all accurate and complete.
- **Strategy** — are the chosen methods, surveys, models, guidance, and sequencing a sound fit, is the route actually implementable, and is it likely to produce a permit-ready evidentiary record?

Be brief and specific. Do not write the EIA report yourself — only review.

# f7: report review

Review `EIA_REPORT.md` — the permit-ready environmental impact assessment report and its supporting findings. Verify it directly against the project materials.

- Inspect the reported findings for anomalies, unsupported dismissals, unreasonable significance conclusions, missing mitigation, or contradictions with the project description.
- Write a separate `compliance_check.md` that re-checks every required topic, permit trigger, impact pathway, threshold, mitigation commitment, and monitoring requirement against the reported conclusions; name any topics that are missing, inconsistent, or unsupported.
- Read the report and spot-check it for defects — unused baseline facts, dead sections, copied boilerplate, mitigation that is too vague to enforce, or conclusions that contradict the evidence. A defect that quietly weakens the permit record is worse than one that is obvious.
- Treat the report as finished only when every required issue is addressed and each conclusion is supported by evidence — not merely when the document reads smoothly.
- Check the length of `EIA_REPORT.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `EIA_REPORT.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this is incomplete, but I'm telling you it is incomplete" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never assess impacts, write the report, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the impact assessment lead to draft the assessment approach.
- **Assessment approach loop** — have the impact assessment lead and regulatory challenge reviewer iterate on the scope and evidence strategy until the regulatory challenge reviewer scores the approach 10.
- **Report loop** — once the approach is settled, have the EIA report author and permit-compliance auditor iterate on the report until the permit-compliance auditor scores it 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer’s `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: assessment plan revision pass

The impact-analysis approach lives in `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the regulatory challenge reviewer as a loop.

- If `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the assessment approach from scratch.
- If `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md` already exists, it carries the regulatory challenge reviewer’s `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `ASSESSMENT_PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the approach and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
