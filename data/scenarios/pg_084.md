# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that develops corporate tax legislation by drafting macroeconomic policy and running adversarial accounting simulations in parallel, with each side informing the other. A legislative mandate arrives as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the macroeconomic policy (revenue bounds, structural constraints, legal invariants) and revising the accounting simulation (an adversarial tax evasion setup that exploits it).

The five agents:

- **drafting-chair** orchestrates the run by alternating one policy step with one accounting step and tracking progress. It does no policy drafting, simulating, or reviewing itself.
- **policy loop** — the **macroeconomist** writes the macroeconomic policy analysis to `POLICY.md`; the **tax-attorney** judges it. The macroeconomist reads `EVASION.md` on each revision pass to incorporate empirical evidence from the evasion setup.
- **accounting loop** — the **tax-strategist** writes the accounting simulation code and its resulting tax liability to `EVASION.md`; the **irs-auditor** judges them. The tax-strategist reads `POLICY.md` on each revision pass to align the tax setup with the latest legal claims and revenue bounds.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `problem.md` — the original legislative mandate, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `POLICY.md` — the macroeconomic policy analysis. Macroeconomist writes; tax-attorney reviews; tax-strategist and irs-auditor read it to know which revenue floors and legal invariants the simulated corporations must respect (or actively circumvent).
- `EVASION.md` — the accounting simulation code and the final tax liability. Tax-strategist writes; irs-auditor reviews; macroeconomist and tax-attorney read it to ground the policy bounds in observed, stress-tested numbers.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The drafting-chair reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a draft-then-simulate pipeline where the policy must converge before the accounting setup starts, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. Policy may tighten after seeing the simulation's exploited loopholes; the accounting strategy may pivot after seeing the policy's refined anti-avoidance constraints.

# f1: legislative mandate

You are working on corporate tax legislation, which may involve international transfer pricing, intellectual property, capital gains, or a combination. The original legislative mandate is given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: macroeconomist's deliverable

Produce the macroeconomic policy analysis for the legislation and write it to `POLICY.md`. A complete analysis covers four kinds of claim, each justified with an economic proof sketch or a citation.

- **Revenue bounds** — lower bounds on tax revenue derived from dynamic scoring, macroeconomic baselines, or unshielded domestic assets; upper bounds from maximum taxable capacity or Laffer curve peak estimates.
- **Structural properties** — jurisdictional ring-fencing, tax base elasticity, safe harbor symmetries, decomposability into independent corporate subsidiaries. State what holds, where, and under which economic assumptions.
- **Legal Invariants** — rules every legally compliant entity must preserve (conservation of taxable events, double-entry accounting parity, total-capital-mass conditions). The evasion simulation's output must formally satisfy them; the irs-auditor will check with `audit.py`.
- **Behavioral complexity** — expected compliance costs, risk of capital flight (offshoring), or stabilization rates for iterative tax phase-ins.

Ground your claims against `EVASION.md` if it has reported numbers. A predicted revenue bound that is grossly shattered by the strategist's evasion setup means either the policy is full of loopholes or the simulation is committing outright illegal fraud — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. "The revenue is bounded below by the domestic sales baseline, value $73B" is fine; "the tax code has some structure that prevents base erosion" is not.

# f3: macroeconomic policy toolbox

Match the tool to the legislative structure; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in tax policy analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Dynamic scoring / baseline bounds | Corporate income tax; lower bound on minimum expected revenue, baseline gap analysis |
| Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) analysis | Multinational taxation; certify domestic retention of profits and intellectual property |
| Tax burden incidence analysis | Payroll, VAT, or excise taxes; tracing the true economic burden from capital to labor |
| Deadweight loss / Laffer curve analysis | Marginal rate changes; measuring behavioral suppression of the taxable base |
| General Equilibrium (CGE) modeling | Broad policy shifts; existence of new market equilibrium, geometric stabilization of supply chains |
| Microsimulation models | Randomized taxpayer behavior; high-probability bounds on revenue collection or compliance error |
| Safe harbor provisions | Overly complex tax codes; justify resorting to simplified heuristic tax brackets to minimize evasion risk |
| Capital flight sensitivity analysis | Global corporate taxes; stability of tax revenue under perturbation by emerging tax havens |

# f4: writing the accounting simulation

Read `problem.md` and `POLICY.md`, then implement the adversarial accounting simulation:

- Include every import. Create each financial variable and subsidiary with the correct jurisdiction and capitalization, define the tax minimization objective and every legal constraint exactly as the mandate states. Calculate the financials, check the audit status, and print both the accounting setup and the final effective tax rate.
- Make the implementation testable against `POLICY.md`. If the policy states an unbreakable revenue floor R, your adversarial simulation should try to achieve a tax liability below R by exploiting loopholes. If the policy states a legal invariant, your corporate structure must formally satisfy it to avoid outright fraud.
- Run the code yourself and watch it through to completion — do not stop while it is still running. If it errors, find and fix the root cause; do not bury errors under try/except.
- Sanity-check the answer against `POLICY.md`. A tax liability that must be positive should not come back as an infinite refund; a legal constraint the policy says must hold must hold.

Write the simulation code and its results up in `EVASION.md`.

# f5: accounting doc discipline

Keep `EVASION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page brief; record only the final corporate structure and move iteration-by-iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: policy review

Review `POLICY.md` against `problem.md` and against the numbers reported in `EVASION.md` (if any).

- **Faithfulness** — does the policy correctly address the mandate stated in `problem.md`? Check definitions, corporate entity types, and legislative translations.
- **Economic soundness** — for each claim, walk through the policy justification or citation. Flag hand-waving steps. Verify cited economic models apply under the stated assumptions.
- **Empirical grounding** — open `EVASION.md`. If the simulation reports effective tax liabilities, do they fall within the policy's predicted revenue bounds? A revenue floor that the tax-strategist easily shatters by orders of magnitude is a signal the anti-avoidance rules are too weak; tighten them, or note explicitly that the bound is only illustrative.

Be brief and specific. Do not draft policy yourself — only review. If a claim is flawed, say which one and why.

# f7: accounting setup review

Review `EVASION.md` against `problem.md` and against `POLICY.md`.

- Inspect the reported tax liability for anomalies, unreasonable deductions, or illegal constraint violations stated directly in `problem.md`.
- Open `POLICY.md`. For every revenue bound and legal invariant the policy claims, write a check into a separate `audit.py`: does the simulation's output formally satisfy the invariant, fall within the predicted bound, or successfully exploit a loophole? Run it. Name any subsidiary, transfer price, or transaction that illegally violates constraints.
- Read the accounting simulation code and spot-check it for bugs — unused corporate entities, dead tax shields, logic that contradicts the chosen accounting strategy. A bug that silently inflates the tax paid is worse than one that crashes.
- Treat the result as finished only when every legal constraint holds, every policy-claimed invariant is formally satisfied, and the minimized tax liability is fully stress-tested against the policy's bounds — not merely when the simulation runs without error.
- Check the length of `EVASION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `EVASION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this evasion strategy doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never draft policy, simulate accounting, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Dispatch the macroeconomist for the first policy step (macroeconomist, then tax-attorney), then the tax-strategist for the first accounting step (tax-strategist, then irs-auditor).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one policy step with one accounting step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even a perfect-looking policy may need to revise once the accounting simulation reports actual tax numbers.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 policy steps + 10 accounting steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: policy revision pass

The macroeconomic policy analysis lives in `POLICY.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the tax-attorney as a loop, alternating with the accounting loop.

- If `POLICY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the analysis from scratch based on `problem.md`.
- If `POLICY.md` already exists, it carries the tax-attorney's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `EVASION.md` and look at the latest reported tax figures — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your policy claims. Revise `POLICY.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any empirical evidence that changes your analysis. Don't discard the previous analysis and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: accounting revision pass

The accounting simulation code and its results live in `EVASION.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the irs-auditor as a loop, alternating with the policy loop.

- If `EVASION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the evasion setup from scratch based on `problem.md`.
- If `EVASION.md` already exists, it carries the irs-auditor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `POLICY.md` and check whether the latest policy introduces revenue bounds, legal invariants, or structural claims your current setup doesn't yet respect — for example a tighter minimum tax your simulation is now far from circumventing, or a legal invariant your corporate structure outright violates. Revise `EVASION.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest policy. Don't discard the previous strategy and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the drafting-chair: what you did, what difficulties you hit (or didn't resolve), and any open questions.
