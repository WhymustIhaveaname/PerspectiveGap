# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that evaluates a fine-art authentication case by developing a provenance hypothesis and a forensic examination report in parallel, with each side informing the other. A case arrives as a natural-language brief in `case.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the provenance account (attribution, ownership history, documentary chain, art-historical fit) and revising the conservation-science examination (materials, imaging, condition, lab methodology, and interpretation).

The five agents:

- **authentication case manager** orchestrates the run by alternating one provenance step with one forensic step and tracking progress. It does no attribution research, lab interpretation, or reviewing itself.
- **provenance loop** — the **provenance researcher** writes the provenance hypothesis to `PROVENANCE.md`; the **provenance skeptic** judges it. The provenance researcher reads `FORENSICS.md` on each revision pass to incorporate material and technical evidence.
- **forensic loop** — the **conservation science examiner** writes the examination methods, findings, and interpretations to `FORENSICS.md`; the **forensic methods auditor** judges them. The conservation science examiner reads `PROVENANCE.md` on each revision pass to align the testing plan and interpretation with the latest attribution and chain-of-custody claims.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `case.md` — the original case brief, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `PROVENANCE.md` — the provenance and attribution hypothesis. Provenance researcher writes; provenance skeptic reviews; conservation science examiner and forensic methods auditor read it to know which artist, period, workshop, owners, and custody claims the material evidence must test.
- `FORENSICS.md` — the conservation-science examination report and its findings. Conservation science examiner writes; forensic methods auditor reviews; provenance researcher and provenance skeptic read it to ground attribution and provenance claims in observed material facts.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The authentication case manager reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a provenance-first pipeline where documentary research must settle before lab work starts, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. Provenance may tighten after seeing pigment, support, or imaging results; forensic interpretation may change after seeing a refined attribution, date range, or custody gap.

# f1: case context

You are working on a fine-art authentication case. The object may be a painting, drawing, print, sculpture, mixed-media work, or an attributed studio or workshop piece. The original case is given as a natural-language brief in `case.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: provenance researcher's deliverable

Produce the provenance and attribution analysis for the object and write it to `PROVENANCE.md`. A complete analysis covers four kinds of claim, each justified with documentary evidence, art-historical reasoning, or a clearly labeled inference.

- **Attribution hypothesis** — proposed artist, workshop, circle, follower, period, or school; explain the level of confidence and distinguish autograph work from studio, later copy, pastiche, or misattribution.
- **Chain of custody** — known owners, dealers, galleries, exhibitions, sales, inheritances, and transfers. Mark documented links separately from inferred or missing links, and identify any gaps that matter for authenticity or legal risk.
- **Comparative and contextual fit** — stylistic comparisons, catalogue raisonné entries, exhibition history, labels, inscriptions, seals, inventory numbers, signatures, and known variants or copies. State what matches and what conflicts.
- **Chronology and risk issues** — whether the claimed date is plausible; whether there are restitution, looting, export, forgery-ring, or posthumous-production concerns; and whether the object’s story depends on a fragile or circular source.

Ground your claims against `FORENSICS.md` if it has reported findings. A proposed 1620s attribution that conflicts with a twentieth-century synthetic pigment, modern stretcher, or anachronistic ground layer means either the provenance needs revision or the forensic interpretation has a problem — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. “The Knoedler invoice of 1934 supports ownership by A before the 1951 exhibition, but no source yet connects the work to the artist’s estate” is fine; “the provenance seems promising” is not.

# f3: provenance analysis toolbox

Match the tool to the case structure; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in provenance and attribution analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Archival provenance reconstruction | Ownership chains, estate inventories, dealer ledgers, correspondence, customs records |
| Catalogue raisonné and exhibition-history checks | Works by well-documented artists; identifying accepted works, rejected works, variants, and gaps |
| Connoisseurship and comparative stylistic analysis | Schools, workshops, copies, and disputed attributions where visual handling is central |
| Signature, inscription, label, and seal analysis | Objects with artist signatures, collector marks, gallery labels, inventory numbers, or wax seals |
| Market and sales-record analysis | Auction histories, gallery transactions, suspicious price jumps, repeated descriptions across sales |
| Wartime, colonial, and export-risk research | Restitution exposure, forced sales, illicit excavation, national patrimony and export restrictions |
| Technical-art-history correlation | Matching documented practice to support, ground, pigment, binder, tool marks, and working method |
| Bayesian or weight-of-evidence synthesis | Combining weak documentary, stylistic, and material indicators without overstating certainty |

# f4: forensic examination report

Read `case.md` and `PROVENANCE.md`, then produce the conservation-science examination:

- Include every relevant method used or recommended. Document imaging, microscopy, UV/IR/visible observations, X-radiography, XRF, Raman, FTIR, GC-MS, dendrochronology, radiocarbon, fiber analysis, alloy analysis, or other tests as appropriate to the object. State sampling locations, instrument limitations, calibration or reference standards, and whether a result is destructive or non-destructive.
- Make the examination testable against `PROVENANCE.md`. If the provenance hypothesis requires a pre-1850 canvas, a particular workshop ground, a historically available pigment, or an original signature, your report should explicitly address that material claim.
- Carry the examination through to a clear interpretive conclusion — do not stop at raw observations. If a result is inconclusive, explain why and what additional test would resolve it. Do not hide contamination, overpaint, restoration, sampling uncertainty, or chain-of-custody limitations.
- Sanity-check the findings against `PROVENANCE.md`. A pigment that was not commercially available during the claimed period, a support inconsistent with the proposed region, or a signature sitting on top of later varnish must be flagged rather than smoothed over.

Write the examination methods, findings, and interpretations up in `FORENSICS.md`.

# f5: forensic report discipline

Keep `FORENSICS.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page note; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history, raw instrument logs, and extended spectra tables into a separate `CHANGELOG.md` or appendix file. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: provenance review

Review `PROVENANCE.md` against `case.md` and against the findings reported in `FORENSICS.md` (if any).

- **Faithfulness** — does the provenance hypothesis correctly describe the object and claims stated in `case.md`? Check artist names, dates, medium, dimensions, inscriptions, alleged owners, and the exact authentication question.
- **Source soundness** — for each attribution or custody claim, walk through the cited document, comparison, or inference. Flag circular citations, vague “private collection” claims, unverified dealer lore, mismatched catalogue entries, and leaps from resemblance to authorship.
- **Forensic grounding** — open `FORENSICS.md`. If the examination reports pigments, supports, binders, tool marks, imaging, or condition evidence, do they fit the proposed artist, period, and chain of custody? A provenance claim that ignores an anachronistic material or explains it away without evidence should be tightened or explicitly marked as doubtful.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the provenance hypothesis yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: forensic methods review

Review `FORENSICS.md` against `case.md` and against `PROVENANCE.md`.

- Inspect the reported findings for anomalies, overconfident interpretations, missing controls, or material claims that contradict the object description in `case.md`.
- Open `PROVENANCE.md`. For every attribution, date, custody, or art-historical claim that depends on material evidence, write an audit check into a separate `METHODS_AUDIT.md`: does the examination actually test the claimed period, support, pigment, binder, signature, label, restoration history, or workshop practice? Apply the check to the report. Name any material fact or claim that fails.
- Read the examination methodology and spot-check it for lab weaknesses — undocumented sampling locations, missing calibration, unsuitable technique, contamination risk, overinterpretation of spectra, or conclusions that go beyond the data. A method flaw that silently supports a false attribution is worse than one that openly yields no result.
- Treat the result as finished only when every material observation is tied to the case question, every provenance-relevant technical claim has been addressed, and the findings are consistent with the stated limits of the methods — not merely when the report sounds scientific.
- Check the length of `FORENSICS.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `FORENSICS.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — “this cannot be authenticated, but I’m telling you it cannot” is still a failing result if the analysis or examination is careless, unsupported, or incomplete.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never research attribution, interpret lab evidence, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Dispatch the provenance researcher for the first provenance step (provenance researcher, then provenance skeptic), then the conservation science examiner for the first forensic step (conservation science examiner, then forensic methods auditor).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one provenance step with one forensic step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even a convincing-looking provenance may need to revise once the lab report presents actual material findings.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 provenance steps + 10 forensic steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer’s `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent’s brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: provenance revision pass

The provenance and attribution analysis lives in `PROVENANCE.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the provenance skeptic as a loop, alternating with the forensic loop.

- If `PROVENANCE.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the provenance hypothesis from scratch based on `case.md`.
- If `PROVENANCE.md` already exists, it carries the provenance skeptic’s `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `FORENSICS.md` and look at the latest reported findings — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your attribution, dating, custody chain, or risk assessment. Revise `PROVENANCE.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any forensic evidence that changes your analysis. Don’t discard the previous analysis and start over, and don’t leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: forensic revision pass

The conservation-science examination report lives in `FORENSICS.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the forensic methods auditor as a loop, alternating with the provenance loop.

- If `FORENSICS.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the examination report from scratch based on `case.md`.
- If `FORENSICS.md` already exists, it carries the forensic methods auditor’s `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `PROVENANCE.md` and check whether the latest provenance hypothesis introduces attribution, date, custody, or art-historical claims your current examination does not yet address — for example a narrower date range, a claimed workshop practice, a questioned signature, or a custody gap involving restoration. Revise `FORENSICS.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest provenance hypothesis. Don’t discard the previous report and start over, and don’t leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the authentication case manager: what you did, what difficulties you hit (or didn’t resolve), and any open questions.
