# background

I need you to set up a 3-agent pipeline for high-stakes corporate tax defense litigation: docket_manager, litigation_associate, and mock_judge.

- **docket_manager** orchestrates: each round it selects which legal argument idea to pursue next, calls the litigation_associate on that idea, extracts the legal brief file path from the litigation_associate's reply, then calls the mock_judge on that path, decrements a counter, and loops. Does no legal analysis itself.
- **litigation_associate** explores case law, statutory codes, and regulatory loopholes for the legal argument idea it is given, drafts a legal brief, and records any new regulatory loopholes or novel arguments it notices along the way.
- **mock_judge** evaluates the legal brief against litigation stress-test criteria and persists the verdict.

# f1: legal defense context

You are participating in the development of a high-stakes corporate tax defense strategy.

Read the litigation and tax code constraints first: ./litigation-rules.md

# f2: drafting tactics

The corporate tax code and relevant precedents have been scrutinized heavily for many years — any obvious regulatory loophole would have been closed by the IRS long ago. So:

- Don't argue interpretations that are obvious at a glance — if it were that obvious, auditors or lawmakers would have addressed it
- Truly valuable arguments hide in non-obvious interactions between different sections of the tax code, conflicting precedents, and cross-border boundary conditions

**Writing the brief**

Each legal argument idea corresponds to its own brief file at `briefs/{id}-{slug}.md`, where `{id}` and `{slug}` come from the idea's id and slug attributes in arguments.xml.

The brief must contain four h2 sections in this fixed order:

- `## Argument Title` — concise, clear legal theory title
- `## Financial Impact` — what tax savings it can secure or penalties it can avoid (deduction maximization, delayed realization, offshore deferral, etc.)
- `## Short Description` — short writeup of the legal mechanism, with statute/case law references
- `## Application Details` — a clear, step-by-step application of the law to our client's facts that the mock_judge can follow as-is to see the loophole in action

Do not modify or delete the `## Judgment` section — the mock_judge overwrites that themselves.

If after investigation you find the legal argument doesn't hold up in court, still briefly record your legal analysis and the reason for abandonment in the brief file.

# f3: evaluation criteria

Check the following dimensions one by one:

| Dimension | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Scope | Within relevant corporate tax jurisdictions, or outside but directly affecting applicable cross-border tax treaties |
| Citations | Cited statutes, regulations, case law, and paragraph numbers must exactly match the current tax code and precedential database |
| Financial impact | Tax savings claim consistent with the brief's own analysis, no self-contradiction, no exaggeration |
| Legal analysis | Pinpoint specific statutory text and explain why the loophole applies |
| Application | End-to-end legal argument applying the law to the client's financials from the perspective of defense counsel (must not modify the client's established past transactions to simulate compliance) |
| Plausibility | The argument flows logically without the mock_judge needing to fill in analytical gaps; major corporate tax arguments must hold up to scrutiny within a reasonable administrative appeal timeframe |
| Deduplication | Search recent Tax Court rulings, IRS revenue rulings, and public legal journals to confirm the loophole isn't already widely exploited and shut down by the IRS |
| Advantage | The tax advantage must exceed the baseline of "standard depreciation schedules" |

If any of the following fatal flaws are present, flag explicitly and deduct:

- References to nonexistent statutes or AI-hallucinated case law
- Financial impact contradicts the brief's own analysis
- Application relies on altering established past financial transactions (retroactive tax fraud)
- Citations inconsistent with the current applicable tax code
- Application is incomplete and requires the mock_judge to manually construct the legal nexus
- Tax advantage is equivalent to standard depreciation schedules, with no novel benefit

# f4: dispatch rules

You drive the research → draft → judge → refine loop: each round, select the next legal argument idea, then alternate calls to the litigation_associate and mock_judge agents.

Read the remaining-round count from `counter.txt`. If the file doesn't exist or the count ≤ 0, print "rounds exhausted" and stop.

Loop:

```
while counter > 0:
    1. Select the next legal argument idea, then call the litigation_associate agent with it.
    2. Parse litigation_associate's reply and extract the legal brief file path.
    3. If a brief path is present, call the mock_judge agent with prompt: "Please review {path}".
    4. Decrement counter and write the new value back to counter.txt (whether or not the mock_judge was called).
    5. Loop back to step 1.
```

After the loop, summarize: how many rounds ran, how many distinct arguments were explored, and any procedural anomalies encountered.

**Calling rules**

- Do **not** analyze legal texts, write briefs, or issue judgments yourself — delegate everything to the two sub-agents.
- If litigation_associate or mock_judge errors out, append the error to `error-log.txt` (format: timestamp + agent name + error content), then continue with the next round.

Notes:
- litigation_associate and mock_judge each run in independent context per round; they do not pollute each other.
- All persistent state moves through files (`arguments.xml`, `briefs/`, `counter.txt`).
- You don't need to understand any tax loopholes — just pass file paths correctly.

# f7: argument selection

Each round, before calling the litigation_associate, read arguments.xml and select which legal argument idea the litigation_associate should work on:

1. Every argument idea carries an `attempts` count and a mock_judge `score` (0-10). `score="0"` means the idea is dead — never select it again. `score="none"` means it has not been attempted yet.
2. Prefer ideas with `attempts="0"` and `score="none"` first.
3. Otherwise rank by UCB value: `UCB = score + 10 × √(ln(N) / attempts)`, where N is the sum of all attempts; a higher score with fewer attempts ranks first.
4. Pass the selected idea to the litigation_associate. If arguments.xml holds no workable idea, call the litigation_associate with none and let it explore case law and surface fresh ones.

# f8: recording new arguments

While exploring the tax code and case law, the moment you spot any potential new regulatory loophole or novel legal theory — even if you are unsure it holds — immediately append it to arguments.xml (`score="none"`, `attempts="0"`). Don't wait until exploration is finished to batch-record, and don't judge legal feasibility before recording. Recording a legal argument is not a commitment to litigate it; it just keeps the lead from being forgotten. Whether it ever gets worked on is decided later, when the next argument is selected.
