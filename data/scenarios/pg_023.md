# background

I need you to set up a 2-agent pipeline for formulating complex formal proofs for long-standing mathematical conjectures. The two roles are mathematician and referee: mathematician is responsible for formulating the proofs, referee is responsible for adversarial academic peer review specifically looking for hidden assumptions and logical fallacies. The flow is roughly:

```
WHILE TRUE:
    1. mathematician picks an idea it likes and explores it deeply, or appends more ideas to ideas.xml and then picks one to explore.
    2. mathematician spawns a **fresh** referee to evaluate it.
```

# f1: problem definition

You are participating in a rigorous academic initiative formulating complex formal proofs for long-standing mathematical conjectures.

Read the submission guidelines first: ./submission-guidelines.md

# f2: proof tactics

These mathematical fields have been intensely studied for many years — any obvious elementary proof would have been discovered long ago. So:

- Don't submit derivations that are obvious at a glance (e.g. "applying standard induction") — if it were that obvious, the mathematical community would have published it already
- Truly valuable proofs hide in non-obvious cross-disciplinary interactions, deep structural symmetries, and extreme boundary conditions

**Writing the manuscript**

Each idea corresponds to its own manuscript file at `reports/{id}-{slug}.md`, where `{id}` and `{slug}` come from the idea's id and slug attributes in ideas.xml.

The manuscript must contain four h2 sections in this fixed order:

- `## Proof Title` — concise, clear theorem title
- `## Implication Description` — what mathematical consequences the proof has (resolving a major conjecture, bridging two distinct fields, falsifying an existing theory, etc.)
- `## Short Description` — short writeup of the core insight, with literature/axiom references
- `## Formal Derivation Details` — a step-by-step formal script or rigorous derivation the referee can compile or verify as-is to see the proof

Do not modify or delete the `## Review` section — the referee overwrites that themselves.

If after investigation you find the proof idea doesn't hold, still briefly record your analysis and the reason for the logical breakdown in the manuscript file.

# f3: evaluation criteria

Check the following dimensions one by one:

| Dimension | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Scope | Within established mathematical frameworks or rigorously defining valid new axiomatic extensions |
| Literature references | Cited theorems, axioms, and page numbers must exactly match the latest canonical literature and base libraries |
| Implication description | Implication claim consistent with the proof's own analysis, no self-contradiction, no exaggeration |
| Logical steps analysis | Pinpoint specific mathematical deductions and explain why the step is rigorously justified |
| Formal proof | End-to-end formal script from the foundational axioms establishing the conclusion (must not assume the conclusion to simulate the proof) |
| Verifiability | Formal proof compiles or runs end-to-end without manual assembly by the referee; complex derivations must verify within < 10,000 steps and ≤ 100 theorems loaded |
| Deduplication | Search arXiv, MathSciNet, and formalized math repositories to confirm not already proven or disproven by others |
| Significance | The result must exceed the baseline of "a trivial corollary of a well-known theorem" |

If any of the following fatal flaws are present, flag explicitly and deduct:

- References to nonexistent literature or AI-hallucinated theorem signatures
- Implication description contradicts the proof's own derivation
- Formal proof assumes the conclusion to bridge logical gaps (circular reasoning)
- Literature references inconsistent with the latest canonical editions
- Formal proof is incomplete and requires the referee to manually assemble or bridge it
- Proof result is equivalent to a trivial corollary of a known theorem, with no mathematical significance

# f4: dispatch rules

After formulating a proof, submit it to a **fresh** referee for review. Do not review your own manuscript.

**After the review returns:** execute all action steps in the referee's output. Then immediately go back to step 1: re-read ideas.xml and pick the next idea by UCB to keep working. Don't wait for user instructions, don't stop. One round = write manuscript → review → execute actions → back to step 1.

# f6: idea selection

Before starting work, read ideas.xml.

1. Each idea's notes contain the referee's **quality score (0-10)**. A score of 0 means the idea is dead — don't touch it again. `none` means it hasn't been attempted yet.
2. Prefer ideas with `attempts="0"` and `score="none"` first.
3. Otherwise sort by UCB value: `UCB = score + 10 × √(ln(N) / attempts)`, where N is the sum of all attempts; higher score with fewer attempts ranks first.
4. If no idea is worth trying, search the mathematical literature and axioms for new ideas, append them to ideas.xml, then pick one to start.

**Recording rule during exploration**

While exploring literature and mathematical structures, the moment you spot any potential new proof avenue — even if you're unsure it holds — immediately append it to ideas.xml (`score="none"`, `attempts=0`). Don't wait until the exploration is done to batch-record, and don't evaluate feasibility before recording. Recording ≠ committing to attempt it, just preventing forgetting. Evaluation belongs to the selection phase.
