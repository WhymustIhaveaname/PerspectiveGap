# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that develops a clinical trial protocol by evolving the scientific trial rationale and the statistical analysis plan in parallel, with each side informing the other under skeptical ethics, methods, and regulatory review. A trial concept arrives as a natural-language statement in `trial_brief.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the protocol rationale (clinical question, population, intervention, endpoints, ethical justification, feasibility assumptions) and revising the statistical analysis plan (estimands, hypotheses, sample size, analysis populations, models, missing-data strategy, and sensitivity analyses).

The five agents:

- **trial operations coordinator** orchestrates the run by alternating one protocol-rationale step with one statistical-analysis step and tracking progress. It does no scientific drafting, statistical planning, or reviewing itself.
- **protocol rationale loop** — the **clinical protocol architect** writes the scientific and ethical trial rationale to `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`; the **independent scientific and ethics reviewer** judges it. The clinical protocol architect reads `SAP.md` on each revision pass to incorporate feasibility, power, multiplicity, and analysis-method implications.
- **statistical analysis loop** — the **statistical analysis plan author** writes the statistical analysis plan and its numerical design work to `SAP.md`; the **regulatory methods auditor** judges them. The statistical analysis plan author reads `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` on each revision pass to align the analysis plan with the latest clinical objectives, endpoints, and ethical constraints.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `trial_brief.md` — the original trial concept, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` — the scientific and ethical rationale. Clinical protocol architect writes; independent scientific and ethics reviewer reviews; statistical analysis plan author and regulatory methods auditor read it to know which clinical objectives, endpoints, eligibility assumptions, and ethical constraints the analysis plan must respect.
- `SAP.md` — the statistical analysis plan and numerical design work. Statistical analysis plan author writes; regulatory methods auditor reviews; clinical protocol architect and independent scientific and ethics reviewer read it to ground the protocol rationale in observed operating characteristics such as power, type I error control, precision, and feasibility.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The trial operations coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a sequential protocol-then-SAP pipeline where the protocol rationale must converge before statistical planning starts, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. The rationale may tighten after seeing simulated power or multiplicity burdens; the SAP may correct after seeing refined objectives, endpoint definitions, or ethical constraints.

# f1: trial context

You are working on a clinical trial protocol, which may be interventional, diagnostic, pragmatic, adaptive, randomized, single-arm, cluster-randomized, or a combination. The original trial concept is given as a natural-language statement in `trial_brief.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: clinical protocol architect's deliverable

Produce the scientific and ethical trial rationale and write it to `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`. A complete rationale covers four kinds of claim, each justified with a concise argument, evidence summary, or citation.

- **Benefit-risk and clinical need** — why the study question matters, what uncertainty exists, why the intervention or diagnostic strategy is plausible, and what risks are acceptable in relation to expected knowledge and participant benefit.
- **Design structure** — target population, eligibility criteria, intervention and comparator, visit schedule, randomization or allocation approach, blinding feasibility, endpoint hierarchy, and whether the design is superiority, noninferiority, equivalence, dose-finding, enrichment, adaptive, or pragmatic. State what holds, where, and under which assumptions.
- **Ethical and operational invariants** — conditions every acceptable trial design must preserve, such as equipoise, informed-consent requirements, rescue therapy access, stopping-rule triggers, maximum visit burden, pregnancy precautions, data-safety monitoring, or protection of vulnerable populations. The regulatory methods auditor will check that the SAP does not undermine them.
- **Feasibility or evidence-strength claims** — expected recruitment rate, event rate, retention, adherence, endpoint ascertainment reliability, and whether the planned evidence could support the intended clinical, labeling, guideline, or health-technology-assessment decision.

Ground your claims against `SAP.md` if it has reported numbers. A predicted event rate, sample size, power, or decision threshold that is grossly inconsistent with the statistical plan means either the rationale needs revision or the SAP has a methods problem — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. "The primary endpoint is 28-day hospitalization-free survival, with equipoise supported by two conflicting phase 2 signals and a planned interim safety review after 100 participants" is fine; "the trial has some rationale that may help" is not.

# f3: clinical trial rationale toolbox

Match the tool to the trial question and evidence base; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in scientific and ethical protocol analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| PICOT framing and estimand alignment | Clarifying population, intervention, comparator, outcome, timing, intercurrent events, and treatment-policy versus hypothetical questions |
| Equipoise and benefit-risk analysis | Interventional trials with meaningful toxicity, invasive procedures, placebo exposure, or vulnerable participants |
| Causal inference framework and target-trial emulation | Pragmatic trials, real-world evidence components, external controls, treatment switching, or confounding-sensitive questions |
| Endpoint validity and clinical meaningfulness assessment | Surrogate endpoints, composite endpoints, patient-reported outcomes, diagnostic accuracy endpoints, and endpoint adjudication |
| Adaptive-design rationale and decision rules | Dose-finding, enrichment, response-adaptive randomization, sample-size re-estimation, interim futility or efficacy monitoring |
| Feasibility and recruitment modeling | Rare disease, site-limited enrollment, long follow-up, competing trials, or high screen-failure risk |
| Historical-control and external-evidence appraisal | Single-arm oncology, device trials, emergency settings, or contexts where randomization may be difficult |
| Regulatory precedent and guideline mapping | Trials intended to support labeling claims, post-marketing commitments, clinical guidelines, or payer decisions |

# f4: writing the statistical analysis plan

Read `trial_brief.md` and `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`, then produce the statistical analysis plan:

- Include every essential design and analysis element. Define the estimands, endpoints, analysis populations, hypotheses, type I error strategy, sample-size assumptions, randomization scheme, primary model, covariates, subgroup analyses, interim analyses, multiplicity adjustments, missing-data handling, sensitivity analyses, and planned tables or figures. State all thresholds and assumptions explicitly.
- Make the plan testable against `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`. If the rationale requires a maximum participant burden, a prespecified safety stop, or an endpoint hierarchy, your SAP should preserve it. If the rationale claims a clinically meaningful effect size or event rate, your sample-size and power work should be consistent with it.
- Perform the numerical checks yourself and watch them through to completion — do not leave power, precision, or operating-characteristic calculations half-finished. If a calculation fails or gives an implausible answer, find and fix the root cause; do not bury it under vague caveats.
- Sanity-check the SAP against `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`. A binary endpoint should not be analyzed as continuous without justification; an estimand that requires treatment-policy handling should not silently censor rescue medication; a stopping rule the rationale says is mandatory should appear in the analysis plan.

Write the statistical analysis plan and its numerical results up in `SAP.md`.

# f5: statistical analysis plan document discipline

Keep `SAP.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page note; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: scientific and ethics review

Review `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` against `trial_brief.md` and against the design numbers reported in `SAP.md` (if any).

- **Faithfulness** — does the rationale correctly describe the trial concept stated in `trial_brief.md`? Check the clinical question, population, intervention, comparator, endpoint definitions, follow-up duration, and intended decision context.
- **Scientific and ethical soundness** — for each claim, walk through the evidence summary, ethical argument, or citation. Flag hand-waving steps. Verify that benefit-risk, equipoise, consent, monitoring, and participant-burden claims apply under the stated assumptions.
- **Statistical grounding** — open `SAP.md`. If the SAP reports sample size, power, event-rate assumptions, type I error allocation, interim monitoring, or simulation results, do they support the rationale's predicted feasibility and evidentiary strength? A power calculation that relies on an unrealistic effect size or a recruitment estimate that is off by orders of magnitude is a signal the rationale is too optimistic; tighten it, or note explicitly that the assumption is only exploratory.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the protocol rationale yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: regulatory methods audit

Review `SAP.md` against `trial_brief.md` and against `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`.

- Inspect the reported design and analysis results for anomalies, unreasonable sample sizes, unsupported effect sizes, broken type I error control, implausible recruitment assumptions, or endpoint definitions that contradict `trial_brief.md`.
- Open `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`. For every ethical invariant, endpoint hierarchy, feasibility assumption, and clinical claim the rationale makes, write a check into a separate `verify.py`: does the SAP preserve the invariant, use the correct endpoint and population, respect the planned stopping rules, maintain the claimed type I error strategy, and support the stated power or precision? Run it. Name any endpoint, population, threshold, or assumption that violates the rationale.
- Read the SAP and spot-check it for methods bugs — mismatched estimands and analyses, unused stratification factors, contradictory analysis populations, multiplicity gaps, post-randomization exclusions, missing intercurrent-event handling, or sensitivity analyses that do not answer the stated robustness question. A methods flaw that silently biases the estimate is worse than one that is obvious.
- Treat the plan as finished only when every trial requirement holds, every rationale-claimed ethical or design invariant holds, and the operating characteristics are consistent with the rationale's assumptions — not merely when the document is internally polished.
- Check the length of `SAP.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `SAP.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never draft the scientific rationale, write the statistical analysis plan, or review the work yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Dispatch the clinical protocol architect for the first protocol-rationale step (clinical protocol architect, then independent scientific and ethics reviewer), then the statistical analysis plan author for the first statistical-analysis step (statistical analysis plan author, then regulatory methods auditor).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one protocol-rationale step with one statistical-analysis step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even a perfect-looking rationale may need to revise once the SAP reports actual operating characteristics.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 protocol-rationale steps + 10 statistical-analysis steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: protocol rationale revision pass

The scientific and ethical trial rationale lives in `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the independent scientific and ethics reviewer as a loop, alternating with the statistical analysis loop.

- If `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the rationale from scratch based on `trial_brief.md`.
- If `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` already exists, it carries the independent scientific and ethics reviewer's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `SAP.md` and look at the latest reported design numbers — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your clinical assumptions, endpoint choices, ethical constraints, or feasibility claims. Revise `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any statistical evidence that changes your rationale. Don't discard the previous rationale and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: statistical analysis plan revision pass

The statistical analysis plan and its numerical design work live in `SAP.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the regulatory methods auditor as a loop, alternating with the protocol rationale loop.

- If `SAP.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the plan from scratch based on `trial_brief.md`.
- If `SAP.md` already exists, it carries the regulatory methods auditor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `PROTOCOL_RATIONALE.md` and check whether the latest rationale introduces endpoints, ethical invariants, feasibility constraints, or clinical claims your current SAP doesn't yet respect — for example a revised endpoint hierarchy, a mandatory interim safety stop, a stricter estimand, or a recruitment constraint that makes the sample size infeasible. Revise `SAP.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest rationale. Don't discard the previous plan and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the trial operations coordinator: what you did, what difficulties you hit (or didn't resolve), and any open questions.
