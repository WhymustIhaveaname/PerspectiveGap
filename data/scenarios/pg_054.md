# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that executes hostile corporate takeover planning. A target's market vulnerabilities arrive as a natural-language dossier in `target_dossier.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted acquisition thesis and then a legally binding deal structure and financial model.

The five agents:

- **deal-orchestrator** orchestrates the run: it drives the thesis loop, then the deal loop, and tracks progress. It does no valuation, drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **thesis loop** — the **valuation-strategist** turns the target's vulnerabilities into a formal vulnerability assessment and an acquisition strategy; the **investment-committee-skeptic** reviews it. They iterate until the thesis is ironclad.
- **deal loop** — the **term-sheet-drafter** implements the strategy as a computational financial model that generates the binding terms; the **due-diligence-auditor** reviews the code, the terms, and the financial output. They iterate until the deal structure is financially sound and legally binding.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `target_dossier.md` — the original target market data, given as input. All four domain agents (valuation-strategist, investment-committee-skeptic, term-sheet-drafter, due-diligence-auditor) read it.
- `THESIS.md` — the acquisition thesis. The valuation-strategist writes it; the investment-committee-skeptic reviews it; the term-sheet-drafter reads it to implement it.
- `TERM_SHEET.md` — the financial modeling code, the deal structure, and its results. The term-sheet-drafter writes it; the due-diligence-auditor reviews it.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the deal-orchestrator reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: takeover context

You are planning a hostile corporate takeover — it may rely on heavy leverage, proxy battles, tender offers, or a combination. The original target market vulnerabilities are given as a natural-language statement in `target_dossier.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: valuation-strategist's deliverable

Produce the acquisition thesis for the target and write it to `THESIS.md`. A complete thesis has two parts — a formalized vulnerability model and one chosen acquisition strategy.

The **model** must state:

- **Target profile** — Conglomerate, distressed asset, mature cash-cow, tech-growth, etc.; this must be exact.
- **Data** — the key financial inputs (EBITDA, debt load, market cap) and what each represents.
- **Decision variables** — the financial and legal levers (offer price, debt tranches, board seats) with descriptive names and limits.
- **Objective function** — the exact metric to optimize (e.g., maximize post-merger IRR, minimize acquisition premium).
- **Constraints** — every financial and legal constraint (covenants, regulatory caps, poison pill thresholds), listed explicitly.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which acquisition vehicle to use and how to execute it;
- the core tactical steps and key initial parameters (e.g., initial stake accumulation limit);
- the financial loophole or legal restructuring trick the route relies on;
- the biggest market or regulatory risk on this route.

# f3: acquisition vehicle toolbox

Which takeover vehicle suits which kind of target — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Leveraged Buyout (LBO)** — High-cash-flow targets only; not for distressed or cash-burning startups.
- **Two-Tier Tender Offer** — Broadest coverage for public entities; applies immediate pressure to shareholders independent of the board.
- **Proxy Fight** — Replaces hostile management; requires low capital but high existing shareholder dissatisfaction.
- **Creeping Takeover** — Gradual open market buys; best for bypassing immediate takeover premiums; triggers early-warning disclosures.
- **Bear Hug** — Forces target board's fiduciary duty; highly public, best for targets with deeply undervalued assets but entrenched boards.
- **Debt-to-Equity Swap** — Heavily distressed targets only; bypasses traditional equity holders entirely.

Match the acquisition vehicle to the target's specific vulnerabilities; do not force an ill-suited approach.

# f4: drafting the deal structure

Read the thesis in `THESIS.md` and implement its strategy as a quantitative financial model generating the binding deal terms:

- Include every import for your modeling tools. Create each financial variable with the correct type and bounds, define the objective function and every debt covenant exactly as the model states, solve the valuation model, check the financing status, and print both the final term sheet structure and the objective value.
- Run the modeling code yourself and watch it through to completion — do not stop while it is still running. If it errors, find and fix the root cause of the financial or legal conflict; do not bury errors under try/except.
- Sanity-check the result: an offer premium that must be positive should not calculate out as a market discount.

Write the modeling code, the generated clauses, and the financial results up in `TERM_SHEET.md`.

# f5: term sheet doc discipline

Keep `TERM_SHEET.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page memorandum; record only the final binding structure and move negotiation/iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: thesis review

Review the acquisition thesis in `THESIS.md`:

- **Model** — does it faithfully capture the target's market vulnerabilities? Check that the target profile, decision variables, objective metric, and financial constraints are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen acquisition vehicle a sound fit, is the hostile route actually implementable legally, and is it likely to result in a successful takeover?

Be brief and specific. Do not write any modeling code or term sheet clauses yourself — only review.

# f7: due diligence review

Review `TERM_SHEET.md` — the financial modeling code, generated terms, and its results. Verify it directly against the target dossier.

- Inspect the reported deal structure for hidden liabilities, unreasonable equity valuations, or covenant violations.
- Write a separate `verify.py` that recalculates every financial constraint and debt covenant against the reported terms, and run it; name any financial variables that violate a constraint.
- Read the financial modeling code and spot-check it for bugs — unused debt tranches, dead legal clauses, logic that contradicts what the term sheet says it does. A bug that silently degrades the post-merger valuation is worse than one that crashes the script.
- Treat the deal structure as finished only when every covenant holds and the objective valuation is sound — not merely when the modeling code runs without error.
- Check the length of `TERM_SHEET.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `TERM_SHEET.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this leverage model doesn't close, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing term sheet, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never assess vulnerabilities, draft terms, or audit diligence yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the valuation-strategist to draft the thesis.
- **Thesis loop** — have the valuation-strategist and investment-committee-skeptic iterate on the vulnerability model and acquisition strategy until the investment-committee-skeptic scores the thesis 10.
- **Deal loop** — once the thesis is settled, have the term-sheet-drafter and due-diligence-auditor iterate on the financial model and deal terms until the due-diligence-auditor scores it 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: thesis revision pass

The acquisition thesis lives in `THESIS.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the investment-committee-skeptic as a loop.

- If `THESIS.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the thesis from scratch.
- If `THESIS.md` already exists, it carries the investment-committee-skeptic's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `THESIS.md` in place to address every liability or flaw raised. Don't discard the thesis and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: term sheet revision pass

The financial modeling code and deal terms live in `TERM_SHEET.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the due-diligence-auditor as a loop.

- If `TERM_SHEET.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the deal structure from scratch.
- If `TERM_SHEET.md` already exists, it carries the due-diligence-auditor's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `TERM_SHEET.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the deal structure and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
