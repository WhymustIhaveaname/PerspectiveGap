# background

I need you to set up a 6-agent pipeline for civil litigation preparation. A case arrives as a factual and procedural summary in `casefile.md`, and the pipeline turns it into vetted legal theories and then a working motion or brief that has been adversarially reviewed for weaknesses.

The six agents:

- **case coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the theory phase, then the drafting phase, and tracks progress. It does no legal analysis, drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **litigation strategist** is invoked once at init and produces K differentiated candidate legal theories, one per `theories/{id}-{slug}/THEORY.md`. It never enters the per-theory revision loop.
- **theory reviser** is invoked once per theory-refinement step in the loop. It revises a single assigned theory's `THEORY.md` based on the opposing-counsel analyst's last review for that theory. It never creates new theories.
- **opposing-counsel analyst** reviews the theory it is currently assigned to.
- **brief drafter** drafts the motion or brief for the theory it is currently assigned to.
- **brief challenge reviewer** adversarially reviews the drafted motion or brief for the theory it is currently assigned to.

The pipeline hands work off through theory-namespaced files:

- `casefile.md` — the original case summary, given as input. All five domain agents (litigation strategist, theory reviser, opposing-counsel analyst, brief drafter, brief challenge reviewer) read it.
- `theories/{id}-{slug}/THEORY.md` — one theory per directory, K theories total. The litigation strategist writes K differentiated candidates at init; the theory reviser revises a specific assigned theory in the loop; the opposing-counsel analyst reviews the theory it is assigned to; the brief drafter reads the theory it is assigned to.
- `theories/{id}-{slug}/BRIEF.md` — the motion or brief draft and supporting analysis for that theory. The brief drafter writes it; the brief challenge reviewer reviews it.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the case coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

The case coordinator round-robins across the K theories, first in a theory phase (each theory iterates its theory reviser ↔ opposing-counsel analyst loop) and then in a drafting phase (each theory iterates its brief drafter ↔ brief challenge reviewer loop). Cross-theory reading does not happen: an agent assigned to one theory never reads another theory's files.

# f1: case context

You are working on a civil litigation matter — it may involve contract claims, tort claims, statutory causes of action, equitable relief, procedural motions, or a combination. The original case summary is given in `casefile.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: litigation strategist's deliverable

Produce K differentiated candidate legal theories, each in its own `theories/{id}-{slug}/THEORY.md`. The K theories should differ meaningfully in their legal route so distinct approaches can be compared downstream, not in trivial ways like changing headings or rephrasing the same argument.

A complete theory in any `THEORY.md` has two parts — a formalized case theory and one chosen litigation strategy.

The **case theory** must state:

- **Procedural posture and requested relief** — for example, complaint, motion to dismiss opposition, summary judgment motion, preliminary injunction, sanctions motion, or discovery motion; this must be exact.
- **Key facts** — the facts that matter legally, including dates, parties, communications, documents, injuries, damages, and procedural deadlines where relevant.
- **Claims, defenses, or issues presented** — each cause of action, affirmative defense, procedural issue, or remedy being advanced.
- **Legal standard** — the governing rule, elements, burden of proof, and any controlling or persuasive authority needed to frame the argument.
- **Element-by-element application** — every required element or factor, listed explicitly with the facts that support it and any known weaknesses.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which procedural vehicle or filing to use and how to frame it;
- the argument sequence and the key evidence or authority to lead with;
- the doctrinal hook, burden-shifting rule, presumption, waiver point, or procedural leverage the route relies on;
- the biggest litigation risk on this route.

# f3: civil-litigation theory toolbox

Which litigation route suits which kind of civil matter — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Rule 12 motion practice** — best for legal insufficiency on the pleadings, jurisdictional defects, limitations apparent on the face of the complaint, or failure to plead required elements; not suited to fact-heavy merits disputes.
- **Summary judgment** — best when discovery has created a record showing no genuine dispute of material fact; requires admissible evidence and careful citation to the record.
- **Preliminary injunction** — best for urgent equitable relief where irreparable harm, likelihood of success, balance of equities, and public interest can be shown; weak if damages would be adequate.
- **Contract theory** — best where a valid agreement, breach, causation, and damages can be proved through documents, performance history, and communications.
- **Tort theory** — best where duty, breach, causation, and damages can be tied to concrete conduct; often vulnerable on duty, foreseeability, privilege, or economic-loss limitations.
- **Statutory claim or defense** — best when the statute supplies clear elements, remedies, fee shifting, limitations periods, or administrative prerequisites; check exhaustion, notice, standing, and preemption.
- **Equitable theory** — best where legal remedies are inadequate and facts support fairness-based relief such as estoppel, unjust enrichment, constructive trust, or specific performance; highly fact-sensitive.
- **Discovery or evidentiary motion** — best when the immediate objective is compelling production, excluding unreliable evidence, protecting privileged material, or shaping the record before merits briefing.

Match the procedural vehicle and legal theory to the case posture and available facts; do not force an ill-suited route.

# f4: drafting the brief

Read the theory in your assigned theory's `THEORY.md` and implement its strategy as a motion or brief:

- Include the caption placeholder, requested relief, introduction, factual background, legal standard, argument sections, and conclusion. State the governing rule accurately, apply every required element or factor, and cite the facts and authorities the theory depends on.
- Draft the argument through to completion — do not leave placeholders such as “add citation,” “insert facts,” or “research later.” If a weakness appears, address it directly rather than hiding it.
- Sanity-check the filing: the requested relief should match the procedural posture, factual assertions should be supported by the casefile or identified exhibits, and the argument should not rely on elements the record cannot support.

Write the draft and its supporting notes up in your assigned theory's `BRIEF.md`. Do not read or touch other theories' files.

# f5: brief doc discipline

Keep your assigned theory's `BRIEF.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page note; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history into the theory's `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: theory review

Review the theory in your assigned theory's `THEORY.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned theory; do not look at other theories.

- **Case theory** — does it faithfully capture the case? Check that the procedural posture, requested relief, claims or defenses, legal standard, and element-by-element factual application are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen procedural vehicle and argument route a sound fit, is the route actually draftable from the available facts and law, and is it likely to survive adversarial opposition?

Be brief and specific. Do not draft any motion or brief yourself — only review.

# f7: brief review

Review your assigned theory's `BRIEF.md` — the motion or brief draft and its supporting analysis. Verify it directly against the casefile. Operate strictly on the assigned theory; do not look at other theories.

- Inspect the requested relief and argument for anomalies, overclaims, unsupported factual assertions, missing elements, or concessions an opponent could exploit.
- Write a separate `authority_check.md` under the theory's directory that re-checks every required element, factual assertion, record citation, and legal authority against the reported draft; name any facts, elements, or citations that do not hold up.
- Read the brief and spot-check it for drafting defects — unused facts, dead arguments, misstated standards, contradictory positions, missing burdens, or logic that contradicts what the draft says it proves. A defect that silently weakens the filing is worse than one that is obvious.
- Treat the draft as finished only when every required element or factor is addressed, the requested relief is procedurally available, and the argument is legally sound — not merely when the prose reads smoothly.
- Check the length of the theory's `BRIEF.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the theory's `BRIEF.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never analyze the law, draft, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the litigation strategist once to draft K differentiated candidate theories, each in its own `theories/{id}-{slug}/` directory. After it returns, read the directory list to learn the K theory ids. The litigation strategist is not invoked again after this.
- **Theory phase** — round-robin across the K theories. For each theory in turn, dispatch theory reviser (revise that theory based on its opposing-counsel analyst's review) then opposing-counsel analyst (score that theory). After one round-robin pass through all K theories, start the next pass. A theory is considered done in this phase when its opposing-counsel analyst score reaches 10; stop scheduling more theory-phase calls for it but continue with the others. After all K theories hit opposing-counsel analyst 10, or each theory has had 10 theory-phase iterations, move to the drafting phase.
- **Drafting phase** — round-robin across the K theories. For each theory in turn, dispatch brief drafter (revise that theory's brief based on its brief challenge reviewer's review) then brief challenge reviewer (score that theory's brief). The pipeline stops when any theory reaches brief challenge reviewer score 10, or each theory has had 10 drafting-phase iterations.
- When dispatching, always pass the theory's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `THEORY.md` / `BRIEF.md` to operate on.
- Keep the current phase, per-theory iteration counts, per-theory latest scores, and current theory index in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant theory's reviewer `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: theory revision pass

Read your assigned theory's `THEORY.md`. It carries the opposing-counsel analyst's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the theory's `THEORY.md` in place to address every point raised. Preserve the two-part structure (formalized case theory plus chosen litigation strategy). Don't discard the theory and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. You revise; you do not create new theories. Do not read or touch other theories' files.

# f11: brief revision pass

Your motion or brief and its supporting analysis live in your assigned theory's `BRIEF.md`.

- If your assigned theory's `BRIEF.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that theory: produce the draft from scratch based on the theory's `THEORY.md`.
- If the theory's `BRIEF.md` already exists, it carries the brief challenge reviewer's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the theory's `BRIEF.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the brief and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. Do not read or touch other theories' files.
