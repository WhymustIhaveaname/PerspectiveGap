# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline for appellate litigation preparation. The team develops a case theory and a draft appellate brief in parallel, with each side informing the other. The case materials arrive as a record-and-issues packet in `record.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the case theory (standard of review, doctrinal path, preservation, evidentiary fit, and remedies) and revising the draft brief (a filing-ready argument that implements those choices).

The five agents:

- **litigation workflow coordinator** orchestrates the run by alternating one case-theory step with one brief-drafting step and tracking progress. It does no legal analysis, drafting, cite-checking, or reviewing itself.
- **case-theory loop** — the **case theory counsel** writes the litigation theory to `CASE_THEORY.md`; the **devil's-advocate legal reviewer** judges it. The case theory counsel reads `BRIEF.md` on each revision pass to incorporate what the draft reveals about record gaps, weak authorities, and argument feasibility.
- **brief loop** — the **appellate brief drafter** writes the draft appellate brief and supporting citation notes to `BRIEF.md`; the **opposing-counsel brief examiner** judges them. The appellate brief drafter reads `CASE_THEORY.md` on each revision pass to align the brief with the latest strategic theory.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `record.md` — the original case packet, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `CASE_THEORY.md` — the case theory. Case theory counsel writes; devil's-advocate legal reviewer reviews; appellate brief drafter and opposing-counsel brief examiner read it to know which standards, doctrines, preservation positions, and remedies the brief must respect.
- `BRIEF.md` — the draft appellate brief and citation support. Appellate brief drafter writes; opposing-counsel brief examiner reviews; case theory counsel and devil's-advocate legal reviewer read it to ground the theory in actual authorities, record citations, and drafting constraints.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The litigation workflow coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a strategy-then-drafting pipeline where the case theory must settle before the brief starts, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. The case theory may tighten after the draft exposes a preservation problem or adverse precedent; the brief may change after the theory refines the standard of review, narrows the issue, or changes the requested relief.

# f1: case context

You are working on an appellate matter. The case may involve civil, criminal, administrative, constitutional, statutory, or mixed issues. The original case packet is given in `record.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: case theory counsel's deliverable

Produce the case theory for the appeal and write it to `CASE_THEORY.md`. A complete theory covers four kinds of claim, each justified with record support, controlling authority, or a clear litigation judgment.

- **Issue framing and relief** — identify the appellate issues, the precise ruling or judgment being challenged, the requested disposition, and any fallback relief.
- **Doctrinal path** — state the governing rule, standard of review, burden allocation, elements, exceptions, and any threshold doctrines such as jurisdiction, mootness, waiver, forfeiture, exhaustion, harmless error, or preservation.
- **Record anchors** — identify the facts and procedural events every viable argument must preserve: objections made or not made, transcript pages, trial-court findings, exhibits, agency findings, and judgment language.
- **Risk and response** — identify adverse precedent, bad facts, likely waiver arguments, harmless-error theories, credibility obstacles, and the best answer to each.

Ground your theory against `BRIEF.md` if it has exposed drafting constraints or citation problems. If a theory depends on an authority the brief cannot support, or on a fact the record does not actually contain, the theory needs revision. Be concrete. “Abuse-of-discretion review applies to the evidentiary ruling, but the embedded Rule 702 interpretation is reviewed de novo under Smith v. Jones” is useful; “we have a strong fairness argument” is not.

# f3: case theory toolbox

Match the legal tool to the case structure; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in appellate case-theory analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Standard-of-review leverage | Issues where de novo review, clear-error review, abuse-of-discretion review, or substantial-evidence review changes the likelihood of reversal |
| Preservation and waiver analysis | Arguments vulnerable to forfeiture, invited error, exhaustion, failure to object, inadequate briefing, or jurisdictional default |
| Statutory interpretation canons | Textual disputes involving plain meaning, whole-act rule, expressio unius, ejusdem generis, surplusage, constitutional avoidance, or agency deference |
| Constitutional tiers and burden shifting | Equal protection, due process, First Amendment, search-and-seizure, confrontation, or other rights-based appeals |
| Harmless-error and prejudice analysis | Trial error, evidentiary error, instructional error, sentencing error, or administrative procedural error where reversal depends on effect |
| Record-based fact reconstruction | Appeals where transcript chronology, exhibits, findings, objections, or agency record citations determine viability |
| Adverse-precedent distinction | Cases controlled or threatened by unfavorable authority that must be distinguished, limited, preserved for higher review, or candidly confronted |
| Remedy and jurisdiction mapping | Appeals involving finality, appealability, remand scope, vacatur, resentencing, injunctions, mandamus, or administrative remand |

# f4: drafting the brief

Read `record.md` and `CASE_THEORY.md`, then draft the appellate brief:

- Include the required appellate components appropriate to the matter: jurisdictional statement, issues presented, statement of the case, statement of facts, summary of argument, argument headings, standard of review, conclusion, and any necessary certificate or addendum notes.
- Make the draft testable against `CASE_THEORY.md`. If the theory depends on de novo review, the brief should state and support that standard. If the theory identifies a preservation point, the brief should cite the record page where it happened. If the theory chooses a fallback remedy, the conclusion should request it.
- Cite controlling authority accurately and use precise record citations. Do not rely on invented cases, vague “see generally” support, or facts not found in `record.md`.
- Read the draft through as if filing it. If an argument contradicts the record, omits an element, misstates the standard of review, or ignores adverse authority, fix the root cause rather than hiding it in a footnote.
- Sanity-check the brief against `CASE_THEORY.md`. A fact that must be preserved should have a record cite; a doctrine the theory says controls should appear in the argument; a requested remedy should match the identified error.

Write the draft brief and its citation notes in `BRIEF.md`.

# f5: brief doc discipline

Keep `BRIEF.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a compact internal appellate draft; record only the current filing-ready position and move iteration-by-iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: case theory review

Review `CASE_THEORY.md` against `record.md` and against the draft arguments and citation support reported in `BRIEF.md` (if any).

- **Faithfulness** — does the theory correctly describe the case in `record.md`? Check procedural posture, parties, rulings, issue preservation, facts, record citations, and requested relief.
- **Doctrinal soundness** — for each legal claim, walk through the cited authority or reasoning. Flag unsupported leaps, wrong standards of review, missing elements, jurisdictional defects, or failure to confront controlling adverse precedent.
- **Draft grounding** — open `BRIEF.md`. If the draft cites authorities or record pages, do they support the theory's route to reversal or affirmance? A theory that cannot be translated into a supported brief is not merely “strategic”; it is unstable. Tighten it, or note explicitly that the draft exposes a record or authority problem.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the case theory yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: brief review

Review `BRIEF.md` against `record.md` and against `CASE_THEORY.md`.

- Inspect the draft for anomalies, unsupported factual assertions, unreasonable remedy requests, contradictions with the procedural posture, or arguments that the appellate court cannot reach.
- Open `CASE_THEORY.md`. For every standard of review, preservation claim, doctrinal element, adverse-precedent distinction, and requested remedy the theory claims, create a separate `audit.md`: does the brief support it with authority, pin cites, record citations, and argument text? Name any fact, case, doctrine, or remedy that fails the audit.
- Read the brief closely and spot-check it for legal-drafting bugs — missing elements, miscited authority, record cites that do not support the sentence, arguments that waive better points, or rhetoric that contradicts the selected theory. A subtle misstatement that invites an appellee's waiver or harmless-error response is worse than an obvious typo.
- Treat the draft as finished only when every reachable issue is supported, every theory-claimed record anchor is cited, every doctrinal element is argued, and the requested relief is consistent with the theory — not merely when the document reads smoothly.
- Check the length of `BRIEF.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `BRIEF.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being candid about its own shortcomings — “this argument probably fails, but I am telling you it fails” is still a failing work product, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never analyze, draft, cite-check, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Dispatch the case theory counsel for the first case-theory step (case theory counsel, then devil's-advocate legal reviewer), then the appellate brief drafter for the first brief step (appellate brief drafter, then opposing-counsel brief examiner).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one case-theory step with one brief step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even a perfect-looking theory may need to revise once the draft exposes citation, preservation, or record problems.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 case-theory steps + 10 brief steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: case theory revision pass

The case theory lives in `CASE_THEORY.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the devil's-advocate legal reviewer as a loop, alternating with the brief loop.

- If `CASE_THEORY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the theory from scratch based on `record.md`.
- If `CASE_THEORY.md` already exists, it carries the devil's-advocate legal reviewer's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `BRIEF.md` and look at the latest draft, authorities, record citations, and citation notes — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your theory. Revise `CASE_THEORY.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any drafting evidence that changes your analysis. Don't discard the previous theory and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: brief revision pass

The draft appellate brief and citation notes live in `BRIEF.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the opposing-counsel brief examiner as a loop, alternating with the case-theory loop.

- If `BRIEF.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the brief from scratch based on `record.md`.
- If `BRIEF.md` already exists, it carries the opposing-counsel brief examiner's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `CASE_THEORY.md` and check whether the latest theory introduces standards of review, preservation positions, adverse-precedent distinctions, record anchors, or remedy choices your current draft does not yet support — for example a changed harmless-error response, a newly identified jurisdictional defect, or a record citation your argument lacks. Revise `BRIEF.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest theory. Don't discard the previous draft and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the litigation workflow coordinator: what you did, what difficulties you hit (or didn't resolve), and any open questions.
