# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that develops covert intelligence operations by planning high-level geopolitical threat assessments and ground-level field tactics in parallel, with each side informing the other. A mission arrives as a natural-language directive in `mission_brief.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the strategic analysis (risk thresholds, geopolitical dynamics, operational constraints) and revising the tactics (a logistical field plan that executes within those parameters).

The five agents:

- **mission-controller** orchestrates the run by alternating one assessment step with one tactical step and tracking progress. It does no intelligence gathering, tactical planning, or reviewing itself.
- **assessment loop** — the **intelligence-analyst** writes the geopolitical threat assessment to `ASSESSMENT.md`; the **red-team-adversary** judges it. The intelligence-analyst reads `TACTICS.md` on each revision pass to incorporate logistical realities and field constraints.
- **tactics loop** — the **field-tactician** writes the ground-level logistical and tactical plan to `TACTICS.md`; the **security-auditor** judges them. The field-tactician reads `ASSESSMENT.md` on each revision pass to align the operation with the latest strategic and geopolitical claims.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through plan-namespaced files:

- `mission_brief.md` — the original operational directive, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md` — one pre-existing plan per directory, K plans total, given as input (read-only). All four domain agents read the PLAN.md of the plan they are currently assigned to.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/ASSESSMENT.md` — the threat assessment for that plan. intelligence-analyst writes; red-team-adversary reviews; field-tactician and security-auditor read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/TACTICS.md` — the field tactics and logistics for that plan. field-tactician writes; security-auditor reviews; intelligence-analyst and red-team-adversary read it.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The mission-controller reads those scores to drive the loops.

Each plan develops independently. Cross-read between ASSESSMENT.md and TACTICS.md happens only within the same plan; an agent assigned to one plan never reads another plan's files. The mission-controller round-robins across the K plans: each plan gets one assessment step plus one tactical step per full cycle.

Within each plan, neither threat assessment nor field tactics finishes first. Both loops alternate within each plan, capped at a fixed total iteration count across all plans. Strategic assessments may tighten after seeing the plan's logistical field realities; tactics may adapt after seeing the plan's assessment's refined risk thresholds.

# f1: mission context

You are planning a covert intelligence operation, which may involve infiltration, extraction, sabotage, reconnaissance, or a combination. The original operational directive is given as a natural-language statement in `mission_brief.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: intelligence analyst's deliverable

Produce the threat assessment for the plan the mission-controller has assigned you to this round, and write it to that plan's `ASSESSMENT.md` (under `plans/{id}-{slug}/ASSESSMENT.md`). A complete analysis covers four kinds of claim, each justified with concrete intelligence or historical precedent.

- **Risk Thresholds** — upper bounds on acceptable casualties, operational exposure, or diplomatic fallout; lower bounds on the required intelligence yield or strategic impact.
- **Geopolitical Dynamics** — regional alliances, rivalries, surveillance grid overlaps, faction symmetries, or decomposability into localized, independent sub-operations. State what holds, where, and under which assumptions.
- **Operational Constraints** — absolute limits every operational phase must preserve (e.g., no lethal force, strict extraction windows, absolute deniability). The tactical plan must satisfy them; the security-auditor will check with `wargame.py`.
- **Timeline & Escalation** — worst-case time to target, expected timeline variations given randomized enemy patrols, or risk escalation rates if the operation is prolonged.

Ground your claims against the plan's `TACTICS.md` if it has reported logistics. A predicted exposure risk that is grossly off from the tactician's wargamed numbers means either the assessment needs revision or the tactics contain a flaw — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. "The maximum time to target is strictly bounded by the 4-hour satellite blind spot" is fine; "the target area has some guards that might delay us" is not. Do not read or aggregate across other plans' files.

# f3: intelligence analysis toolbox

Match the analytical tool to the geopolitical and operational context; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in threat assessment.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Threat Matrix Analysis / Worst-case Modeling | High-risk environments; establishing hard limits on exposure vs. potential payoff |
| Vulnerability Scoring | Target infrastructure; certifying a facility's weakest point for infiltration |
| Network Analysis / Link Analysis | Counter-terrorism or political nodes; identifying key figures for maximum disruption |
| Resource Allocation Optimization | Logistically strained operations; greedy deployment of finite surveillance assets |
| Game Theoretic Equilibria | Counter-intelligence; predicting rival agency or local faction counter-moves |
| Probabilistic Risk Assessment | Unpredictable patrol routes; high-probability bounds on extraction success or cover blowout |
| Operational Redlining | Categorical impossibilities; proving a direct approach is non-viable to justify a complex heuristic |
| Contingency Sensitivity Analysis | Fluid environments; assessing the stability of the mission if critical intel or initial assets are compromised |

# f4: drafting the field tactics

You operate on the plan the mission-controller has assigned you to this round. Read `mission_brief.md`, your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`, and your assigned plan's `ASSESSMENT.md`, then structure the logistical plan:

- Detail every asset, team composition, equipment loadout, and timeline precisely as the mission requires. Define the primary objective and every constraint exactly as the brief states. Lay out the logistics, check operational viability, and define both the insertion route and exfiltration point.
- Make the implementation verifiable against the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md`. If the assessment states a maximum exposure threshold R, your tactical plan must utilize routes and gear that keep risk below R. If the assessment states an operational constraint (e.g., deniability), your output must strictly enforce it.
- Wargame the operational plan yourself and trace it through to completion — do not stop mid-operation. If a tactical bottleneck is found, resolve the root cause; do not bury fatal flaws under assumptions of "adapt on the fly".
- Sanity-check the logistics against the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md`. An extraction window that requires 4 hours should not be squeezed into a 2.5-hour timeframe; an exposure limit the assessment says holds should hold.

Write the tactical plan and its logistics up in your assigned plan's `TACTICS.md`. Do not read or write any other plan's files.

# f5: tactical doc discipline

Keep `TACTICS.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page brief; record only the final operational state and move iteration-by-iteration planning history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: assessment review

Review your assigned plan's `ASSESSMENT.md` against `mission_brief.md`, against the plan's `PLAN.md`, and against the logistics reported in the plan's `TACTICS.md` (if any). Operate strictly on the assigned plan's files; do not look at other plans.

- **Faithfulness** — does the assessment correctly describe the geopolitical and operational context stated in `mission_brief.md` under the framing set by the plan's `PLAN.md`? Check definitions, asset types, constraint interpretations.
- **Analytical Soundness** — for each claim, walk through the justification or intelligence precedent. Flag hand-waving assumptions about enemy capabilities. Verify cited threat thresholds apply under the stated operational parameters.
- **Empirical Grounding** — open the plan's `TACTICS.md`. If the tactician reports expected detection rates or timeframes, do they fall within the assessment's predicted risk thresholds? A worst-case scenario that is overly pessimistic by orders of magnitude means the constraints are too rigid; adjust them, or note explicitly that the threshold is only a baseline.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the assessment yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong or strategically flawed, say which one and why.

# f7: tactics review

Review your assigned plan's `TACTICS.md` against `mission_brief.md`, against the plan's `PLAN.md`, and against the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned plan's files; do not look at other plans.

- Inspect the reported logistical plan for anomalies, unreasonable risk profiles, or operational violations stated directly in `mission_brief.md` or in the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- Open the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md`. For every risk threshold and operational constraint the assessment claims, write a procedural script (`wargame.py`) under the plan's directory to simulate the timeline and loadout: does the tactical plan satisfy the constraints, stay within the risk threshold, and achieve the required intel yield? Run it. Name any asset or route that violates.
- Read the tactical plan and spot-check it for fatal flaws — unused gear, dead-end extraction routes, logic that contradicts the chosen infiltration method. A flaw that silently compromises cover is worse than an abort criterion.
- Treat the plan as finished only when every parameter holds, every assessment-claimed constraint holds, and the timeline is consistent with the assessment's thresholds — not merely when the logistics superficially seem plausible.
- Check the length of the plan's `TACTICS.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the plan's `TACTICS.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this route is a death trap, but I'm telling you it is" is still a failing plan, not a passing one.

# f9: mission control

You orchestrate the run; you never analyze intel, plan tactics, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — discover the K plan directories under `plans/{id}-{slug}/`. Create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0`, `current_plan_idx: 0`, and a per-plan record holding each plan's id, last side, and latest assessment/tactics scores.
- **Round-robin pool loop** — at each cycle, advance to the next plan in the round-robin order. For that plan, dispatch one assessment step (intelligence-analyst, then red-team-adversary) and one tactical step (field-tactician, then security-auditor); then move to the next plan. After all K plans have each had one cycle, loop back to the first plan.
- When dispatching, always pass the plan's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `PLAN.md` / `ASSESSMENT.md` / `TACTICS.md` to operate on.
- Neither side terminates early on its own score within a plan; even a perfect-looking strategic assessment may need to revise once the field-tactician reports actual ground realities. The pool keeps cycling even after individual plans look done.
- Cap the total at 20 × K dispatches (10 assessment + 10 tactics per plan, summed across K plans). After the cap, stop and summarize per plan.
- Keep the current iteration count, current plan index, and per-plan latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant plan's reviewer `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved operational flaw, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + plan id + agent name + content) and continue with the next dispatch.

# f10: assessment revision pass

You operate on the plan the mission-controller has assigned you to this round. Your threat assessment lives in that plan's `ASSESSMENT.md`. You may be invoked on the same plan more than once — within each plan the pipeline runs you and the red-team-adversary as a loop, alternating with that plan's tactics loop, while the mission-controller round-robins across plans.

- If your assigned plan's `ASSESSMENT.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the analysis from scratch based on `mission_brief.md` and the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md` already exists, it carries the red-team-adversary's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open the plan's `TACTICS.md` and look at the latest reported logistics — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your strategic claims. Revise the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any empirical evidence that changes your analysis. Don't discard the previous assessment and start over, and don't leave a flagged vulnerability unaddressed.
- Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f11: tactics revision pass

You operate on the plan the mission-controller has assigned you to this round. Your field logistics and tactical plans live in that plan's `TACTICS.md`. You may be invoked on the same plan more than once — within each plan the pipeline runs you and the security-auditor as a loop, alternating with that plan's assessment loop, while the mission-controller round-robins across plans.

- If your assigned plan's `TACTICS.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the tactical operations from scratch based on `mission_brief.md` and the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `TACTICS.md` already exists, it carries the security-auditor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open the plan's `ASSESSMENT.md` and check whether the latest assessment introduces risk thresholds, constraints, or structural limits your current operations don't yet respect — for example a tighter exposure bound your routes currently fail, or an operational constraint your equipment violates. Revise the plan's `TACTICS.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest strategic parameters. Don't discard the previous logistics and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
- Do not read or touch other plans' files.
