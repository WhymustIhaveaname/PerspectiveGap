# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline for preclinical drug discovery that develops in-silico molecular docking models and empirical in-vitro synthesis and assay testing in parallel, with each side informing the other. A target disease profile and desired therapeutic mechanism arrive as a natural-language statement in `target_profile.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the computational model (binding affinities, pharmacophores, structural invariants) and revising the empirical results (synthesized compounds and in-vitro assay readouts that achieve those properties).

The five agents:

- **research-director** orchestrates the run by alternating one in-silico step with one in-vitro step and tracking progress. It does no modeling, synthesizing, or reviewing itself.
- **in-silico loop** — the **computational-biologist** writes the molecular docking models and computational analysis to `IN_SILICO.md`; the **pharmacokinetics-assessor** judges it. The computational-biologist reads `IN_VITRO.md` on each revision pass to incorporate empirical assay evidence.
- **in-vitro loop** — the **synthesis-chemist** documents the synthesis routes, executes the protocols, and records assay results to `IN_VITRO.md`; the **assay-validator** judges them. The synthesis-chemist reads `IN_SILICO.md` on each revision pass to align the chemical design with the latest theoretical structural claims.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `target_profile.md` — the original biological target and disease profile, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `IN_SILICO.md` — the computational docking analysis. Computational-biologist writes; pharmacokinetics-assessor reviews; synthesis-chemist and assay-validator read it to know which structural invariants and binding energies the physical compound must respect.
- `IN_VITRO.md` — the synthesis methodology and empirical assay results. Synthesis-chemist writes; assay-validator reviews; computational-biologist and pharmacokinetics-assessor read it to ground the docking simulations in observed bioassay numbers.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The research-director reads those scores to drive the loops.

Unlike a sequential design-then-test pipeline where the model must converge before synthesis starts, here neither side ever finishes first. Both loops alternate forever, capped at a fixed iteration count. In-silico models may tighten after seeing empirical binding numbers; in-vitro chemistry may correct after seeing the model's refined binding pockets.

# f1: target context

You are working on a preclinical drug discovery campaign, targeting a specific biological mechanism (e.g., kinase inhibition, GPCR antagonism, protein-protein interaction). The original biological target and desired therapeutic profile are given as a natural-language statement in `target_profile.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: computational-biologist's deliverable

Produce the in-silico molecular docking and computational analysis for the target and write it to `IN_SILICO.md`. A complete analysis covers four kinds of claims, each justified with structural modeling or literature citation.

- **Binding Affinity Predictions** — lower bounds on binding free energy (ΔG) from molecular dynamics or docking score relaxations; target IC50/Kd thresholds necessary for efficacy.
- **Structural Conformations** — pharmacophore features, spatial constraints, essential hydrogen bond interactions, and steric hindrance properties. State what must interact, where, and under which pocket conformations.
- **Physicochemical Invariants** — quantities every synthesized lead molecule must preserve (e.g., Lipinski's Rule of 5 parameters like Molecular Weight, LogP, specific polar surface area limits). The physical molecule should satisfy them; the assay-validator will check with a structural parser in `verify.py`.
- **ADMET Projections** — expected absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity profiles (e.g., predicted CYP inhibition, hERG liability, or off-target toxicity risks).

Ground your claims against `IN_VITRO.md` if it has reported assay numbers. A predicted sub-nanomolar affinity that is grossly off from an observed lack of empirical activity means either the computational model needs revision (e.g., ignored solvent effects) or the synthesized compound degraded — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. "The predicted ΔG is bounded below at -12 kcal/mol based on the deep hydrophobic pocket" is fine; "the molecule has some structure that might bind well" is not.

# f3: in-silico modeling toolbox

Match the computational tool to the biological target's structure; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in in-silico drug design.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Structure-Based Docking (e.g., Glide, AutoDock) | Targets with known high-resolution crystal structures; initial pose generation and scoring |
| Molecular Dynamics (MD) Simulations | Assessing target conformational flexibility, transient pockets, and complex stability |
| Free Energy Perturbation (FEP) | Precise relative binding affinity predictions between closely related lead analogs |
| Ligand-Based Pharmacophore Modeling | When target 3D structure is unknown; inferring requirements from known actives |
| Quantum Mechanics/Molecular Mechanics (QM/MM) | High-precision modeling of covalent inhibitors or enzymatic transition states |
| QSAR Statistical Modeling | Establishing robust correlations between chemical features and empirical bioactivity |
| ADMET Predictors (e.g., SwissADME) | Forecasting pharmacokinetic profiles and filtering out toxicophores early |
| Structural Clustering / Scaffold Hopping | Diversifying chemical space while preserving core required interactions |

# f4: performing the synthesis and assay

Read `target_profile.md` and `IN_SILICO.md`, then execute the synthesis and bioassays:

- Include every required reagent and solvent. Detail the retrosynthetic route, reaction conditions, and purification steps. Execute the synthesis, run the biochemical and evaluationular assays exactly as the target profile dictates, and record both the structural confirmation (e.g., NMR/MS results) and the empirical bioactivity (e.g., IC50, percent inhibition).
- Make the physical compound testable against `IN_SILICO.md`. If the computational model states a required binding affinity threshold, your synthesized compound should aim to achieve an IC50 at least that potent. If the model states a structural invariant (e.g., MW < 500), your synthesized molecule must satisfy it.
- Run the full synthesis and assay pipeline yourself and watch it through to completion — do not stop mid-reaction. If it yields an impure product or fails entirely, find and fix the root cause; do not bury poor yields or target-misses under vague language.
- Sanity-check the physical results against `IN_SILICO.md`. A target with strict molecular weight limits or discrete chiral centers should not come back violating those rules; an affinity the model insists is highly potent should reflect in the empirical readouts.

Write the synthesis methodology and empirical assay results up in `IN_VITRO.md`.

# f5: in-vitro doc discipline

Keep `IN_VITRO.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page summary; record only the final optimized route and assay readouts, and move iteration-by-iteration failed reaction history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: in-silico review

Review `IN_SILICO.md` against `target_profile.md` and against the bioactivity numbers reported in `IN_VITRO.md` (if any).

- **Target Alignment** — does the in-silico model correctly address the biological mechanism and disease profile stated in `target_profile.md`? Check target pocket definitions, receptor types, and therapeutic mechanisms.
- **Modeling Soundness** — for each claim, walk through the docking or simulation rationale. Flag oversimplified docking parameters or ignored solvent effects. Verify ADMET assumptions apply to the proposed delivery route.
- **Empirical Grounding** — open `IN_VITRO.md`. If the physical assays report IC50 or binding values, do they fall within the model's predicted affinity ranges? A predicted highly potent binding energy that empirically yields >10µM is a signal the scoring function is fundamentally misaligned; refine the model, or note explicitly that the docking score is currently unreliable.

Be brief and specific. Do not write the computational models yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: in-vitro assay review

Review `IN_VITRO.md` against `target_profile.md` and against `IN_SILICO.md`.

- Inspect the reported assay readouts for anomalies, unrealistic reaction yields, or cytotoxicity limits violated as stated directly in `target_profile.md`.
- Open `IN_SILICO.md`. For every physicochemical invariant and structural property the model claims, write a check into a separate `verify.py` (e.g., using RDKit to parse the SMILES string): does the synthesized compound's structure satisfy the invariant, fall within the predicted ADMET parameters, and achieve the predicted potency? Run it. Name any property or functional group that violates.
- Read the synthesis and assay protocols and spot-check them for methodological flaws — cross-reactivity risks, impure intermediates, uncalibrated assay baselines. An assay artifact that creates a false positive is worse than a failed synthesis.
- Treat the result as finished only when every disease target constraint holds, every in-silico invariant holds, and the bioassay confirms the model's predicted affinity — not merely when a chemical is successfully synthesized.
- Check the length of `IN_VITRO.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `IN_VITRO.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this compound precipitated in the assay buffer and didn't work, but I'm telling you it didn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never model, synthesize, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0` and `last_side: none`. Dispatch the computational-biologist for the first in-silico step (computational-biologist, then pharmacokinetics-assessor), then the synthesis-chemist for the first in-vitro step (synthesis-chemist, then assay-validator).
- **Alternation loop** — alternate one in-silico step with one in-vitro step. Neither side terminates early on its own score; even a perfect-looking molecular docking model may need to revise once the in-vitro assay reports actual empirical numbers.
- Cap the total at 20 iterations (10 in-silico steps + 10 in-vitro steps). After the cap, stop and summarize.
- Keep the current iteration count, last side, and the two latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the reviewer's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + agent name + content) and continue with the next step.

# f10: in-silico revision pass

The molecular docking models and computational analysis live in `IN_SILICO.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the pharmacokinetics-assessor as a loop, alternating with the in-vitro loop.

- If `IN_SILICO.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the computational analysis from scratch based on `target_profile.md`.
- If `IN_SILICO.md` already exists, it carries the pharmacokinetics-assessor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `IN_VITRO.md` and look at the latest reported assay numbers — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your structural modeling. Revise `IN_SILICO.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any empirical evidence that changes your analysis. Don't discard the previous computational model and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: in-vitro revision pass

The synthesis routes and empirical assay results live in `IN_VITRO.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the assay-validator as a loop, alternating with the in-silico loop.

- If `IN_VITRO.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: design the synthesis and run the initial assays from scratch based on `target_profile.md`.
- If `IN_VITRO.md` already exists, it carries the assay-validator's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open `IN_SILICO.md` and check whether the latest model introduces required binding energies, pharmacophores, or physicochemical invariants your current synthesized compound doesn't yet respect — for example a tighter molecular weight limit your lead is now far from achieving, or an off-target interaction your molecule violates. Revise `IN_VITRO.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest computational model. Don't discard the previous synthesis logs entirely to start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f12: report back

When you finish, briefly report to the research-director: what you did, what difficulties you hit (or didn't resolve), and any open questions.
