# background

I need you to set up a 6-agent pipeline that engineers a macroeconomic intervention to stabilize a collapsing national currency and drafts the specific legislative mandates and banking regulations to enforce it. A crisis brief arrives as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted plan and then working, verified legislative text and regulations.

The six agents:

- **policy-coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the plan phase, then the drafting phase, and tracks progress. It does no economic modeling, drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **macro-theorist** is invoked once at init and produces K differentiated candidate intervention plans, one per `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. It never enters the per-plan revision loop.
- **intervention-tuner** is invoked once per plan-refinement step in the loop. It revises a single assigned plan's `PLAN.md` based on the economic-skeptic's last review for that plan. It never creates new plans.
- **economic-skeptic** reviews the plan it is currently assigned to.
- **legislation-drafter** implements the strategy as legislative mandates and banking regulations for the plan it is currently assigned to.
- **compliance-stress-tester** reviews the drafted legislation and regulations of the plan it is currently assigned to.

The pipeline hands work off through plan-namespaced files:

- `problem.md` — the original crisis statement, given as input. All five domain agents (macro-theorist, intervention-tuner, economic-skeptic, legislation-drafter, compliance-stress-tester) read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md` — one plan per directory, K plans total. The macro-theorist writes K differentiated candidates at init; the intervention-tuner revises a specific assigned plan in the loop; the economic-skeptic reviews the plan it is assigned to; the legislation-drafter reads the plan it is assigned to.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/SOLUTION.md` — the drafted legislation and regulations for that plan. The legislation-drafter writes it; the compliance-stress-tester reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the policy-coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

The policy-coordinator round-robins across the K plans, first in a plan phase (each plan iterates its intervention-tuner ↔ economic-skeptic loop) and then in a drafting phase (each plan iterates its legislation-drafter ↔ compliance-stress-tester loop). Cross-plan reading does not happen: an agent assigned to one plan never reads another plan's files.

# f1: problem context

You are working on a macroeconomic crisis where the national currency is collapsing — it may involve hyperinflation, capital flight, debt default, or a combination. The original crisis overview is given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: macro-theorist's deliverable

Produce K differentiated candidate plans, each in its own `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. The K plans should differ meaningfully in their macroeconomic strategy so distinct routes can be compared downstream, not in trivial ways like naming conventions.

A complete plan in any `PLAN.md` has two parts — a formalized economic model and one chosen intervention strategy.

The **model** must state:

- **Crisis type** — hyperinflation, balance of payments crisis, sovereign debt crisis, etc.; this must be exact.
- **Data** — the key economic indicators (inflation rate, foreign reserves, GDP, money supply) and what each represents.
- **Variables** — the policy levers and market responses (interest rates, exchange rate pegs, capital controls), with descriptive names.
- **Primary objective** — the exact economic metric to stabilize or optimize.
- **Constraints** — every political, legal, or fiscal constraint, listed explicitly.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which policy framework to use and how to deploy it;
- the timeline and key initial policy settings;
- the underlying economic mechanism or structural reform the route relies on;
- the biggest risk or potential negative externality on this route.

# f3: intervention toolbox

Which macroeconomic policy framework suits which kind of crisis — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Currency Board / Peg** — locks the exchange rate to a stable foreign currency; stops inflation quickly but requires massive foreign reserves.
- **Capital Controls** — limits the flow of foreign capital in and out of the country; stems capital flight but discourages future investment.
- **Austerity / Fiscal Consolidation** — severe cuts in government spending and tax hikes; reduces deficit and debt but risks deep recession.
- **Interest Rate Shock (Volcker Shock)** — drastic increases in the central bank's policy rate; crushes inflation expectations but can bankrupt domestic borrowers.
- **Debt Restructuring** — negotiating haircuts or maturity extensions with creditors; necessary for unsustainable sovereign debt but triggers temporary default.
- **Dual Exchange Rate System** — separate rates for essential imports versus financial transactions; preserves reserves for essentials but creates a black market and distortions.

Match the policy tool to the root cause of the currency collapse; do not force an ill-suited one.

# f4: drafting the legislation

Read the plan in your assigned plan's `PLAN.md` and implement its strategy as legislative mandates and banking regulations:

- Include every required statute. Draft the text with the correct legal definitions and jurisdictional bounds, define the primary objective and every constraint exactly as the model states, operationalize the mandates, verify the enforcement mechanism, and clearly specify both the regulatory actions and the intended economic outcomes.
- Simulate the enforcement of the text yourself and trace it through to its economic outcome — do not stop at mere enactment. If it creates legal contradictions, find and fix the root cause; do not bury errors under generic override clauses.
- Sanity-check the mandates: a regulation meant to cap capital withdrawals must not accidentally permit unlimited corporate transfers.

Write the legislation and its expected outcomes up in your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md`. Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f5: legislation doc discipline

Keep your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page legal brief; record only the final drafted text and move iteration-by-iteration legislative history into the plan's `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: plan review

Review the plan in your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned plan; do not look at other plans.

- **Model** — does it faithfully capture the economic crisis? Check that the crisis type, variables, primary objective, and constraints are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen policy framework a sound fit, is the intervention actually implementable, and is it likely to stabilize the currency?

Be brief and specific. Do not draft any legislation yourself — only review.

# f7: compliance review

Review your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` — the legislative text and its regulatory enforcement. Verify it directly against the crisis statement. Operate strictly on the assigned plan; do not look at other plans.

- Inspect the reported draft for legal anomalies, unreasonable administrative burdens, or constraint violations.
- Write a separate `verify.py` under the plan's directory that programmatically checks every quantitative or logical constraint against the drafted regulatory thresholds, and run it; name any clauses or parameters that violate a constraint.
- Read the legislative text and spot-check it for bugs — unused regulatory powers, dead statutes, or enforcement mechanisms that contradict what the bill claims to do. A loophole that silently undermines the policy is worse than a procedural clash.
- Treat the draft as finished only when every legal constraint holds and the policy objective is soundly met — not merely when the text sounds official.
- Check the length of the plan's `SOLUTION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the plan's `SOLUTION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this framework doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never model, draft, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the macro-theorist once to draft K differentiated candidate plans, each in its own `plans/{id}-{slug}/` directory. After it returns, read the directory list to learn the K plan ids. The macro-theorist is not invoked again after this.
- **Plan phase** — round-robin across the K plans. For each plan in turn, dispatch intervention-tuner (revise that plan based on its economic-skeptic's review) then economic-skeptic (score that plan). After one round-robin pass through all K plans, start the next pass. A plan is considered done in this phase when its economic-skeptic score reaches 10; stop scheduling more plan-phase calls for it but continue with the others. After all K plans hit economic-skeptic 10, or each plan has had 10 plan-phase iterations, move to the drafting phase.
- **Drafting phase** — round-robin across the K plans. For each plan in turn, dispatch legislation-drafter (revise that plan's drafted legislation based on its compliance-stress-tester's review) then compliance-stress-tester (score that plan's legislation). The pipeline stops when any plan reaches compliance-stress-tester score 10, or each plan has had 10 drafting-phase iterations.
- When dispatching, always pass the plan's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `PLAN.md` / `SOLUTION.md` to operate on.
- Keep the current phase, per-plan iteration counts, per-plan latest scores, and current plan index in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant plan's critic `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: plan revision pass

Read your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`. It carries the economic-skeptic's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the plan's `PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Preserve the two-part structure (formalized economic model plus chosen intervention strategy). Don't discard the plan and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. You revise; you do not create new plans. Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f11: legislative revision pass

Your legislative mandates and regulations live in your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md`.

- If your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the drafted text from scratch based on the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `SOLUTION.md` already exists, it carries the compliance-stress-tester's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the plan's `SOLUTION.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the draft and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. Do not read or touch other plans' files.
