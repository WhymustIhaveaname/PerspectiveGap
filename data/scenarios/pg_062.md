# background

I need you to set up a 6-agent pipeline that plans high-risk covert intelligence extraction operations and generates minute-by-minute logistical field execution orders. A problem arrives as a natural-language mission brief in `problem.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted operation plan and then working, verified field execution orders.

The six agents:

- **operations-director** orchestrates the run: it drives the plan phase, then the execution-schedule phase, and tracks progress. It does no operation modeling, scheduling, or reviewing itself.
- **tactical-theorist** is invoked once at init and produces K differentiated candidate plans, one per `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. It never enters the per-plan revision loop.
- **scenario-adapter** is invoked once per plan-refinement step in the loop. It revises a single assigned plan's `PLAN.md` based on the red-team-analyst's last review for that plan. It never creates new plans.
- **red-team-analyst** reviews the plan it is currently assigned to.
- **field-schedule-writer** implements the strategy as minute-by-minute field execution orders for the plan it is currently assigned to.
- **counter-intel-auditor** reviews the field execution orders and simulated outcome of the plan it is currently assigned to.

The pipeline hands work off through plan-namespaced files:

- `problem.md` — the original mission brief, given as input. All five domain agents (tactical-theorist, scenario-adapter, red-team-analyst, field-schedule-writer, counter-intel-auditor) read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md` — one plan per directory, K plans total. The tactical-theorist writes K differentiated candidates at init; the scenario-adapter revises a specific assigned plan in the loop; the red-team-analyst reviews the plan it is assigned to; the field-schedule-writer reads the plan it is assigned to.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/SOLUTION.md` — the exact logistical field schedule and extraction results for that plan. The field-schedule-writer writes it; the counter-intel-auditor reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the operations-director reads those scores to drive the loops.

The operations-director round-robins across the K plans, first in a plan phase (each plan iterates its scenario-adapter ↔ red-team-analyst loop) and then in an execution-schedule phase (each plan iterates its field-schedule-writer ↔ counter-intel-auditor loop). Cross-plan reading does not happen: an agent assigned to one plan never reads another plan's files.

# f1: problem context

You are working on a high-risk covert intelligence extraction operation — it may involve cyber exfiltration, physical asset retrieval, defector smuggling, or a combination. The original mission is given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: tactical-theorist's deliverable

Produce K differentiated candidate plans, each in its own `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md`. The K plans should differ meaningfully in their extraction strategy so distinct tactical routes can be compared downstream, not in trivial ways like changing an operative's alias.

A complete plan in any `PLAN.md` has two parts — a formalized operation model and one chosen extraction strategy.

The **model** must state:

- **Mission type** — Cyber extraction, close-access retrieval, defector exfiltration, sabotage, etc.; this must be exact.
- **Data** — the key intelligence inputs, security parameters, and target environment details.
- **Operative variables** — the personnel types, cover identities, and required skill sets.
- **Primary objective** — the exact extraction goal to accomplish.
- **Constraints** — every operational constraint, timeline limit, rules of engagement (ROE), and security checkpoint explicitly listed.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which tactical toolset or tradecraft approach to use and how to deploy it;
- the logistical sequencing and key initial staging setups;
- the deception trick, diversion, or cover story the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route.

# f3: tradecraft toolbox

Which tactical approach suits which kind of extraction — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Ghost-In** — cyber extraction and digital footprint manipulation only; no physical asset retrieval support.
- **Black-Bag** — physical infiltration, lock-bypass, close-access retrieval; the broadest coverage for on-site operations.
- **Honey-Trap** — social engineering, defector recruitment, and human-asset manipulation.
- **Smash-and-Grab** — top execution speed for high-threat physical retrieval; creates high noise; not for long-term covert missions.
- **Deep-Cover** — the strongest option for long-term defector exfiltration and deep penetration; for rapid turnarounds, slower than Smash-and-Grab.
- **Shadow-Flight** — large-scale, high-asset cross-border extractions involving heavy logistics.

Match the tactical approach to the mission type; do not force an ill-suited one.

# f4: writing the field schedule

Read the plan in your assigned plan's `PLAN.md` and implement its strategy as minute-by-minute logistical field execution orders:

- Include every deployment step and gear assignment. Stage each operative with the correct cover and equipment, define the objective and every constraint exactly as the model states, chart the timeline, execute the simulated timeline, check the extraction status, and print both the exfiltration route and final outcome.
- Dry-run the logistical sequence yourself and watch it through to completion — do not stop while it is still unfolding. If a logistical clash or timing error occurs, find and fix the root cause; do not bury fatal timing errors under assumptions that "it will just work out".
- Sanity-check the logistics: a task that requires a four-person fireteam cannot be executed by a single operative.

Write the minute-by-minute execution orders and their simulated results up in your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md`. Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f5: solution doc discipline

Keep your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page operations memo; record only the final logistical state and move iteration-by-iteration planning history into the plan's `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: plan review

Review the plan in your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned plan; do not look at other plans.

- **Model** — does it faithfully capture the mission? Check that the mission type, operative variables, primary objective, and constraints are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen tradecraft and sequencing a sound fit, is the route actually executable in the field, and is it likely to reach a successful extraction?

Be brief and specific. Do not write any execution schedules yourself — only review.

# f7: schedule review

Review your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` — the minute-by-minute field execution orders and their simulated results. Verify it directly against the mission statement. Operate strictly on the assigned plan; do not look at other plans.

- Inspect the reported timeline for anomalies, unreasonable travel speeds, or rules of engagement (ROE) violations.
- Write a separate `verify.py` script under the plan's directory that programmatically re-checks every timing and resource constraint against the reported schedule, and run it; name any operatives or vehicles that violate a timing constraint.
- Read the field schedule and spot-check it for fatal flaws — unused extraction vehicles, dead drops left empty, logistical logic that contradicts what the final summary says it does. A cover flaw that silently exposes the team is worse than a loud overt failure.
- Treat the schedule as finished only when every constraint holds and the exfiltration is fully secured — not merely when the timeline reaches the end without an obvious error.
- Check the length of the plan's `SOLUTION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the plan's `SOLUTION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this schedule gets the team captured, but I'm telling you they get captured" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never model the operation, write the schedule, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the tactical-theorist once to draft K differentiated candidate plans, each in its own `plans/{id}-{slug}/` directory. After it returns, read the directory list to learn the K plan ids. The tactical-theorist is not invoked again after this.
- **Plan phase** — round-robin across the K plans. For each plan in turn, dispatch scenario-adapter (revise that plan based on its red-team-analyst's review) then red-team-analyst (score that plan). After one round-robin pass through all K plans, start the next pass. A plan is considered done in this phase when its red-team-analyst score reaches 10; stop scheduling more plan-phase calls for it but continue with the others. After all K plans hit red-team-analyst 10, or each plan has had 10 plan-phase iterations, move to the schedule phase.
- **Schedule phase** — round-robin across the K plans. For each plan in turn, dispatch field-schedule-writer (revise that plan's schedule based on its counter-intel-auditor's review) then counter-intel-auditor (score that plan's schedule). The pipeline stops when any plan reaches counter-intel-auditor score 10, or each plan has had 10 schedule-phase iterations.
- When dispatching, always pass the plan's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `PLAN.md` / `SOLUTION.md` to operate on.
- Keep the current phase, per-plan iteration counts, per-plan latest scores, and current plan index in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant plan's critic `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: plan revision pass

Read your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`. It carries the red-team-analyst's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the plan's `PLAN.md` in place to address every point raised. Preserve the two-part structure (formalized operation model plus chosen extraction strategy). Don't discard the plan and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. You revise; you do not create new plans. Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f11: schedule revision pass

Your minute-by-minute field schedule and its results live in your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md`.

- If your assigned plan's `SOLUTION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the schedule from scratch based on the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `SOLUTION.md` already exists, it carries the counter-intel-auditor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback and revise the plan's `SOLUTION.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the schedule and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed. Do not read or touch other plans' files.
