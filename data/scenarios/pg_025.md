# background

I need you to set up a 2-agent pipeline for a pharmacovigilance signal-detection challenge. The two roles are signal analyst and medical safety reviewer: signal analyst is responsible for finding and documenting plausible drug safety signals from public evidence, medical safety reviewer is responsible for judging whether the signal analyst's claim is valid and sufficiently supported. The flow is roughly:

```
WHILE TRUE:
    1. signal analyst picks a signal hypothesis it likes and explores it deeply, or appends more hypotheses to ideas.xml and then picks one to explore.
    2. signal analyst spawns a **fresh** medical safety reviewer to evaluate it.
```

# f1: problem definition

You are participating in a pharmacovigilance signal-detection challenge where public clinical and adverse-event evidence may reveal a serious drug safety issue.

Read the challenge rules first: ./contest-rules.md

# f2: signal investigation tactics

Most marketed drugs already have extensive labeling, trial data, and post-marketing surveillance history. Obvious known adverse reactions are rarely valuable as new signals. So:

- Don't report issues that are plainly already established in the current prescribing information, risk management materials, or major regulatory safety communications unless you can show a materially new population, severity, mechanism, or risk magnitude
- Truly valuable safety signals usually hide in non-obvious patterns across case narratives, trial exclusions, subgroup effects, time-to-onset, drug-drug interactions, dechallenge/rechallenge evidence, medication errors, or inconsistencies between public data sources

**Writing the report**

Each signal hypothesis corresponds to its own report file at `reports/{id}-{slug}.md`, where `{id}` and `{slug}` come from the hypothesis's id and slug attributes in ideas.xml.

The report must contain four h2 sections in this fixed order:

- `## Signal Title` — concise, clear title naming the drug, event, and at-risk context
- `## Public Health Impact` — what harm it can cause, including seriousness, exposed population, preventability, and clinical consequences
- `## Short Description` — short writeup of the suspected safety issue, with references to specific public evidence sources such as labels, trial registrations/results, FDA/EMA documents, FAERS summaries, case reports, or peer-reviewed analyses
- `## Reproduction Details` — a reproducible method the medical safety reviewer can follow as-is to verify the evidence pattern, including data sources, search terms, filters, dates accessed, and any analysis script or spreadsheet logic

Do not modify or delete the `## Review` section — the medical safety reviewer overwrites that themselves.

If after investigation you find the signal hypothesis doesn't hold, still briefly record your analysis and the reason for rejection in the report file.

# f3: evaluation criteria

Check the following dimensions one by one:

| Dimension | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Scope | Within public clinical, regulatory, biomedical literature, labeling, or adverse-event evidence for a drug safety question; the claim must concern a serious or clinically meaningful adverse outcome |
| Evidence references | Cited labels, trial records, regulatory documents, publications, database fields, case counts, dates, and quoted passages must exactly match the public sources used |
| Public health impact | Impact claim consistent with the report's own analysis, no self-contradiction, no exaggeration beyond severity, frequency, or exposed-population evidence |
| Causal rationale | Pinpoint the specific evidence pattern and explain why it suggests a plausible drug-event association rather than only background disease, confounding, indication bias, or reporting artifact |
| Reproducible analysis | End-to-end search or analysis workflow showing how the signal was identified from the public data, from the analyst's perspective, without altering or selectively fabricating source data |
| Reproducibility | Workflow runs end-to-end without manual assembly by the medical safety reviewer; database queries, filters, denominators, time windows, and any calculations must be specified clearly enough to reproduce |
| Deduplication | Search current prescribing information, FDA/EMA safety communications, ClinicalTrials.gov, PubMed, major pharmacovigilance publications, and public regulatory reviews to confirm the issue is not already established or officially resolved |
| Incremental signal value | The claimed safety issue must add meaningful information beyond the baseline fact that spontaneous adverse-event reports are confounded and underreported |

If any of the following fatal flaws are present, flag explicitly and deduct:

- References to nonexistent documents, fabricated case counts, or AI-hallucinated quotations
- Public health impact contradicts the report's own analysis
- Reproducible analysis depends on altered, cherry-picked, or manually invented source data
- Evidence references inconsistent with the cited public sources or access dates
- Workflow is incomplete and requires the medical safety reviewer to manually reconstruct queries, filters, or calculations
- Signal is merely a restatement of a known labeled risk, background disease risk, or generic spontaneous-reporting limitation, with no incremental safety insight

# f4: dispatch rules

After documenting a safety signal, submit it to a **fresh** medical safety reviewer for review. Do not review your own report.

**After the review returns:** execute all action steps in the medical safety reviewer's output. Then immediately go back to step 1: re-read ideas.xml and pick the next hypothesis by UCB to keep working. Don't wait for user instructions, don't stop. One round = write report → review → execute actions → back to step 1.

# f6: hypothesis selection

Before starting work, read ideas.xml.

1. Each hypothesis's notes contain the medical safety reviewer's **quality score (0-10)**. A score of 0 means the hypothesis is dead — don't touch it again. `none` means it hasn't been attempted yet.
2. Prefer hypotheses with `attempts="0"` and `score="none"` first.
3. Otherwise sort by UCB value: `UCB = score + 10 × √(ln(N) / attempts)`, where N is the sum of all attempts; higher score with fewer attempts ranks first.
4. If no hypothesis is worth trying, search public evidence sources for new hypotheses, append them to ideas.xml, then pick one to start.

**Recording rule during exploration**

While exploring labels, trial records, regulatory reviews, adverse-event data, case reports, or literature, the moment you spot any potential new safety signal — even if you're unsure it holds — immediately append it to ideas.xml (`score="none"`, `attempts=0`). Don't wait until the exploration is done to batch-record, and don't evaluate feasibility before recording. Recording ≠ committing to attempt it, just preventing forgetting. Evaluation belongs to the selection phase.
