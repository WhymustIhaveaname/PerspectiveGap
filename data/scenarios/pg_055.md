# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline for civil appellate litigation support. A matter arrives as a trial record excerpt and issue statement in `record.md`, and the pipeline turns it into a vetted appellate theory and then a polished, record-supported appellate brief.

The five agents:

- **docket coordinator** orchestrates the run: it drives the theory loop, then the brief loop, and tracks progress. It does no legal analysis, drafting, or reviewing itself.
- **theory loop** — the **appellate strategist** turns the record and issue statement into a coherent appellate theory and briefing strategy; the **red-team appellate counsel** challenges it. They iterate until the theory is sound.
- **brief loop** — the **brief drafter** writes the brief from the vetted theory; the **opposing-counsel brief reviewer** reviews the brief as a hostile reader would. They iterate until the brief is accurate, persuasive, and court-ready.

The pipeline hands work off through three files:

- `record.md` — the trial record excerpt, procedural posture, and issue statement, given as input. All four legal-domain agents read it.
- `THEORY.md` — the appellate theory. The appellate strategist writes it; the red-team appellate counsel reviews it; the brief drafter reads it to draft the brief.
- `BRIEF.md` — the polished appellate brief. The brief drafter writes it; the opposing-counsel brief reviewer reviews it.

Each critic records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, and the docket coordinator reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: appeal context

You are working on a civil appeal. The trial record excerpt, procedural posture, judgment or order being appealed, and issue statement are provided in `record.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: appellate strategist's deliverable

Produce the appellate theory and briefing strategy for the matter and write it to `THEORY.md`. A complete theory has two parts — a formalized appellate analysis and one chosen briefing strategy.

The **analysis** must state:

- **Appeal posture** — final judgment or appealable order, court, parties, and the relief sought on appeal.
- **Issues presented** — the exact questions the appellate court should decide, framed in a way that fits the record.
- **Standard of review** — the governing standard for each issue, with any preservation or harmless-error implications.
- **Material record facts** — the key facts, rulings, objections, motions, and record citations that matter to the appeal.
- **Governing law** — the controlling statutes, rules, and precedent needed for the argument.
- **Theory of error** — precisely what the trial court or opposing party did wrong, why it matters, and how it affected the judgment.
- **Remedy** — reversal, vacatur, remand instructions, modification, new trial, or other specific relief.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising route — must state:

- which issue should lead and which arguments should be omitted or subordinated;
- how the standard of review will be handled;
- the core precedent and record facts the argument will rely on;
- the anticipated appellee response and the best answer to it;
- the biggest risk in this appellate route.

# f3: appellate theory toolbox

Which appellate route suits which kind of issue — use it to choose the strategy:

- **Pure legal error** — best when the issue is preserved and turns on statutory interpretation, contract interpretation, rule construction, or another question reviewed de novo.
- **Summary judgment or dismissal error** — strong when the record can be framed in the nonmovant's favor and the trial court applied the wrong legal rule or ignored a genuine dispute.
- **Evidentiary or trial-management error** — viable when the ruling was preserved, the standard is abuse of discretion, and the excluded or admitted evidence probably affected the outcome.
- **Jury-instruction error** — useful when the requested instruction correctly stated the law, was supported by the evidence, was refused or misstated, and prejudice can be shown.
- **Sufficiency or weight-of-the-evidence challenge** — difficult on appeal; use only when the record is unusually one-sided or the governing burden of proof creates a clear legal defect.
- **Procedural due process or notice defect** — strong when the record shows lack of notice, denial of a meaningful opportunity to be heard, or a ruling outside the issues actually litigated.
- **Preservation-limited fallback** — use only when the best issue was not cleanly preserved; address forfeiture, plain-error or fundamental-error doctrine if available, and harmless-error risk.

Match the appellate route to the record, the issue, and the standard of review; do not force an argument that the appellate court is unlikely to reach or accept.

# f4: writing the brief

Read the theory in `THEORY.md` and draft the appellate brief from it:

- Include all core sections needed for a polished civil appellate brief: caption placeholder if needed, table-ready headings, issue presented, statement of the case, statement of facts, summary of argument, argument, and conclusion.
- Use the chosen theory exactly: frame the issue, state the standard of review, marshal the record facts, cite controlling authority, answer the likely appellee arguments, and request the specified relief.
- Support every material factual assertion with a record citation from `record.md`. Do not invent facts, procedural events, quotations, objections, rulings, or citations.
- Check the brief yourself from start to finish. Fix missing record cites, unsupported legal propositions, internal contradictions, and placeholders. Do not leave TODOs, bracketed guesses, or uncited assertions for someone else.
- Sanity-check the requested relief: it must match the appellate posture and the error theory.

Write the brief in `BRIEF.md`.

# f5: brief doc discipline

Keep `BRIEF.md` lean and court-ready. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 6000 words (~40KB), roughly the length of a focused principal brief or substantial motion brief; move drafting history, abandoned arguments, and research notes into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: theory review

Review the theory in `THEORY.md`:

- **Appellate analysis** — does it faithfully capture the record and issue statement? Check that the appeal posture, issues presented, standards of review, material facts, governing law, error theory, preservation, prejudice, and remedy are accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen appellate route a sound fit, is it actually briefable from this record, and is it likely to give the appellant the best chance of relief?

Be brief and specific. Do not draft the brief yourself — only review the theory.

# f7: brief review

Review `BRIEF.md` — the appellate brief and its supporting citations. Verify it directly against the trial record and issue statement.

- Inspect the argument for anomalies, overstatements, waived issues, unsupported standards of review, unreasonable remedies, or factual assertions that the record does not support.
- Write a separate `cite_check.md` that re-checks every material factual assertion and record citation against `record.md`; name any assertion that lacks support or misstates the cited material.
- Read the brief closely and spot-check it for legal and drafting defects — missing authorities, dead arguments, contradictory positions, abandoned preservation problems, or language that promises more than the record can deliver. A subtle misstatement that invites reversal or sanctions is worse than an obvious typo.
- Treat the brief as finished only when the record support is accurate, the legal theory is coherent, and the requested relief is sound — not merely when the document reads smoothly.
- Check the length of `BRIEF.md`: if it exceeds 40KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `BRIEF.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never analyze the appeal, draft the brief, or review the work yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the appellate strategist to draft the theory.
- **Theory loop** — have the appellate strategist and red-team appellate counsel iterate on the appellate theory and strategy until the red-team appellate counsel scores the theory 10.
- **Brief loop** — once the theory is settled, have the brief drafter and opposing-counsel brief reviewer iterate on the brief until the opposing-counsel brief reviewer scores it 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the critic's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: theory revision pass

The appellate theory lives in `THEORY.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the pipeline runs you and the red-team appellate counsel as a loop.

- If `THEORY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the theory from scratch.
- If `THEORY.md` already exists, it carries the red-team appellate counsel's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `THEORY.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the theory and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
