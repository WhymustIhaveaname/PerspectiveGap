# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent investigative journalism publication workflow. A source packet arrives as a collection of tips, documents, interview notes, data extracts, and background material in `source_packet.md`, and the workflow turns it into a vetted reporting theory and then a publishable, fact-checked story.

The five agents:

- **assigning editor** orchestrates the run: it drives the reporting-theory loop, then the story loop, and tracks progress. It does no reporting, writing, or reviewing itself.
- **reporting-theory loop** — the **investigative reporter** turns the source packet into a clear reporting theory and verification strategy; the **skeptical investigations editor** reviews it. They iterate until the theory is well supported and reportable.
- **story loop** — the **story writer** drafts the story from the vetted theory; the **fact-checking editor** reviews the draft, source support, and attribution. They iterate until the story is accurate and publishable.

The workflow hands work off through three files:

- `source_packet.md` — the original source material, given as input. All four domain agents (investigative reporter, skeptical investigations editor, story writer, fact-checking editor) read it.
- `THEORY.md` — the vetted reporting theory. The investigative reporter writes it; the skeptical investigations editor reviews it; the story writer reads it to draft the story.
- `STORY.md` — the publishable story draft, source notes, and fact-check status. The story writer writes it; the fact-checking editor reviews it.

Each editor records the score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file they reviewed, and the assigning editor reads those scores to drive the loops.

# f1: source packet context

You are working on an investigative journalism assignment. The original material is given in `source_packet.md`; it may include confidential tips, public records, leaked documents, interview notes, timelines, datasets, prior coverage, and statements from affected parties. Read it before starting your part.

# f2: investigative reporter's deliverable

Produce the reporting theory for the assignment and write it to `THEORY.md`. A complete theory has two parts — a structured evidence theory and one chosen verification strategy.

The **evidence theory** must state:

- **Story type** — accountability investigation, public-records exposé, data-driven investigation, human-impact feature with investigative findings, enterprise follow-up, and so on; this must be exact.
- **Known facts** — the key confirmed items in the source packet and what each one establishes.
- **Core claims** — the specific factual assertions the story would need to make, with descriptive names.
- **Publication thesis** — the exact claim or finding the story would advance if verified.
- **Evidence gaps and constraints** — every missing source, weak link, legal sensitivity, privacy issue, or attribution limitation, listed explicitly.

The **strategy** — weigh the options and commit to the single most promising reporting route — must state:

- which reporting method to prioritize and how to execute it;
- the verification sequence and its key initial targets;
- the sourcing or corroboration move the route relies on;
- the biggest risk on this route.

# f3: reporting toolbox

Which reporting route suits which kind of source packet — use it to choose one for the strategy:

- **Public-records buildout** — strongest when the key facts can be verified through filings, budgets, contracts, court records, inspection reports, emails obtained by records request, or meeting minutes; slower when agencies may delay.
- **Document authentication and source corroboration** — best for leaked memos, internal spreadsheets, screenshots, or whistleblower packets; requires provenance checks and at least one independent corroborating source.
- **Data analysis** — best when the source packet includes structured records large enough to reveal patterns; requires cleaning, methodology notes, and reproducibility.
- **Interview-driven reconstruction** — best for misconduct timelines, institutional failures, or events witnessed by many people; needs multiple independent accounts and careful handling of memory conflicts.
- **Right-of-reply and institutional response** — essential when the story names people or organizations accused of wrongdoing; not sufficient by itself to prove the thesis.
- **Legal and ethics review track** — required for high-risk allegations, confidential sources, minors, medical information, sealed records, or potential defamation exposure; it can reshape what can be published.

Match the route to the evidence problem; do not force a method that cannot support the claims.

# f4: writing the story

Read the theory in `THEORY.md` and implement its strategy as a publishable story draft:

- Include every necessary attribution. State each factual claim with the correct source support, quote accurately, identify anonymous or confidential sourcing limits, preserve chronology, and avoid implying more than the evidence proves.
- Complete the draft and verification work yourself and follow it through to the end — do not stop while key claims are still unchecked. If a claim lacks support, fix the root cause by obtaining support, narrowing the claim, attributing it properly, or removing it; do not hide uncertainty in vague language.
- Sanity-check the story: a claim that depends on a document, dataset, interview, or public record should be traceable to that support, and a number should not appear unless its calculation and units are clear.

Write the story and its verification notes up in `STORY.md`.

# f5: story document discipline

Keep `STORY.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page note; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration reporting history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: reporting theory review

Review the theory in `THEORY.md`:

- **Evidence theory** — does it faithfully capture the source packet? Check that the story type, known facts, core claims, publication thesis, and evidence gaps are all accurate.
- **Strategy** — is the chosen reporting method and verification sequence a sound fit, is the route actually executable, and is it likely to produce a publishable, well-supported story?

Be brief and specific. Do not draft the story yourself — only review.

# f7: story fact-check review

Review `STORY.md` — the story draft and its verification notes. Verify it directly against the source packet.

- Inspect the reported findings for anomalies, overstated conclusions, missing context, unreasonable numbers, or claims that go beyond the available evidence.
- Write a separate `VERIFY.md` that re-checks every factual assertion against the cited source support, and complete it; name any claims that lack support, have conflicting support, or need narrower wording.
- Read the story draft and spot-check it for bugs — unused quotes, unattributed facts, dead-end claims, chronology errors, or language that contradicts what the verification notes say. A subtle unsupported implication is worse than an obvious gap.
- Treat the story as finished only when every material claim is supported and the publication thesis is sound — not merely when the draft reads smoothly.
- Check the length of `STORY.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of `STORY.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this isn't supported, but I'm saying it isn't supported" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never report, write, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — dispatch the investigative reporter to draft the theory.
- **Reporting-theory loop** — have the investigative reporter and skeptical investigations editor iterate on the evidence theory and verification strategy until the skeptical investigations editor scores the theory 10.
- **Story loop** — once the theory is settled, have the story writer and fact-checking editor iterate on the draft until the fact-checking editor scores it 10.
- Cap each loop at 10 iterations.
- Keep the current phase, iteration count, and latest score in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the editor's `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.

# f10: reporting theory revision pass

The reporting theory lives in `THEORY.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the workflow runs you and the skeptical investigations editor as a loop.

- If `THEORY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the theory from scratch.
- If `THEORY.md` already exists, it carries the skeptical investigations editor's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `THEORY.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the theory and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.

# f11: story revision pass

The story draft and verification notes live in `STORY.md`, and you may be invoked on it more than once — the workflow runs you and the fact-checking editor as a loop.

- If `STORY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass: produce the story from scratch.
- If `STORY.md` already exists, it carries the fact-checking editor's `<review>` block: read that feedback and revise `STORY.md` in place to address every point raised. Don't discard the story and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
