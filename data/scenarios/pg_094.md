# background

I need you to set up a 5-agent pipeline that orchestrates a political campaign by developing central policy platforms and localized ground-game voter mobilization in parallel, with each side informing the other. A campaign challenge arrives as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`. The pipeline alternates between revising the policy (turnout ceilings, non-negotiables, coalition structures) and revising the mobilization plan (a data-driven targeting implementation that achieves those goals).

The five agents:

- **campaign-manager** orchestrates the run by alternating one policy step with one mobilization step and tracking progress. It does no drafting, scripting, or reviewing itself.
- **policy loop** — the **policy-architect** writes the central policy platform to `POLICY.md`; the **opposition-researcher** judges it. The policy-architect reads `MOBILIZATION.md` on each revision pass to incorporate empirical turnout and polling evidence.
- **mobilization loop** — the **mobilization-director** writes voter targeting scripts and ground-game assignments to `MOBILIZATION.md`; the **compliance-auditor** judges them. The mobilization-director reads `POLICY.md` on each revision pass to align the field operations with the latest central platform claims.

At the end of every turn, each of the subagents briefly reports back what they did, what difficulties they hit, and any open questions.

The pipeline hands work off through plan-namespaced files:

- `problem.md` — the original campaign challenge, read-only. All four domain agents read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/PLAN.md` — one pre-existing strategic plan per directory, K plans total, given as input (read-only). All four domain agents read the PLAN.md of the plan they are currently assigned to.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/POLICY.md` — the central policy platform for that plan. Policy-architect writes; opposition-researcher reviews; mobilization-director and compliance-auditor read it.
- `plans/{id}-{slug}/MOBILIZATION.md` — the targeting scripts, field assignments, and their simulated results for that plan. Mobilization-director writes; compliance-auditor reviews; policy-architect and opposition-researcher read it.

Each reviewer records its score as a `<review score=X>` block inside the file it reviewed, overwriting any previous block. The campaign-manager reads those scores to drive the loops.

Each plan develops independently. Cross-read between POLICY.md and MOBILIZATION.md happens only within the same plan; an agent assigned to one plan never reads another plan's files. The campaign-manager round-robins across the K plans: each plan gets one policy step plus one mobilization step per full cycle.

Within each plan, neither policy drafting nor ground-game scripting finishes first. Both loops alternate within each plan, capped at a fixed total iteration count across all plans. Policy may tighten after seeing the plan's mobilization empirical numbers; mobilization scripts may correct after seeing the plan's refined policy constraints.

# f1: campaign context

You are working on a political campaign orchestration challenge, which may involve state, local, federal, or combined electoral races. The original campaign challenge is given as a natural-language statement in `problem.md`; read it before starting your part.

# f2: policy-architect's deliverable

Produce the central policy platform for the plan the campaign-manager has assigned you to this round, and write it to that plan's `POLICY.md` (under `plans/{id}-{slug}/POLICY.md`). A complete platform covers four kinds of claim, each justified with historical precedent or demographic analysis.

- **Bounds** — lower bounds on voter turnout from core base models; upper bounds on persuasion targets in swing demographics.
- **Structural properties** — coalition synergies, demographic overlaps, regional messaging variations, geographic clusters. State what messaging holds, where, and under which cultural assumptions.
- **Invariants** — non-negotiable party platform pillars (e.g., a specific stance on labor or taxes) that every localized field strategy must preserve. The mobilization output should honor them; the compliance-auditor will check with `verify.py`.
- **Complexity or momentum** — expected time to reach media saturation, saturation decay rates, or the difficulty of mobilizing low-propensity voters in specific cohorts.

Ground your claims against the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` if it has reported simulated turnout or budget numbers. A predicted turnout ceiling that is grossly off from the ground-game simulation means either the policy appeals are too weak or the targeting script has a bug — diagnose which side has the issue and state it. Be concrete. "The baseline turnout is bounded below by the union household model, value 12,500" is fine; "the campaign has some momentum that may help" is not. Do not read or aggregate across other plans' files.

# f3: policy drafting toolbox

Match the strategic tool to the demographic structure; do not force an ill-suited one. The list below is useful in policy and messaging analysis.

| Tool | Suitable for |
|---|---|
| Demographic wedge issues | Polarized electorates; driving a wedge into the opponent's coalition |
| Messaging consistency checks | Media-saturated environments; certifying local surrogate statements align with central platform |
| Base consolidation analysis | Low-turnout races, primary elections; ensuring reliable voters are locked in |
| Persuasion scaling models | High-turnout general elections; (1-1/e) and similar marginal return curves for ad spends |
| Polling equilibrium | Iterative debate cycles; finding a stable narrative advantage |
| Margin of error analysis | Close races; high-probability bounds on victory margins and vote deficits |
| Issue pivot strategies | Scandal or hostile media environments; justifying a shift to safer campaign topics |
| Sensitivity / crisis resilience | Stability of polling numbers under unexpected October surprises or oppo dumps |

# f4: drafting the mobilization scripts

You operate on the plan the campaign-manager has assigned you to this round. Read `problem.md`, your assigned plan's `PLAN.md`, and your assigned plan's `POLICY.md`, then implement the ground-game targeting and resource allocation scripts:

- Include every import. Create each variable with the correct type and bounds, define the voter targets and every budget/volunteer constraint exactly as the campaign challenge states. Run the targeting script, check the resource allocation status, and print both the final district assignments and the estimated turnout value.
- Make the implementation testable against the plan's `POLICY.md`. If the policy states a base turnout floor B, your targeting script should achieve an estimated turnout of at least B. If the policy states an invariant (e.g., no union funds spent in non-union zones), your output should satisfy it.
- Run the code yourself and watch it through to completion — do not stop while it is still running. If it errors, find and fix the root cause; do not bury errors under try/except.
- Sanity-check the answer against the plan's `POLICY.md`. A quantity that must be a whole number (like volunteers) should not come back fractional; a demographic constraint the policy says holds should hold.

Write the code and its results up in your assigned plan's `MOBILIZATION.md`. Do not read or write any other plan's files.

# f5: mobilization doc discipline

Keep `MOBILIZATION.md` lean. Its body — everything except the `<review>` block — must stay under 3000 words (~20KB), about a four-page memo; record only the final state and move iteration-by-iteration history into a separate `CHANGELOG.md`. Bold text must not exceed 5% of the file: if everything is bold, nothing is.

# f6: policy review

Review your assigned plan's `POLICY.md` against `problem.md`, against the plan's `PLAN.md`, and against the numbers reported in the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` (if any). Operate strictly on the assigned plan's files; do not look at other plans.

- **Faithfulness** — does the policy platform correctly address the electorate stated in `problem.md` under the framing set by the plan's `PLAN.md`? Check demographic definitions, regional segments, and constraint translations.
- **Logic soundness** — for each claim, walk through the historical precedent or demographic analysis. Flag hand-waving steps. Verify cited models apply under the stated assumptions.
- **Empirical grounding** — open the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md`. If the ground-game scripts report estimated turnout or costs, do they fall within the policy's predicted bounds? A turnout ceiling that is loose by orders of magnitude is a signal the model is too optimistic; tighten it, or note explicitly that the projection is only illustrative.

Be brief and specific. Do not write policy yourself — only review. If a claim is wrong, say which one and why.

# f7: mobilization compliance review

Review your assigned plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` against `problem.md`, against the plan's `PLAN.md`, and against the plan's `POLICY.md`. Operate strictly on the assigned plan's files; do not look at other plans.

- Inspect the reported turnout or budget allocation for anomalies, unreasonable expectations, or constraint violations stated directly in `problem.md` or in the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- Open the plan's `POLICY.md`. For every demographic bound and invariant the policy claims, write a check into a separate `verify.py` under the plan's directory: does the ground-game script's output satisfy the messaging invariant, fall within the predicted turnout bound, achieve the required persuasion scale? Run it. Name any variable or region that violates.
- Read the targeting scripts and spot-check them for bugs — unused funds, dead code, logic that contradicts the chosen demographic strategy. A bug that silently misallocates volunteers is worse than one that crashes.
- Treat the result as finished only when every budget constraint holds, every policy-claimed invariant holds, and the estimated turnout is consistent with the policy's bounds — not merely when the code runs without error.
- Check the length of the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md`: if it exceeds 20KB, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.
- Check the bold ratio of the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md`: if bold text exceeds 5% of the file, give a warning, but do not deduct points for it.

# f8: scoring

Score the work from 1 to 10, and record your verdict by overwriting the single `<review score=X>` block at the end of the document you reviewed — overwrite the existing one, never append a second.

Score strictly on real merit. Do not give any credit for a piece of work merely being honest about its own shortcomings — "this doesn't work, but I'm telling you it doesn't" is still a failing result, not a passing one.

# f9: orchestration

You orchestrate the run; you never draft platforms, write targeting scripts, or review yourself — you only dispatch the right agent at the right time.

- **Init** — discover the K plan directories under `plans/{id}-{slug}/`. Create `STATE.md` with `iter: 0`, `current_plan_idx: 0`, and a per-plan record holding each plan's id, last side, and latest policy/mobilization scores.
- **Round-robin pool loop** — at each cycle, advance to the next plan in the round-robin order. For that plan, dispatch one policy step (policy-architect, then opposition-researcher) and one mobilization step (mobilization-director, then compliance-auditor); then move to the next plan. After all K plans have each had one cycle, loop back to the first plan.
- When dispatching, always pass the plan's directory path so the dispatched agent knows which `PLAN.md` / `POLICY.md` / `MOBILIZATION.md` to operate on.
- Neither side terminates early on its own score within a plan; even a perfect-looking policy may need to revise once the mobilization script reports actual simulated turnout. The pool keeps cycling even after individual plans look done.
- Cap the total at 20 × K dispatches (10 policy + 10 mobilization per plan, summed across K plans). After the cap, stop and summarize per plan.
- Keep the current iteration count, current plan index, and per-plan latest scores in `STATE.md`, accurate after each dispatch. Read each score from the relevant plan's reviewer `<review score=X>` block. Commit progress after each step.
- Read each agent's brief narrative reply on return. If an agent reports an unresolved error, append it to `error-log.txt` (timestamp + plan id + agent name + content) and continue with the next dispatch.

# f10: policy revision pass

You operate on the plan the campaign-manager has assigned you to this round. Your central policy platform lives in that plan's `POLICY.md`. You may be invoked on the same plan more than once — within each plan the pipeline runs you and the opposition-researcher as a loop, alternating with that plan's mobilization loop, while the campaign-manager round-robins across plans.

- If your assigned plan's `POLICY.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the platform from scratch based on `problem.md` and the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `POLICY.md` already exists, it carries the opposition-researcher's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` and look at the latest reported numbers — they may invalidate, sharpen, or motivate revisions to your claims. Revise the plan's `POLICY.md` in place to address every reviewer point raised and to incorporate any empirical evidence that changes your analysis. Don't discard the previous platform and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
- Do not read or touch other plans' files.

# f11: mobilization revision pass

You operate on the plan the campaign-manager has assigned you to this round. Your targeting scripts and their results live in that plan's `MOBILIZATION.md`. You may be invoked on the same plan more than once — within each plan the pipeline runs you and the compliance-auditor as a loop, alternating with that plan's policy loop, while the campaign-manager round-robins across plans.

- If your assigned plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` does not exist yet, this is the first pass for that plan: produce the ground-game plan and scripts from scratch based on `problem.md` and the plan's `PLAN.md`.
- If the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` already exists, it carries the compliance-auditor's `<review>` block. Read that feedback. Then open the plan's `POLICY.md` and check whether the latest policy introduces bounds, invariants, or structural claims your current implementation doesn't yet respect — for example a higher turnout floor your script is now far from achieving, or an invariant your output violates. Revise the plan's `MOBILIZATION.md` in place to address every reviewer point and to align with the latest policy. Don't discard the previous scripts and start over, and don't leave a flagged issue unaddressed.
- Do not read or touch other plans' files.
